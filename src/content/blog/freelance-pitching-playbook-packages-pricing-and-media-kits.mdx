There's no such thing as job security. Not really.

A company can restructure, merge, or lay off your entire department on a Tuesday. You can do everything right and still get a calendar invite titled "Quick Chat" that ends your employment in 12 minutes.

That's not meant to scare you. It's meant to reframe how you think about your career. Having some component of your income be your own, where you can take control and create opportunities for yourself, is incredibly valuable.

Freelancing and consulting aren't just for people who hate having a boss. They're for anyone who wants to build a skill set that makes them more resilient, more confident, and more in control of their earning potential.

This playbook walks you through the full process: building your media kit, structuring your packages, pricing based on outcomes (not hours), and pitching clients in a way that makes it easy for them to say yes.

## Step 1: Build Your Media Kit

Before you pitch anyone, you need a home base. Somewhere a potential client can go to see your work, your rates, and what it's like to work with you. All in one place.

I recommend [Beacons AI](https://beacons.ai/) for this. It's basically a Linktree-style landing page, but the thing that makes it stand out is the Brand Collabs Media Kit feature. When you connect your social accounts (Instagram, Twitter/X, TikTok, LinkedIn), it pulls together a professional media kit automatically.

Here's what to include:

**Your bio.** Two to three sentences. Who you are, what you do, who you help. This is your [identity statement](/blog/craft-your-elevator-pitch-the-identity-statement-formula) in written form.

**Your services.** What do you actually offer? Be specific. "Social media management" is vague. "4 short-form video edits per week for B2B tech companies" is clear.

**Your rate card.** We'll build this in Step 3. For now, just know this is where your pricing lives.

**Portfolio samples.** 3 to 5 examples of your best work. Screenshots, links, case studies, whatever shows results.

**Testimonials.** Even one strong testimonial makes a difference. If you've done any pro bono work, get testimonials from those clients. You can write the testimonial yourself and send it to them for approval. As I told one of my coaching clients, Sandra: "The absolute least I would get from any pro bono work is a testimonial. You can write it for yourself and then give it to them. They'll almost never say no to that."

**Contact info.** How to reach you. A booking link for a discovery call is even better.

You don't need a fancy website. You don't need a logo. You don't need a 40-page portfolio. A clean Beacons page with the essentials above is enough to look professional and make it easy for clients to evaluate you.

## Step 2: Define 3 Tiered Packages (Good / Better / Best)

This is where most freelancers go wrong. They quote a single price and hope the client says yes. If the client says no, the conversation is over.

Tiered packages fix this. They give your client options, and they anchor the conversation around value instead of cost.

Here's the structure:

### The Starter Package (Good)
Your entry-level offering. Lower price point. Limited scope. This is what someone buys when they want to test the waters with you.

**Example for a social media freelancer:**
- 4 short-form videos per month
- Basic editing and captioning
- 1 round of revisions per video
- Monthly performance summary
- **Price: $1,000/month**

### The Growth Package (Better)
Your sweet spot. This is where most clients should land. More deliverables, more strategy, more impact.

**Example:**
- 8 short-form videos per month
- Custom content strategy aligned to their goals
- Platform-specific optimization (different cuts for different platforms)
- 2 rounds of revisions per video
- Bi-weekly strategy calls
- **Price: $2,000/month**

### The Premium Package (Best)
Your full-service offering. Maximum value, maximum commitment. This is for clients who want you deeply embedded in their business.

**Example:**
- 12 short-form videos per month
- Full content strategy and editorial calendar
- Community management and engagement
- Monthly analytics report with recommendations
- Weekly strategy calls
- Priority turnaround
- **Price: $3,500/month**

**Why this works:** The middle tier looks like the obvious choice. It's got significantly more value than the Starter, but it's not as expensive as the Premium. Behavioral economists call this the "decoy effect." The Premium makes the Growth package look reasonable. The Starter makes it look like a no-brainer upgrade.

And here's the key: rather than discounting your price, add more value to each tier. Don't lower the number. Increase what they get.

Adjust these numbers based on your industry, experience level, and the complexity of the work. The framework stays the same whether you're doing content creation, consulting, graphic design, copywriting, or coaching.

## Step 3: Set Outcome-Based Pricing (Not Hourly)

Do not fall into the hourly trap.

When you charge by the hour, you get penalized for being good at your job. The faster you work, the less you earn. That's backwards.

Outcome-based pricing flips this. You charge based on the value you deliver to the client, not the time it takes you to deliver it.

**The Picasso Napkin Story**

There's a famous story about Picasso. Someone asked him to sketch something on a napkin at a restaurant. He drew it in 30 seconds and said, "That'll be $10,000."

The person was shocked. "$10,000? That took you 30 seconds!"

Picasso replied: "It didn't take 30 seconds. It took me 30 years."

The same principle applies to your freelance work. Just because you can write a landing page in 2 hours doesn't mean it's worth $120 (2 hours at $60/hour). If that landing page generates $50,000 in revenue for the client, your work was worth far more than $120.

**A real example from coaching.** I spent about 3 hours over 2 weeks coaching a client on salary negotiation. I charged $2,500 for that. The outcome? She negotiated a $160,000 increase in her salary from her last job. My fee was a tiny fraction of the value she received. If I'd charged hourly, I would have made maybe $450. Instead, I priced based on the outcome.

**How to calculate outcome-based pricing:**

1. **Identify the client's goal.** What result do they need? More followers? More leads? Higher conversion rate? A new product launch?
2. **Estimate the value of that result.** If they get 1,000 new email subscribers, what's each subscriber worth to their business? If you help them launch a product that generates $100K in revenue, what's your contribution worth?
3. **Price at 10 to 20% of the projected value.** If the outcome is worth $50,000 to the client, a $5,000 to $10,000 package is reasonable. They're getting a massive return, and you're being compensated fairly.
4. **Frame it as an investment, not a cost.** "For $2,000, you'll get a content system that generates an estimated 500 leads per quarter" is a very different pitch than "$2,000 for 8 videos."

If a client pushes back on your pricing, the conversation should be about the value of the outcome, not the number of hours. If they can only see hours, they're not the right client.

## Step 4: The "First Two Free" Strategy

This is the move that makes everything else easier, especially when you're just starting out.

The pitch is simple: "I'll do the first two for free. If you like how it goes, here's what the ongoing engagement looks like."

It's a no-brainer for the client. Zero risk. They get to see your work, your process, your communication style. And you get to build your portfolio with real client work and real results.

Here's how I coach people to use this:

**Approach the client referencing their work.** Don't lead with "I need clients." Lead with "I love what you're doing with [specific thing]. I'd love to help you take it further."

**Propose a sprint.** "I'd love to do a 2-week sprint where I create [specific deliverables]. We can set some objectives together, like getting X new followers, Y sign-ups, or Z engagement rate."

**Deliver the first two for free.** Go above and beyond. Make them look amazing. This is your audition.

**Then pitch the ongoing package.** "Here's what a monthly engagement would look like" and hand them your tiered pricing.

Sandra, one of my coaching clients, was doing pro bono content creation for local businesses. Great work, but no path to getting paid. We walked through this exact strategy: use AI to mock up a proposal, package the work she was already doing into a clear offering, and pitch the client on a one-month paid trial.

You don't need a big landing page. You don't need a fancy proposal template. Mock it up in ChatGPT or Claude, take a screenshot, crop out the header, and send it to your prospective client. That's literally how I've landed some of the biggest one-on-one coaching clients I've gotten. Just mocking things up and sending them over.

**Critical step: always get a testimonial.** Even if the pro bono client never converts to paid, get a testimonial. Write it yourself and send it to them: "Would you be open to confirming this testimonial so I can use it for other clients?" They'll almost never say no.

## Step 5: Pitch from Their Needs, Not Your Skills

This is where most freelancers lose the deal. They lead with what they can do instead of what the client needs done.

Nobody cares about your skill set in the abstract. They care about their problem getting solved.

**The wrong pitch:**
"I'm a social media manager with 5 years of experience. I can do content strategy, video editing, community management, analytics, and paid ads. Here's my rate."

That's a list of features. It tells the client nothing about what you'll actually do for them.

**The right pitch:**
"I saw that your last 3 Instagram Reels got under 500 views each, but your static posts are doing 2x that. I think there's an opportunity to restructure your video content to match what's already working on your feed. I'd love to do a 2-week sprint where I create 4 Reels using your existing brand assets. If the engagement rate jumps, we can talk about what a monthly package looks like."

See the difference? The second pitch:

1. Shows you've done your research
2. Identifies a specific problem
3. Proposes a specific solution
4. Makes it easy to say yes (low commitment, clear deliverable)

This is the same principle behind the way we teach people to [quantify their value on a resume](/blog/achievement-mining-how-to-quantify-your-resume-bullets). Whether you're applying for a job or pitching a freelance client, you're really doing the same thing: showing someone the value you'll create for them.

**The consultant mindset.** Megan, one of my coaching clients, had a breakthrough when she stopped approaching networking as "selling herself" and started approaching it as a consultant. Instead of "let me impress you," she led with "how can I help you?" The difference in how people responded was dramatic. As she put it: "If you set that precedent of selling yourself, you're not on equal ground. Whereas if you're going in as a consultant, you have questions, you've done the research, you're talented, they're talented, let's collaborate."

That's the energy you want in every freelance pitch. Equal footing. Mutual benefit. Professional collaboration.

## Step 6: Always Book a Call from a Call

In sales, there's a philosophy: always book a call from a call. Every conversation should end with a next step.

This applies everywhere in freelancing:

- **After a discovery call:** "I'll send you a proposal by Friday. Can we schedule a 15-minute call next Tuesday to walk through it together?"
- **After delivering free work:** "I'd love to share the results and talk about what an ongoing engagement could look like. When works for a quick call?"
- **After a networking conversation:** "Who are 3 other people you think I should connect with?" or "What's an event you'd recommend I check out?"
- **After finishing a project:** "I really enjoyed working together. If you know anyone else who needs [service], I'd love an introduction."

Never let a warm conversation end cold. There should always be a next step, even if it's small.

## The Full Freelance Pitch Checklist

Here's your step-by-step:

1. **Set up your Beacons media kit.** Bio, services, portfolio samples, testimonials, contact info.
2. **Build 3 tiered packages.** Starter, Growth, Premium. Different price points, different scopes.
3. **Price based on outcomes.** Calculate the value you create. Price at 10 to 20% of that value. Never charge hourly for strategic work.
4. **Use the "first two free" strategy.** Deliver exceptional free work, then pitch the paid engagement.
5. **Pitch from their needs.** Research the client. Identify a specific problem. Propose a specific solution.
6. **Get testimonials from every client.** Write it yourself if you need to. Get their approval.
7. **Always book the next step.** Every conversation ends with a clear action item.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I set my prices when I have no freelance experience?

Start by researching what others in your niche charge. Look at freelance platforms like Upwork or Fiverr for a baseline, but don't anchor your pricing there because those platforms race to the bottom. Instead, figure out the value of the outcome you deliver. If you can help a business generate $10,000 in revenue or save them 20 hours per month, your package should reflect a fraction of that value, not the number of hours it took you to do the work. Use the "first two free" strategy to build your portfolio and collect testimonials. Once you have 2 to 3 case studies with results, you can confidently raise your rates because you have proof of what your work is worth.

### What if a client wants to negotiate my price down?

First, don't panic. Negotiation is normal. But don't drop your price. Drop your scope instead. If they can't afford the Growth package at $2,000/month, offer the Starter at $1,000/month with fewer deliverables. This protects your rate while still giving them an option. If they push for hourly, explain the outcome-based model: "I price based on the results we're working toward, not the hours it takes. That way you're paying for impact, not time." If they still insist on hourly and it's below your threshold, they're not the right client. Walk away gracefully and focus on finding someone who values what you bring.

### Can I freelance while working a full-time job?

Yes, and many people do. Check your employment contract first for any non-compete or moonlighting clauses. If you're clear, start with 1 to 2 clients and keep the scope manageable. The "first two free" approach works perfectly here because you're building your portfolio on the side without the pressure of replacing your income. As your client base grows and your confidence increases, you can decide whether to scale up or stay at a comfortable side-income level. Having freelance income alongside a salary also gives you leverage in negotiations. You're never desperate to accept a lowball offer when you've got other revenue coming in.
