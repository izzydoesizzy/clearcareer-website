One of my clients got a job offer for $90,000. Full-time. Benefits. PTO. Standard stuff.

Then, two days before the start date, the company called back. "We'd love to bring you on as a contractor instead."

Same role. Same responsibilities. Same expectations. Just... no benefits, no PTO, no retirement match, and now she'd owe self-employment taxes.

Most people would've said yes. She almost did. She needed the income and didn't want to rock the boat.

We did the math instead.

That $90K salaried role? To match it as a contractor, she needed $130/hour. That's roughly $260,000 annually. Not because she was being greedy. Because that's what the numbers said.

The company blinked. They came back with $115/hour. She took it. That's a $230K annual rate for a role they originally offered at $90K.

The math nobody does is the math that changes everything.

## Why Contract vs. Salary Math Matters

Here's the trap: companies present contract rates as if they're equivalent to salaried offers. They're not. Not even close.

When you're a W-2 employee, your employer covers a massive chunk of your total compensation invisibly. When you're a 1099 contractor, all of that lands on you.

If you don't do the contract vs. salary calculation before signing, you're accepting a pay cut without realizing it. Sometimes a massive one.

Let's break down exactly what you need to account for.

## The 7 Hidden Costs of Contract Work

### 1. Self-Employment Tax (15.3%)

As a W-2 employee, your employer pays half of your Social Security and Medicare taxes. As a contractor, you pay both halves. That's an extra 7.65% right off the top.

On a $100K contract: **$7,650 gone** before you've spent a dollar.

### 2. Health Insurance ($6,000-$24,000/year)

Employer-sponsored health insurance costs companies $8,000-$24,000 per employee per year. As an employee, you might pay $2,000-$6,000 of that. As a contractor, you're covering the whole thing.

A good individual plan runs $500-$800/month. Family coverage? $1,500-$2,000/month.

### 3. Retirement Match ($3,000-$12,000/year)

Most employers match 3-6% of your salary into a 401(k). On a $100K salary, that's $3,000-$6,000 in free money you lose as a contractor. You can still contribute to a SEP-IRA, but nobody's matching you.

### 4. Paid Time Off ($5,000-$15,000/year)

Full-time employees typically get 15-25 days of PTO. As a contractor, every day off is unpaid. If you take 20 days off (4 weeks) on a contract paying $100/hour, that's **$16,000 in lost income**.

Zero PTO doesn't just affect your wallet. It affects your sanity. Budget for time off anyway. Your body will thank you.

### 5. Equipment and Software ($1,000-$5,000/year)

Laptop. Software licenses. Home office setup. Internet upgrade. As an employee, the company provides this. As a contractor, you're buying your own.

### 6. Liability Insurance ($500-$2,000/year)

Depending on your field, you may need professional liability or errors-and-omissions insurance. Another cost that never shows up in the headline rate.

### 7. Risk Premium (10-20%)

Here's the one most people forget. Contracts end. Sometimes abruptly. There's no severance, no unemployment insurance (in most cases), and no guarantee of renewal. You need a buffer for gaps between contracts.

This alone is worth 10-20% on top of everything else.

## The Contract vs. Salary Formula

Here's the formula I walk every client through. Use it. Bookmark this page. Come back to it every time you get a contract offer.

**Step 1: Start with the salaried equivalent**

What would this role pay as a full-time position? Research it using [salary research tools](/blog/salary-research-tools-and-how-to-use-them) and sites like Glassdoor and Levels.fyi. Check our [free career tools](/tools) for more resources.

Let's say the salaried equivalent is $100,000.

**Step 2: Add the hidden costs**

| Cost | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Self-employment tax (7.65% extra) | $7,650 |
| Health insurance | $12,000 |
| Retirement (no match, 5%) | $5,000 |
| PTO (20 days unpaid) | $7,700 |
| Equipment and software | $2,000 |
| Liability insurance | $1,000 |
| **Subtotal** | **$35,350** |

**Step 3: Add the risk premium (15%)**

$100,000 x 0.15 = $15,000

**Step 4: Calculate your total**

$100,000 + $35,350 + $15,000 = **$150,350**

**Step 5: Convert to an hourly rate**

Contractors typically work about 1,880 billable hours per year (accounting for holidays and sick days, not PTO which is already factored in).

$150,350 / 1,880 = **$80/hour**

That means a $100K salaried role requires roughly **$80/hour as a contractor** to break even. Not to get ahead. Just to match.

If someone offers you $50/hour for that same role, they're not offering you $100K. They're offering you the equivalent of $60K-$65K with no benefits and no safety net.

## The Quick-and-Dirty Rule of Thumb

Don't want to do the full calculation every time? Use this:

**Take the salaried equivalent and multiply by 1.4-1.6 to get the equivalent contract total. Divide by 1,880 for the hourly rate.**

- $80K salary = $56-$68/hour contract
- $100K salary = $75-$85/hour contract
- $120K salary = $90-$102/hour contract
- $150K salary = $112-$128/hour contract

These are floor numbers. In high-cost markets or for highly specialized roles, go higher.

## How to Have This Conversation With the Company

When a company switches from salary to contract (or presents a contract offer that feels low), here's what to say:

> "I'm excited about this opportunity. I want to make sure the contract rate accounts for the full package I'd receive as a salaried employee, including benefits, retirement, PTO, and the additional tax burden. Based on my calculations, the equivalent rate would be [your number]. Can we talk about that?"

No apologies. No hedging. Just math.

Most companies know this math. They're counting on *you* not knowing it. When you show up with a spreadsheet and a clear number, the conversation changes completely.

## When Contract Work Actually Makes Sense

I'm not anti-contract. There are situations where it's the right move:

- **The rate is genuinely high.** If they're offering $150/hour for a role that pays $120K salaried, the math works in your favor
- **You want flexibility.** Contract work lets you control your schedule, take breaks between projects, and test different companies
- **It's a foot in the door.** Some companies use contract-to-hire as a tryout. If the rate is fair and there's a clear path to conversion, it can be worth it
- **You're between roles and need income.** A short-term contract at a decent rate beats an extended job search with zero income

The point isn't to avoid contract work. It's to price it correctly.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I calculate the salary equivalent of a contract rate?

Start with the annual contract income (hourly rate x 1,880 billable hours). Then subtract self-employment taxes (15.3%), health insurance ($6K-$24K), lost retirement match (3-6%), unpaid PTO value, and equipment costs. For a quick estimate, take the contract annual total and multiply by 0.6-0.7 to get the rough salaried equivalent. A $100/hour contract ($188K annual) equals roughly $113K-$132K in salaried compensation.

### Should I take a lower contract rate to get my foot in the door?

Only if the contract-to-hire path is clearly defined with a specific timeline and conversion salary. Get it in writing. A vague "this could turn into something full-time" is not a commitment. If you do accept a lower rate, set a 90-day review point and negotiate the permanent offer aggressively. Don't let a discounted contract rate become your salaried baseline.

### Is it normal for companies to switch from salary to contract at the last minute?

It happens more often than it should, and it's a yellow flag. Companies do this to reduce their benefit costs and transfer risk to you. It's not necessarily a dealbreaker, but it means you need to renegotiate the compensation completely. The salaried offer is no longer relevant. Start the contract calculation from scratch and present a rate that accounts for everything you're now covering yourself.
