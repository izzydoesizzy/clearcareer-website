Most people prepare for interviews by Googling "common interview questions" and rehearsing vague answers in their head. Then they walk in, get a question they didn't expect, stumble through a rambling answer, and wonder where it went wrong.

I've watched it happen dozens of times in coaching sessions. The good news? Interview prep is a system, not a talent. And with the right system, you can walk into any interview having already addressed every concern the hiring manager might have before they even bring it up.

This is the exact prep system I use with my coaching clients. It combines AI prompts (these work with ChatGPT, Claude, or any AI assistant), the STAR story framework, and a three-round mock interview method that builds your confidence layer by layer.

## Step 1: Run the Employer Objections Prompt

This is the single most powerful interview prep tool I've found. It flips the script from "What might they ask me?" to "What are they worried about?"

Here's the concept. Every hiring manager has concerns about every candidate. Maybe you lack direct experience in their industry. Maybe there's a gap in your resume. Maybe you've never managed a team this large. They're going to probe those gaps in the interview, whether they come out and say it or not.

So we surface those objections before the interview. Then we build strategies to address each one.

### The Employer Objections Prompt

Open your AI assistant (ChatGPT, Claude, or similar). Paste in the full job description and your resume. Then type:

<PromptBlock id="interview-objections" label="Employer Objections Prompt">
Based on this job description and my resume, what are the top 5 objections a hiring manager might have about my candidacy? For each objection, give me a strong response I can use in the interview.
</PromptBlock>

The AI will analyze the gap between what they want and what you have. It will give you something like:

- **Objection**: Limited experience with financial services, mainly in the energy industry.
- **Counter**: Emphasize transferable skills in stakeholder management, regulatory compliance, and cross-functional project delivery. Highlight how working in a highly regulated industry like energy prepared you for the precision that financial services demands.

I demonstrated this live with Danielle during a coaching call.

<Coach>The AI is flagging "lack of direct senior client business manager experience" as an objection. Here's how it suggests you counter it.</Coach>

<Client>Well, I have done that, I have done that, but it's just not on my resume.</Client>

That happens every time. The objections prompt reveals things you've done but haven't highlighted. Now you know what to put on your resume or what to focus on in your interviews.

### Batch Multiple Job Postings

You can take this further. Paste in three or four similar job descriptions with your resume and say: "These are the types of jobs I'm looking for. Based on that, what are the common objections employers would have?" This gives you an amalgamation of the most frequent gaps, which becomes your prep priority list across all your target roles.

## Step 2: Generate Custom Interview Questions

Now that you know their concerns, let's predict the actual questions.

### The Custom Interview Questions Prompt

In the same chat, type:

<PromptBlock id="interview-custom-questions" label="Custom Interview Questions Prompt">
Based on my resume and this job description, prepare a list of 10 specific interview questions they would ask me. For each question, give me: the topic, a strategy for how I should answer, and a sample answer I can customize.
</PromptBlock>

The AI will generate questions tied directly to the role. Not generic "Tell me about your greatest weakness" questions, but role-specific ones based on the actual requirements.

Each question comes with:
- **Topic**: What skill or area this question is testing
- **Strategy**: The approach you should take in your answer
- **Sample answer**: A starting point you can rewrite in your own words

You can keep refining. "Give me more questions related to managing events and large agency clients." Or "Give me questions about leading cross-functional teams." Stack the questions until you've covered every area the job description touches.

This is what I walked through with the group during a coaching session on interview prep. The combination of objections and custom questions means you've mapped the entire interview before you walk through the door.

## Step 3: Build Your STAR Story Library

Behavioral questions make up a huge percentage of interviews. "Tell me about a time when..." questions. And the best way to answer them is with stories, not summaries.

That's where the STAR framework comes in.

### What STAR Stands For

- **Situation**: What was going on when this happened? Set the context.
- **Task**: What was the specific problem you needed to solve? You haven't done anything yet at this point.
- **Action**: What specific steps did you take? This is the meat of the story.
- **Result**: What was the outcome? Include numbers when you can.

A bad STAR answer: "I improved the process and it worked better."

A good STAR answer: "Our API help center was generating hundreds of support tickets per week. My task was to reduce inquiry volume without adding headcount. I rewrote the documentation, added an FAQ section, and proactively addressed the most common issues before users had to ask. Ticket volume dropped by 50% within two months."

### The Four Core Story Types

I tell every client to prepare [four core STAR stories](/blog/four-stories-you-need-for-any-interview) that cover 90% of behavioral interview questions:

1. **Problem-solving**: A time something broke or was failing, and you stepped in and fixed it.
2. **Leadership or influence**: A time you drove something forward. You didn't have to be the boss, but you made it happen.
3. **Failure and learning**: A time you made a mistake and what you learned from it.
4. **Exceeding expectations**: A time you went above and beyond what was asked.

Each of these maps to multiple common questions:
- "Tell me about a time you dealt with conflict" maps to story 1 or 2
- "Tell me about your biggest failure" maps to story 3
- "Why should we hire you?" maps to story 4

If you have these four stories locked and loaded, you can adapt them to almost any behavioral question.

### Build a Permanent Story Library

Here's the power move. Don't just prep stories for one interview. Create a permanent library in a Google Doc or Notion page.

Write out every story in STAR format. Add new stories after every job. This library follows you for your entire career. Any interview prep you do now doesn't go to waste. In five years, you can still reference the same documents.

I recommended this to Tyler, and it clicked immediately. He asked whether older stories still work. The answer: recent examples are generally better, but if there's a more specifically relevant story from a couple of years ago, use that. You can even give the interviewer a choice: "I can tell you about a time I did X or a time I did Y." A little choose-your-own-adventure. It steers the interview in the direction you want.

### Use AI to Scaffold Your Stories

If you're staring at a blank page, try this prompt:

<PromptBlock id="star-story-scaffolding" label="STAR Story Scaffolding Prompt">
Act like an interview expert. Give me 10 concise examples of specific stories I may want to tell in STAR format as a [your job title]. For each, give me the skill being demonstrated and a story prompt I can use to write my own version.
</PromptBlock>

This gives you prompts like: "Tell a story about a time you reduced costs through process improvement" or "Describe a situation where you had to influence a stakeholder without direct authority." Those prompts jog your memory and help you recall real stories from your experience.

What to avoid in your STAR stories:
- **Negativity**: Don't talk badly about the client, team, or company. Focus on the situation and your actions.
- **Vagueness**: Provide specific examples with real numbers and context.
- **Omitting your role**: Make it clear what you did, not just what the team did or the company achieved.
- **Skipping the results**: Always land the plane. What happened because of what you did?

Bad outcomes are fine. As long as you show what you learned and how you improved the next time, a failure story is actually a strong answer.

## Step 4: The Three-Round Mock Interview Method

Reading your answers isn't practice. Practicing is practice. You need to say the words out loud before you say them to a real interviewer.

Here's the three-round method I use with clients:

### Round 1: Solo Practice

Record yourself answering questions. Use your phone, your laptop, or a free tool like [Google Interview Warmup](https://grow.google/certificates/interview-warmup/), which records your answers and gives feedback on filler words, answer length, and relevance.

The first round is about hearing yourself. You'll immediately notice where you ramble, where you lose your thread, and where your energy drops. This is your "first pancake." It's supposed to be a little rough. That's the whole point.

Anil, one of my clients, initially resisted this step. But once he started practicing out loud while doing daily tasks (reading questions aloud while cooking, answering them on his morning walk), he found where he stumbled before the real interview. That's exactly what you want to discover at this stage, not in front of a hiring panel.

### Round 2: Practice with a Friend or Peer

Have someone ask you questions and give you honest feedback. Not "that was great!" but "You lost me in the middle of that second story" and "I didn't understand what your role was in that project."

If you don't have someone to practice with, find a community. My clients practice with each other all the time. Toastmasters is another great option. Anil credited the networking strategies from our coaching with giving him confidence to join Toastmasters, and it made a noticeable difference in his delivery.

### Round 3: Practice with a Coach or Expert

This is the round where you get real interview conditions. A coach (or someone with hiring experience) will push back, ask follow-up questions, and test your ability to think on your feet.

Each round builds on the last. Solo practice builds familiarity. Peer practice builds comfort. Expert practice builds resilience.

### Use AI as a Mock Interviewer

Between rounds, you can use your AI assistant:

<PromptBlock id="interview-mock-interviewer" label="Mock Interviewer Prompt">
Now act as the hiring manager. Ask me the objection questions one at a time. After each of my responses, give me feedback on how to improve it.
</PromptBlock>

You can do this at midnight in your pajamas. No one has to know. But you'll walk into that interview having already addressed every concern they might have.

## Step 5: Master the "Tell Me About Yourself" Opening

If there's an 80/20 level of effort you can put into your interview, 80% should go into that initial opening.

Why? Because if you can check off most of their boxes in the first two minutes, the rest of the interview gets easier. Some of my clients have told me they've literally seen the interviewer's arm checking boxes on their paper during the opening statement. After that, the conversation became casual. That's where you want to be.

### The Three-Part Structure

Your "Tell me about yourself" answer should cover:

1. **Who you are**: Your title, your identity, what you do
2. **What you've done**: Your top 2-3 achievements with specific numbers
3. **Why this role**: Why you're excited about this specific opportunity at this specific company

Keep it under 60 seconds. Practice it until it sounds natural, not memorized.

You can use this prompt to generate a draft:

<PromptBlock id="tell-me-about-yourself" label="'Tell Me About Yourself' Prompt">
Here's my resume and a brief origin story of my career. Write a 60-second "tell me about yourself" answer following this structure: who I am, what I've done, and why I'm excited about this role.
</PromptBlock>

Then rewrite it in your own words. It should sound like you talking, not like a robot reading a script.

## Step 6: Delivery Techniques That Set You Apart

Great answers with bad delivery still fall flat. Here are the three delivery techniques I coach on:

### Downward Inflection

When you end sentences with your voice going up, it sounds like you're asking a question. Like you're unsure.

Use downward inflection instead. End your statements with your voice going down. It sounds certain. Definitive.

You can't really change how confident you feel. You can prepare, and that will help. But the main thing is to sound more certain by having a downward inflection. You're not asking permission. You're stating a fact.

I worked on this with Raj during a coaching session. He had 15 years of energy sector experience but was ending his statements with an upward lilt that undermined his authority. One simple switch to downward inflection, and his answers sounded completely different.

### The "I Did Three Things" Structure

When your answer involves multiple steps or actions, telegraph the structure upfront.

Instead of: "Well, first I looked at the data, and then I talked to the team, and we decided to run a pilot, and then we measured the results..."

Say: "I did three things. First, I analyzed the usage data. Second, I ran a pilot with the engineering team. Third, I measured the results over 90 days."

When you give people a structure ahead of time, they can follow along. They know what you're about to say. They know when you're wrapping up. If you don't do that, they don't know when your answer is going to end, and they start checking out.

### Facts First, Then Strategy

Lead with the numbers and the checkboxes. Then tell the story of how you got there.

The beginning of your answer should be the main facts: how many clients, what size budgets, what outcomes. Those are the checkbox items the interviewer needs to hear. There are people in the interview who may not have read your resume. They may have skimmed it. You want them to check that box quickly.

Then go into the strategy: how you approached the problem, what decisions you made, why you chose that path.

Keep most answers under two minutes. If you need longer, ask: "Would you like me to go deeper on that?"

## Putting It All Together: The Pre-Interview Checklist

One week before the interview:

- [ ] Run the employer objections prompt
- [ ] Generate custom interview questions
- [ ] Review and update your [four STAR stories](/blog/four-stories-you-need-for-any-interview)
- [ ] Complete your [company research](/blog/company-research-before-interviews-the-complete-playbook)
- [ ] Draft your "Tell me about yourself" opening

Three days before:

- [ ] Round 1 mock interview (solo, recorded)
- [ ] Round 2 mock interview (with a friend or peer)
- [ ] Refine answers based on feedback

The day before:

- [ ] Review your STAR story library
- [ ] Review the employer objections and your counters
- [ ] Practice your opening statement one more time
- [ ] Prepare 3-4 questions to ask the interviewer

If you do this system, you'll walk in more prepared than 95% of candidates. Not because you memorized answers, but because you've already thought through every angle of what they need and mapped your experience to it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How far in advance should I start preparing for an interview?

Start as soon as you get confirmation. Ideally, you want at least one full week of prep time. That gives you enough room to run the AI prompts, build or update your STAR stories, do company research, and complete two to three rounds of mock interviews. If you only have a day or two, focus on the employer objections prompt and your "Tell me about yourself" opening. Those two things will give you the biggest return on limited time.

### What if I get a question I haven't prepared for?

It's going to happen. The prep system isn't about predicting every question. It's about building a library of stories and strategies you can pull from regardless of what they ask. If you have your [four core STAR stories](/blog/four-stories-you-need-for-any-interview) ready, you can adapt them to most behavioral questions on the fly. For unexpected technical or situational questions, use the "I did three things" structure to organize your thoughts in real time. Pause for a few seconds before answering. Interviewers expect and respect a moment of reflection.

### Should I tell the interviewer I used AI to prepare?

No need to announce it, but don't hide it either. If it comes up naturally, you can mention that you used AI tools as part of your research process. Most hiring managers will see that as resourceful, not lazy. The key is that your answers need to sound like you, not like ChatGPT. Use AI to generate the framework and the prompts, then fill in every answer with your real stories, your real numbers, and your real voice. If your answer sounds like it was written by a chatbot, you've skipped the most important step.
