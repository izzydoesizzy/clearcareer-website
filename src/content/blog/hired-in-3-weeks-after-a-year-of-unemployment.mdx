## A Year of Nothing

One of my clients had been unemployed for a full year. Twelve months. She'd been an account manager at a paid media agency, had strong experience in customer success and tech, and was doing all the "right" things: updating her resume, applying online, reaching out to her network.

Nothing was landing.

A year of unemployment does something to your confidence. Every rejection chips away at your belief that you're qualified, capable, or wanted. By the time she reached out for coaching, she wasn't just struggling with her job search. She was struggling with her sense of professional identity.

## What Was Actually Wrong

Her resume was fine. Her experience was solid. The problem wasn't her qualifications.

The problem was her approach. She was showing up to every conversation as a job seeker: someone who needed something from the company. That energy comes through in interviews, in emails, in networking conversations. It's subtle, but hiring managers feel it.

The other issue was clarity. After months of rejection, she'd started casting a wider net, applying to anything that seemed vaguely related. That lack of focus showed up everywhere: in her resume (too generic), in her interview answers (not specific enough), and in her confidence (uncertain about what she actually wanted).

## The Three Shifts

### Shift 1: Resume as a value proposition

We rebuilt her resume around quantifiable contributions. Not "managed client accounts" but the specific results: revenue generated, campaigns delivered, client retention rates, and problems solved. Every bullet point answered the question: "What happened because you were there?"

For the full framework on turning vague job duties into concrete impact statements, read [Achievement Mining: How to Quantify Your Resume Bullets](/blog/achievement-mining-how-to-quantify-your-resume-bullets).

### Shift 2: Show up as a consultant, not an applicant

This was the big mindset shift. Instead of walking into interviews thinking "I hope they pick me," we reframed the approach: walk in as a consultant who's there to understand the company's problems and assess whether she's the right person to solve them.

That meant:
- **Asking diagnostic questions** about the team's challenges before pitching herself
- **Listening actively** for pain points she could address
- **Positioning her experience** as solutions to specific problems they described
- **Treating the interview as a two-way evaluation**, not a one-sided audition

This changes the power dynamic in the room. You stop performing and start consulting.

### Shift 3: Write her own job description

Before her final interview, she took everything she'd learned about the company's needs and wrote a brief document outlining how she'd approach the role in her first 90 days. Not because they asked for it. Because it demonstrated that she understood their problems and had a plan.

The hiring manager later told her this was what sealed the deal.

For more on this approach, read [The 30-60-90 Day Plan: How to Stand Out in Final Round Interviews](/blog/30-60-90-day-plan-how-to-stand-out-in-final-round-interviews).

## The Timeline

| Before Coaching | After Coaching |
|----------------|---------------|
| 12 months unemployed | Hired in 3 weeks |
| Generic resume, wide net | Focused resume, targeted approach |
| Showing up as applicant | Showing up as consultant |
| Hoping to be picked | Evaluating mutual fit |

Three weeks. After a year.

The same person. The same experience. The same job market. Different strategy.

## Why Speed Isn't the Point

Three weeks makes a great headline. But the real story isn't about speed. It's about what changed in how she showed up.

The strategies she learned during those 3 weeks of coaching didn't expire when she got the job. She uses the same consultant mindset in client meetings, in internal presentations, and in every career conversation since.

As she put it: "The strategies I learned not only helped me get hired in three weeks after a year of unemployment but have also transformed how I approach my career."

That's the difference between getting a job and building a career skill set.

## The Industry Context

Her experience wasn't unusual. The average job search in the US takes [5.5 months](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/empsit.nr0.htm) (BLS, 2025), and [1 in 5 job seekers](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/empsit.nr0.htm) are unemployed for 27 weeks or longer. For senior-level roles, [6-9 months](https://economicgraph.linkedin.com/) is typical.

A year of unemployment is more common than most people think. What's less common is having someone help you see the problem clearly enough to fix it in 3 weeks.

## Your Micro-Action for Today

Before your next interview (or networking conversation), write down the 3 biggest problems you think the company or team is facing. Not generic industry challenges. Specific, researched problems based on their job posting, recent news, and what you know about the role.

Then walk into the conversation ready to ask about those problems, listen to the answers, and connect your experience to their needs.

You're not an applicant. You're a consultant evaluating whether this is the right engagement.
