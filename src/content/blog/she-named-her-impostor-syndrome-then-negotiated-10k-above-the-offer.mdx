## The Disconnect

One of my clients was a front-end web developer with real skills. She could build things. She could solve problems. She'd shipped projects that people used every day.

But if you asked her to describe herself professionally, she'd shrink. "I'm still learning." "I'm not as experienced as other developers." "I got lucky on that project."

Sound familiar?

She had impostor syndrome, and it was running the show. It showed up in her resume (vague, underselling), in her interviews (hedging, deflecting credit), and in her salary expectations (asking for less than she was worth because she didn't believe she deserved more).

## Naming the Thing

The first thing we did was name it. Not in a therapy-session way. In a practical, "this is the obstacle and here's how we get around it" way.

Impostor syndrome isn't a personality flaw. It's a pattern. And like any pattern, once you see it, you can interrupt it.

Here's what it looked like for her:

| What She Said | What Was Actually True |
|---------------|----------------------|
| "I'm still learning" | She'd shipped 3 production applications |
| "Anyone could have done that" | She was specifically recruited for her skills |
| "I'm not senior enough" | She'd mentored junior developers and led projects |
| "I got lucky" | She'd consistently delivered results across multiple teams |

The facts didn't support the narrative in her head. They never do.

## Rebuilding the Resume

Her old resume read like a list of technologies she'd used. HTML, CSS, JavaScript, React. A grocery list of tools with no context for what she'd done with them.

We rebuilt it around outcomes:

- What she built (specific projects, not vague descriptions)
- Who it served (users, teams, stakeholders)
- What happened because of her work (time saved, problems solved, revenue impact)

Every bullet answered: "What changed because she was on this project?" We pulled out accomplishments she'd dismissed as "just part of the job" and gave them the weight they deserved.

[Your Resume Is an Ad, Not a Biography](/blog/resume-is-an-ad-not-a-biography) covers this approach in detail. The short version: stop listing what you did and start showing what happened because you did it.

## The Overnight Test

She applied to a handful of targeted roles with her new resume.

The next morning, she woke up to **two interview invitations**.

Her old resume had been getting silence. Her new resume, with the exact same experience, was getting responses overnight. Nothing about her skills had changed. The framing did.

This is why I'm always telling clients: the resume isn't about you. It's about the employer. They're scanning for evidence that you can solve their problems. When your resume speaks their language, you get in the door.

## The Negotiation

She got an offer. A good one. Her first instinct: accept immediately and be grateful.

We talked instead.

The offer was solid, but it was below market rate for her role and experience level. She knew this intellectually but felt uncomfortable pushing back. The impostor syndrome voice was loud: "What if they pull the offer? What if they think I'm being greedy? What if I don't deserve more?"

We prepared a counter-offer that was grounded in:
- **Market data** for front-end developers at her experience level
- **Specific accomplishments** she'd bring to the role
- **A collaborative tone** that framed the ask as a conversation, not a demand

She delivered it. The company came back with **$10,000 more than the original offer**.

No argument. No drama. Just a conversation backed by data and delivered with confidence.

## The Real Win

The $10K matters. Over a career with compounding raises, that single negotiation could be worth [$100,000 or more](https://hbr.org/2014/06/why-women-dont-negotiate-their-job-offers).

But the bigger win was the confidence shift. In her words: "I woke up to interviews after applying with my new resume, and I negotiated $10K above the offer. I feel confident and ready to grow in this exciting role."

She didn't become a different person. She started seeing clearly what was always true: she was good at this, she had evidence to prove it, and she deserved to be compensated accordingly.

## How to Interrupt Impostor Syndrome

If you recognize yourself in this story, here are three things that help:

### 1. Collect evidence against the voice

Every time impostor syndrome says "you're not good enough," write down one fact that contradicts it. A project you shipped. A problem you solved. Feedback you received. Build a file. Refer to it before interviews and negotiations.

### 2. Separate feelings from facts

"I feel like I'm not qualified" is a feeling. "I have 4 years of experience and have shipped 3 production applications" is a fact. Feelings are valid, but you negotiate with facts.

### 3. Practice saying the uncomfortable thing

The words "I'd like to discuss the compensation" feel impossible the first time. Practice them. Say them out loud. Role-play with a friend or a coach. The discomfort fades with repetition. The money you leave on the table doesn't come back.

## Your Micro-Action for Today

Open a new document. Write down 5 professional accomplishments you're tempted to dismiss as "not a big deal." For each one, write what would have happened if you hadn't done it.

That gap between "what happened" and "what would have happened without you" is your value. It's not impostor syndrome's job to assess it. It's yours.
