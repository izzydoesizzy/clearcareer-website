Informational interviews are the single most underused tool in job searching. One 20-minute conversation can tell you more about a company, a role, and an industry than five hours of Googling.

But most people either skip them entirely or do them wrong. They send vague requests for "coffee chats," show up without a plan, and walk away with nothing useful. Or worse, they treat it like a stealth job interview, which makes the other person uncomfortable and shuts down the relationship before it starts.

This playbook covers the full lifecycle: who to target, how to ask, what to prepare, how to conduct the conversation, and how to follow up in a way that turns a single call into a real professional relationship.

Between 75-85% of jobs are obtained through networking. Applying on job boards yields a 2-4% success rate on average. Informational interviews are how you build the network that gets you into that 75-85%.

## The Golden Rule: Ask for Help, Not a Job

Before anything else, internalize this.

When you ask someone for a job, you put them in an awkward position. They may not have the decision-making power to help you. They may not want to put their reputation at risk by introducing you to a hiring manager. The conversation gets stiff. They go into defense mode.

But when you ask for help and advice? Defenses drop. People genuinely like to help, especially when the ask is specific and low-pressure. And here's what happens naturally: asking for help results in warm introductions to other people in their network. Which is exactly what you want.

I've coached hundreds of people through this. The ones who lead with genuine curiosity about the other person's experience always get further than the ones who lead with "Do you know of any openings?"

Instead of asking for a job, ask them for advice, ask them for tips. Then you go from being the only person looking for a job for you to having a whole bunch of ears to the ground.

## Who to Target for Informational Interviews

Not everyone is equally valuable to talk to. Be strategic about who you reach out to.

### 1. People 1-2 Levels Above Your Target Role

If you're targeting a Marketing Coordinator role, talk to Marketing Managers and Directors of Marketing. They understand what the role requires, they know what good candidates look like, and they're often involved in hiring decisions.

### 2. People Who Currently Do the Job You Want

They can tell you what the day-to-day actually looks like. Not the polished version from the job posting, but the real version. What tools do they use? What surprised them about the role? What do they wish they'd known before starting?

### 3. Former Employees of Your Target Companies

This is the secret weapon. People who used to work at your target company will tell you things that current employees can't. They'll be honest about culture, management, and what the company is actually like behind the careers page.

On LinkedIn, search the company name, click "People," then filter by "Past Company." Look for people who left 2-4 years ago. They're close enough to have relevant intel but far enough removed to speak freely.

### 4. Industry Connectors

Event organizers, community leaders, podcast hosts, and active LinkedIn posters in your industry. These people know everyone. One good conversation can lead to five introductions.

### 5. People at Companies on Your Target List

If you've already [built your target company list](/blog/build-your-target-company-list-with-ai), start reaching out to people at those companies. Not to ask for a job. To learn about their work, their team, and their challenges.

## How to Ask: Micro-Asks Beat Coffee Chat Requests

Here's where most people get it wrong. They send messages like:

> "Hi, I'd love to pick your brain over a 20-minute coffee chat about your career."

That message asks for a lot. The person has to check their calendar, find a time slot, mentally prepare for a conversation with a stranger, and block out 30+ minutes including the transition time. For someone they don't know. Most people just don't respond.

Instead, use what I call micro-asks.

### The Micro-Ask Strategy

Instead of asking for a full conversation, ask one very specific question that can be answered in a line or two.

**Example:**

> "Hi [Name], I'm researching companies in [industry] and your background caught my attention. Quick question: what were some of the top competitors you considered before joining [Company]?"

That's it. One question. Easy to answer. No calendar juggling required.

The magic happens after they respond. You follow up with what you did with their advice:

> "Thanks so much. I looked into [Company X] based on your suggestion and found out they're hiring for a role that's a great fit. Really appreciate the tip."

Now you've closed the loop. You've shown you actually used their advice. And you've opened the door for a longer conversation naturally.

Ask very specific questions that are easy to answer in one or two lines. It doesn't require much effort on their part, but it's specific enough to their personal experience that they feel valued.

### The Full Informational Interview Request

When you do want a full conversation, here's the template that gets the highest response rates:

> Hi [Name],
>
> I've been researching [Industry/Company] and your background caught my attention, specifically your work on [specific project, post, or career move].
>
> I'm exploring roles in [area] and would love to hear about your experience at [Company]. Not looking for any favors, just trying to learn from people doing the work I want to do.
>
> Would you have 20 minutes for a quick call in the next couple of weeks?

This works because you're leading with a specific compliment. You're being upfront that this isn't a sneaky job ask. And "20 minutes in the next couple of weeks" is easy to say yes to.

## How to Prepare: The 3-4 Question Rule

You got a yes. The call is scheduled. Now don't blow it.

### Research Before the Call

Spend 15-20 minutes on research:

- Read their LinkedIn profile. Not just the headline. Read their job descriptions, their posts, their recommendations.
- Check the company website. Know what they do, their recent news, any major product launches or changes.
- Google their name. Look for talks, interviews, articles, or podcast appearances.

The more you know going in, the better your questions will be. And people can tell when you've done the homework.

### Prepare 3-4 Specific Questions

Not "Tell me about your role." That's lazy and generic, and they'll give you a lazy and generic answer.

Instead, prepare questions that show you've done research and are thinking deeply about the role or industry. Here's the informational interview question bank I share with my coaching clients:

1. **"What's the biggest challenge your team is facing right now?"** This tells you what problems the company needs solved. If you can position yourself as someone who solves those problems, you're already ahead.

2. **"What skills or experiences would make someone a standout candidate for a role like yours?"** This gives you the unwritten requirements that don't show up in job postings.

3. **"If you were starting over in this field, what would you do differently?"** People love this question. It's personal, it's reflective, and the answers are always insightful.

4. **"What surprised you most about working at [Company] compared to what you expected?"** This gets you real intel about culture and day-to-day work, not the polished version from the careers page.

5. **"Who else would you recommend I talk to? Anyone in your network who's doing interesting work in this space?"** Always ask this. It's how one conversation turns into three.

6. **"What are some companies or competitors in this space that I should be looking at?"** This is a direct way to expand your [target company list](/blog/build-your-target-company-list-with-ai).

Pick 3-4 of these and customize them based on your research. Write them down. Have them in front of you during the call.

## The Introduction Script

When the call starts, don't wing it. Have a brief, clear introduction prepared:

> "Hi [Name], thank you so much for taking the time to speak with me today. My name is [Your Name], and I'm currently [your current situation, e.g., exploring new opportunities in product marketing after five years in brand management]. I'm really interested in [Industry/Company], and I'm excited to learn more about your experiences."

Keep it under 30 seconds. Hit three points:

1. Your name
2. Your current situation (one sentence)
3. Why you're interested in talking to them specifically

Then immediately transition to your first question. The faster you get them talking, the better.

## During the Conversation: The 80/20 Listening Rule

Here's the biggest mistake people make in informational interviews: they talk too much about themselves.

Follow the 80/20 rule. Listen 80% of the time. Talk 20% of the time.

Your role is to ask great questions and actively listen. Take notes. Ask follow-up questions based on what they say. Show genuine curiosity.

When they share something interesting, don't immediately pivot to your own experience. Instead, dig deeper: "That's really interesting. Can you tell me more about how that played out?" or "What would you do differently if you had to approach that again?"

The more they talk, the more you learn. And the more valued they feel. People remember the conversations where they felt heard, not the ones where someone pitched them for 20 minutes.

### Get Them to Talk First

This is a principle I use at networking events too, and it applies perfectly here. People like talking about themselves. Be curious. Ask about their challenges. Ask what excites them about their current projects.

When you listen first, you can tailor your own story to connect with what they've shared. It makes the conversation feel natural instead of scripted.

## How to Follow Up: The 24-Hour Rule

After the call, send a thank-you note within 24 hours. This is non-negotiable.

### The Thank-You Email Template

> Hi [Name],
>
> Thank you so much for taking the time to speak with me today. I really appreciate your insights about [specific thing they shared].
>
> Your advice about [specific recommendation or insight] gave me a lot to think about, and I'm going to [specific action you'll take based on their advice].
>
> Thank you again, and I hope we can stay in touch.
>
> Best,
> [Your Name]

Two things make this work:

1. **Reference something specific** from the conversation. Not "thanks for your insights" but "your suggestion to look into the product analytics team at [Company] was really helpful."
2. **State the action you'll take** based on their advice. This shows you actually listened and intend to use what they shared.

### The Power Move: Close the Loop

This is where most people stop. And it's where the best networkers separate themselves.

Two to four weeks after the call, send a follow-up sharing what you did with their advice:

> "Hi [Name], just wanted to let you know I reached out to [person they mentioned] and we had a great conversation. They connected me with [next person/opportunity]. Really appreciate you pointing me in that direction."

This does three things. It shows you took action. It gives them the satisfaction of knowing their advice mattered. And it keeps the relationship alive without you having to come up with a reason to follow up.

Linh, one of my coaching clients, used this exact approach. She reached out to dozens of people for informational interviews and within three days had three great conversations. Those contacts started introducing her to more people, including an Assistant VP at a university. The conversations compounded because she closed the loop every time.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Mistake 1: Treating it like a job interview.** The moment you start selling yourself, the dynamic changes. They're doing you a favor by having this conversation. Keep the focus on learning from them.

**Mistake 2: Asking generic questions.** "What do you do?" is not an informational interview question. It's what you ask at a party when you have nothing else to say. Be specific.

**Mistake 3: Not following up.** If someone gives you 20 minutes of their time and you don't send a thank-you within 24 hours, you've wasted the interaction. The follow-up is where the relationship starts.

**Mistake 4: Leading with what you lack.** I coached Raj on this directly. He wanted to mention his lack of direct industry experience in informational interviews. Both Anita and I told him: never lead with what you don't have. Focus on the transferable value you bring. Let them decide about the gap.

**Mistake 5: Stopping after one conversation.** One informational interview is nice. Ten is a strategy. Twenty is a pipeline. The people who do this consistently for four to six weeks tell me that opportunities start coming to them instead of them having to chase everything.

## The Full Informational Interview Workflow

Here's the complete system, step by step:

1. [Build your target company list](/blog/build-your-target-company-list-with-ai) using AI
2. Use the [LinkedIn People Finder method](/blog/build-your-target-company-list-with-ai) to identify contacts at those companies
3. Send a micro-ask or full informational interview request
4. Research the person and prepare 3-4 specific questions
5. Conduct the call using the 80/20 listening rule
6. Send a thank-you within 24 hours referencing something specific
7. Close the loop 2-4 weeks later with what you did with their advice
8. Ask for introductions and repeat the cycle

Each conversation builds on the last. Your questions get sharper. Your understanding of the industry deepens. Your network expands. And the right opportunity finds you.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many informational interviews should I be doing per week?

Aim for 2-3 per week when you're actively searching. That's enough to build real momentum without burning out. Over four to six weeks, that's 8-18 conversations, which is enough to develop a real understanding of your target industry and build a network of people who know your name and what you're looking for. Quality matters more than quantity. Three great conversations with the right people will do more for your search than 15 superficial ones.

### What if I'm nervous about reaching out to strangers?

Every single person who gets to this stage feels nervous. That's completely normal. Here's what I tell my clients: you have nothing to lose. The worst thing that happens is they don't respond. That's it. No one has ever gotten angry at receiving a genuine, well-crafted request for advice. Out of the hundreds of people I've coached through this, not one has had a negative experience from reaching out. Start with one message. The second one gets easier. By the fifth, it feels natural.

### Should I reach out to people at companies where I've already applied?

Absolutely. In fact, this is one of the best times to do it. Reach out to someone on the team (not the hiring manager, not HR) and ask about the work, the culture, the challenges. You'll learn things that help you in the interview, and they might put in a good word. Just be transparent: "I recently applied for the [Role] position and I'm doing my research. Would love to learn more about the team." Honesty goes a long way.
