# How to Answer Interview Questions About Experience You Don't Have

"What's your experience with Salesforce?"

You've never touched Salesforce.

Your stomach drops. Your brain scrambles for something, anything, to say. And the words that come out are some version of: "I don't actually have experience with that."

Full stop. Dead air. Interview momentum gone.

Here's what I need you to understand: nobody has 100% of the qualifications for any job. Not even the person who wrote the job posting. The difference between candidates who get offers and candidates who don't isn't whether they have every single skill. It's how they handle the gaps.

## The Redirect Technique for Interview Questions You Can't Directly Answer

The redirect is simple. You acknowledge the gap honestly, then immediately bridge to something you DO have that's relevant.

The formula:

**"I don't have direct experience with [X], but I DO have experience with [Y], which is similar because [specific connection]."**

That's it. No apology. No rambling. No panic. Just a clean pivot to your actual strengths.

Let me show you what this looks like in the real world.

## A Real Example: The Software Pivot

One of my clients was interviewing for a senior operations role. The interviewer asked about her experience with a specific enterprise software platform she'd never used.

Old response (what she would have said before coaching): "I haven't used that software before, sorry."

New response (what she actually said): "I haven't worked in that specific platform, but I spent three years managing our team's workflow in Asana and Monday.com. The core principles are the same: building automated workflows, tracking KPIs across departments, and training team members on adoption. When I transitioned from Asana to Monday.com, I was fully operational within two weeks. I'd expect a similar ramp-up."

She got the offer.

Notice what happened there. She didn't lie. She didn't pretend. She showed the interviewer that the *underlying skill* transfers, and she backed it up with proof that she learns new tools quickly.

## Why "I Don't Have That Experience" Is Never the Full Answer

When you stop at "I don't have that experience," you're handing the interviewer a reason to say no. You're doing their rejection work for them.

But when you redirect, you're reframing the question. You're saying: "Let me show you what I bring that's just as valuable." You're putting the interviewer in a position to say yes.

Here's the thing most candidates miss: the interviewer isn't asking if you have a specific skill in isolation. They're asking if you can do the job. Those are different questions.

## How to Build Your Redirect: Step by Step

### Step 1: Identify the Underlying Skill

When you hear a question about experience you don't have, ask yourself: what's the real skill behind this question?

"Do you have experience with Tableau?" = Can you analyze and visualize data?
"Have you managed a team of 20+?" = Can you lead at scale?
"What's your experience with agile methodology?" = Can you work iteratively and adapt to changing priorities?

The specific tool or scenario is the surface. The skill underneath is what matters.

### Step 2: Find Your Parallel

What HAVE you done that uses that same underlying skill? Different tool, different context, same muscle.

You haven't used Tableau, but you've built dashboards in Google Sheets and Excel that leadership relied on for quarterly decisions. Same skill: turning raw data into clear, actionable visuals.

### Step 3: Bridge with Proof

Connect your parallel experience to the question with a specific example. Numbers help. Timelines help. Outcomes help.

"I built a reporting dashboard in Google Sheets that our VP used for all quarterly board presentations. It pulled data from 4 sources and automated the refresh process. The core skill, translating complex data into clear visuals for decision-makers, is the same. And I've picked up new tools quickly throughout my career."

### Step 4: Show Learning Speed

End with evidence that you learn fast. A time you picked up a new tool. A certification you completed. A transition you made successfully.

This final piece answers the unspoken follow-up question: "Okay, but how long will it take you to get up to speed?"

## Common Interview Questions and Redirect Examples

**"Do you have experience in our industry?"**
Redirect: "My background is in [adjacent industry], which shares [specific similarities]. For example, [parallel]. And in my research, I've found that [specific thing you learned about their industry] aligns closely with my experience in [area]."

**"Have you ever managed a P&L?"**
Redirect: "I haven't owned a full P&L, but I've managed project budgets up to $500K and was responsible for forecasting and variance reporting. I understand the mechanics of revenue, cost management, and margin optimization."

**"What's your experience with [specific methodology]?"**
Redirect: "I haven't worked in [methodology] specifically, but my team used [related approach], which follows similar principles around [specific shared principle]. The transition would be about learning the vocabulary, not the thinking."

## The One Thing You Should Never Do

Never apologize. "I'm sorry, I don't have that experience" puts you in a defensive position from the first word. You have nothing to apologize for. You're in this interview because your background was strong enough to get you here.

Replace "I'm sorry, I don't have..." with "I haven't worked directly in X, but here's what I bring..."

Confident. Honest. Forward-looking.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How honest should I be about skills I don't have in an interview?

Be completely honest. Never claim experience you don't have. Interviewers can spot bluffing, and getting caught destroys your credibility for the rest of the conversation. The redirect technique works because it IS honest. You're acknowledging the gap and showing what you bring instead. Authenticity plus a strong parallel beats a fabricated answer every time.

### What if the missing experience is a core requirement, not a nice-to-have?

Even core requirements have transferable parallels. The key is showing that you understand what the skill involves and that you have the foundational ability to ramp up. If a role requires 5 years of Python and you have zero programming experience, that's a genuine mismatch. But if you have 3 years of Python and they asked for 5, or you have strong experience in a similar language, the redirect works. Focus on closing the gap, not highlighting it.

### Can I use the redirect technique for "years of experience" questions?

Yes. If they ask for 8 years and you have 5, don't get stuck on the number. Focus on what you accomplished in those 5 years. "I have 5 years in this space, and in that time I [specific accomplishment that demonstrates senior-level capability]." Results matter more than years. A candidate with 5 high-impact years often outperforms someone with 8 years of the same year repeated 8 times.
