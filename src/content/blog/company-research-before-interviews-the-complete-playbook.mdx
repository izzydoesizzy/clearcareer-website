Most candidates "research" a company by skimming the About page and reading the mission statement. Then they walk into the interview and say, "I love your company's commitment to innovation."

The interviewer has heard that 47 times this week. It means nothing.

Real company research means walking in like you already work there. Knowing the challenges. Understanding the culture. Having opinions about their strategy. That's what separates "we should hire this person" from "next candidate, please."

I coached Carlos through a prep session for his interview at Hadrian (a subsidiary of Zern). By the time we were done, he knew their Glassdoor patterns, had identified former employees to contact, understood the corporate structure between the two companies, and had questions that made the HR Director sell *him* on why he should join. That's the level of research we're going for.

Here's the complete playbook, from your first five-second AI lookup all the way through using research in the interview room.

## Step 1: The 5-Second Company Snapshot (Start Here)

Before you do anything else, get a quick lay of the land. This takes literally five seconds.

Open ChatGPT, Perplexity, or Gemini and paste this prompt:

<PromptBlock id="company-snapshot" label="5-Second Company Snapshot">
Give me a brief overview of [Company Name]: what they do, their core products/services, their business model, and their top 3-5 competitors.
</PromptBlock>

That's it. Now you know what they sell, how they make money, and who they compete with. You sound prepared before you've even started digging.

I use this before every outreach email, every networking call, every application. Takes five seconds instead of an hour of Googling, and you walk into conversations sounding like you've done your homework. Because you have.

**Pro tip:** Save the competitor list. Once you've done all the research to apply to one company, you can apply to every competitor on that list. You already know the industry. That's free leverage.

## Step 2: LinkedIn Deep Dive (Former Employees Are Your Secret Weapon)

The careers page tells you what the company *wants* you to see. Former employees tell you what it's actually like.

This is the single most valuable research tactic in the entire playbook. One 15-minute conversation with a former employee will give you more useful intel than 3 hours of Googling.

### How to Find Former Employees

1. Go to the company's LinkedIn page
2. Click "People"
3. Click "All Filters"
4. Select "Past Company" (this is the key filter most candidates miss)
5. Look for people who left 2-4 years ago

Why 2-4 years? They're recent enough to have relevant intel but far enough removed to speak candidly. Someone who left last month may still be emotional about it. Someone who left 6 years ago is too far removed to be useful.

When I walked Carlos through this for Hadrian, we found an operational excellence specialist who'd led Lean Six Sigma initiatives there. That was directly relevant to Carlos's project management background. We also found a financial analyst who'd been at the company during a key growth period.

### The Outreach Message Template

Keep it short, specific, and honest:

> *"Hi [Name], my name is [Your Name]. I noticed you used to be a [Role] at [Company]. I'm currently interviewing with the team for a [Your Target Role]. I'd love to hear a bit about your experience working there and any thoughts or advice you may have. Would you be open to a quick 15-minute chat?"*

That's it. No elaborate pitch. No flattery. Just a clear, specific ask.

Here's something Carlos was worried about: "Won't they call the company and tell them I'm snooping around?" Maybe. And that's actually a *good* thing. It tells the company you're thorough. You're doing your homework. That's a signal most candidates never send.

### What to Ask Former Employees

Good questions for your conversation:

- "What was the team culture like day to day?"
- "What surprised you most about working there?"
- "If you were interviewing there today, what would you want to know?"
- "What type of person thrives in that environment?"
- "What were some of the biggest challenges the team was dealing with?"
- "How would you describe the management style?"

The answers to these questions give you material you can't find anywhere else. And when you reference real challenges in your interview ("I understand the team has been navigating some rapid growth"), you sound like an insider, not an outsider trying to get in.

For more on reaching out to people you don't know yet, read the [informational interview playbook](/blog/the-informational-interview-playbook).

### Current Employees: The Pattern Analysis

While you're on the company's LinkedIn People tab, study the current team in your target department. You're looking for three patterns:

**Tenure patterns.** Are people staying 3+ years, or is there constant turnover? If 80% of the team joined in the last 12 months, something happened. That's worth asking about.

**Career progression.** Are people getting promoted internally, or does everyone leave at the same level they joined? Internal promotions signal that the company invests in growth. If nobody gets promoted, that tells you something too.

**Common backgrounds.** What skills, certifications, and experience do current team members share? This tells you what the company actually values, regardless of what the job posting says.

## Step 3: Glassdoor Review Analysis (Patterns, Not Complaints)

Every company has negative Glassdoor reviews. One angry person ranting about their manager isn't a signal. Twelve people saying the same thing across two years? That's a pattern worth paying attention to.

### The 20-Review Method

1. Go to Glassdoor and pull up the company
2. Note the overall rating and "would recommend to a friend" percentage
3. Sort by most recent
4. Read at least 20 reviews
5. Sort by lowest rating and read 10 more
6. Track recurring themes

When I did this live with Diane before her interview at Magnet Forensics, we found a 3.5 out of 5 rating. The top positives were great people, events, and culture. The recurring negatives were old tech stack, inconsistent management, and average pay. That gave us specific, actionable material.

### What to Track

Make a simple two-column list:

**Positives mentioned 3+ times:**
- (e.g., strong team collaboration, flexible work, great benefits)

**Negatives mentioned 3+ times:**
- (e.g., slow decision-making, unclear promotion criteria, Q4 crunch)

**Important context:** Check how old the reviews are. Concerns from 3 years ago may have already been addressed. A cluster of negative reviews right after a layoff likely reflects the emotional moment, not the ongoing reality. Look for trends over time, not snapshots.

### How to Turn Glassdoor Intel into Interview Gold

You're never going to say "I read on Glassdoor that your management is bad." That's career suicide.

Instead, you turn patterns into professional questions. Here's how Carlos and I worked through this:

**Glassdoor pattern:** "Management challenges at mid and upper levels"

**Bad question:** "I saw your Glassdoor reviews mention bad management. What's going on?"

**Good question:** "When I was doing some research, I came across a few reviews that mentioned management as an opportunity area. I'd love to hear a bit about what the company is doing to invest in leadership development."

Carlos even refined his version further: "There seem to be some opportunity areas regarding management at mid and upper levels. Could you share what HR has identified and how the team is working on those?"

**What you're listening for in their answer:** The structure should mirror how a good candidate answers "what's your biggest weakness." You want to hear: "We're aware of these challenges. We've seen the feedback. Here's what we're doing about it."

**The red flag:** They dismiss it. "Oh, that was a long time ago, that's not a problem anymore." Without specifics about what changed, that's a warning sign. Even a company without bad reviews should have an answer to "how are you improving your management practices?"

**The power move:** Use their concerns to flip the script. Ask: "What can I do from day one to start off on the right foot? What are some of the main challenges the team has faced that I could help address?" Now *they're* selling *you* on why you should join.

## Step 4: The Company Research Stack (AI + Built In + Crunchbase)

Beyond LinkedIn and Glassdoor, three tools give you a deep, well-rounded picture of any company.

### AI for Competitor Intelligence

Once you have the company's competitor list from Step 1, go deeper:

<PromptBlock id="competitor-comparison" label="Competitor Comparison Table">
Create a comparison table of [Company] vs. [Competitor 1], [Competitor 2], [Competitor 3]. Include: main products, target market, key strengths, key weaknesses, and recent news.
</PromptBlock>

This is something almost no candidate does. When Diane and I mapped Magnet Forensics against Cellebrite, OpenText, IBM, and Access Data, she could speak intelligently about the competitive landscape. That turned her from "a candidate" into "someone who already understands our market."

**Bonus:** Once you've done this research for one company, apply to their competitors. You already know the industry. All that research you just did? It transfers directly.

### AI for Interview Question Generation

After gathering your company intel, use this prompt:

<PromptBlock id="research-interview-questions" label="Research-Based Interview Questions">
Based on this job description [paste it] and what I've learned about the company [paste your notes], generate 10 interview questions that would show I understand their challenges and have thought about how to solve them.
</PromptBlock>

These become your "Do you have any questions for me?" answers. They'll be specific, informed, and impossible to fake. The interviewer will know you did real work.

### AI for Employer Objection Prep

This is one of my favorite AI prompts for interview prep. Paste your resume and the job description, then ask:

<PromptBlock id="research-employer-objections" label="Employer Objection Prep">
Based on my resume and this job description, what are the top 5 objections a hiring manager might have about my candidacy? For each objection, suggest a strategy to address it.
</PromptBlock>

When I used this with a client who was transitioning from the energy sector, it flagged "limited experience with financial services" and "no direct experience with their distribution partners." Those were exactly the concerns that came up in the interview. Because she'd prepared for them, she had clear, confident answers ready.

You can batch this across 3-4 job postings to find common gaps in your profile and address them once for all your interviews.

### Built In for Culture Data

Built In lets you search companies and see benefits, salaries, culture data, and open roles. Nicole, one of our group members, uses this to compare salary data across competitors and validates it against Glassdoor numbers. You can search by industry to find additional companies in your target space.

### Crunchbase for Business Intelligence

Crunchbase shows you funding history, investors, recent news, leadership changes, and sometimes even the tech stack. A signal like "hired VP of Global Partnerships" tells you the company is investing in growth. "Raised $50M Series C" means they're scaling and likely hiring. Recent news becomes natural conversation starters in interviews.

## Step 5: Social Media and Company Content (Culture Signals)

The company's social media tells you things the corporate website never will.

### What to Look For

**Instagram and TikTok:** Team events, office photos, employee spotlights. Do people look like they actually enjoy being there? Is the content polished corporate stock photos or authentic moments?

**Twitter/X:** How does the company respond to criticism? What topics does leadership engage with? What's the tone?

**YouTube:** Employee testimonials, day-in-the-life videos, and conference presentations by team members.

**Company newsletter:** Sign up for it. Read the last few issues. When Anil was preparing for an interview at the Niagara Falls Public Library, we found their newsletters covered Library Giving Day and AI events. That gave him specific, relevant questions to ask. "I saw you're doing a big push on AI events. How are you finding speakers and workshop presenters? I have some ideas on that."

That kind of question shows you're already thinking about how to contribute. It's the difference between "I'm interested in this job" and "I've already started thinking about how to do this job."

### Culture Green Flags
- Employees resharing company content with genuine personal commentary
- Behind-the-scenes content that feels unscripted
- Leadership posting about team wins and giving credit by name
- Active engagement with followers and honest responses to questions

### Culture Red Flags
- Every post is a product announcement, zero employee content
- Comments are turned off
- The account hasn't posted in months
- Employee spotlight language sounds copy-pasted across every feature

### The LinkedIn Engagement Hack

Here's something I noticed when prepping Diane for Magnet Forensics: the company's LinkedIn posts had almost zero comments. That's an opportunity. Write one thoughtful comment on their recent post, ask a good question, or share a relevant perspective. You'll immediately stand out over every other applicant because literally nobody else is engaging with their content. If the hiring manager sees your name in their comments and then in their applicant pool? That's a connection they won't forget.

## Step 6: Research Your Interviewers (Communication Intelligence)

This step separates good candidates from great ones. Research the specific people who will interview you.

### What to Look Up on LinkedIn

For each interviewer, check:

**Career trajectory.** Did they climb the ladder internally or join from outside? If your interviewer spent 15 years going from individual contributor to VP at this company, they value longevity and institutional knowledge. Frame your answers around commitment and long-term impact.

If they've hopped between startups every 2-3 years, they value speed and adaptability. Frame your answers around quick wins and versatility.

**Content they've posted or shared.** Their posts reveal what they care about professionally. When Carlos found that his interviewer lived in the Chapel Hill area and he'd gone to UNC there, that became a natural icebreaker. "You're interviewing a local."

**Shared connections.** Mutual contacts give you a bridge. You can reference them naturally in conversation.

**Their role in the process.** Different interviewers test different things. An HR Director is validating whether you can do the job. They might play devil's advocate and stress-test your capabilities. The hiring manager or VP is likely assessing culture fit: will you walk into this team and thrive, or will you create friction?

Knowing who you're talking to changes how you deliver your answers. This isn't manipulation. It's communication intelligence. You're adjusting your delivery for a specific audience. That's a professional skill.

### Personal Questions That Build Connection

One of our clients, Raj, had a smart insight: everyone asks about the company, but nobody asks about the interviewer as a person. Try questions like:

- "What excites you most about your role here?"
- "What's been your biggest challenge in this position?"
- "What's something you're most proud of from the last year?"

These get the interviewer telling their own story. And people who feel heard are more likely to advocate for you in the debrief.

## Step 7: The First Question Tells You Everything

Here's something most candidates never think about: the interviewer's opening question reveals their biggest concern about you.

If they open with "Tell me about your management experience," they're worried you might not be senior enough.

If they open with "Why are you leaving your current role?" they're worried about flight risk or red flags.

If they open with "What do you know about our company?" they're testing whether you actually care about this specific role or if you're applying everywhere.

### How to Prepare for This

Before the interview, ask yourself: if I were the hiring manager looking at my resume, what would concern me?

- Career gap? Prepare to address it confidently.
- Industry switch? Prepare to bridge the relevance.
- Short tenures? Prepare to explain the pattern.
- Overqualified? Prepare to explain why this role excites you.

Use the [employer objection prompt](#step-4-the-company-research-stack-ai--built-in--crunchbase) from Step 4 to surface these concerns before the interview. Then you're ready for whatever they open with.

Whatever their first question is, answer it head-on. No deflecting. No rambling. Address the concern, then pivot to your value.

For more on handling questions about gaps in your experience, read [how to answer questions about experience you don't have](/blog/how-to-answer-questions-about-experience-you-dont-have).

## Putting It All Together: Your Research Checklist

Before any interview, work through this list. Budget 2-3 hours spread across a few days so you can absorb what you find.

- [ ] Run the 5-second AI company snapshot (competitors, products, business model)
- [ ] Build a competitor comparison table
- [ ] Search LinkedIn for former employees (2-4 years gone) and message at least 1
- [ ] Study current team members' LinkedIn profiles for tenure, progression, and background patterns
- [ ] Read 20+ Glassdoor reviews and tracked positive/negative patterns
- [ ] Check company social media for culture signals
- [ ] Read their newsletter or recent blog posts
- [ ] Leave a thoughtful comment on their LinkedIn content
- [ ] Research each interviewer's LinkedIn profile
- [ ] Run the employer objection prompt with your resume + job description
- [ ] Prepare 3-5 specific questions based on your research
- [ ] Identify the likely "biggest concern" about your candidacy and prepare your response

That's 2-3 hours of focused work. And it's the difference between "I prepared" and "I showed up."

Once your research is done, pair it with [the 4 stories you need for any interview](/blog/four-stories-you-need-for-any-interview) so you can connect your experience directly to what you've learned about the company. Then use [the complete interview prep system](/blog/the-complete-interview-prep-system) to turn all of this research into structured, confident answers.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much time should I spend researching a company before an interview?

Plan for 2-3 hours of focused research per company. Spread it across a few days so you can absorb what you find. Roughly 30 minutes on LinkedIn (current and former employees), 30 minutes on Glassdoor, 20 minutes on social media and newsletters, 20 minutes on AI-powered research (competitor tables, objection analysis), 20 minutes on interviewer backgrounds, and the rest on the company's website, recent news, and earnings calls if they're public. The research gets faster after your first time because you'll have a system.

### Should I mention my research during the interview?

Yes, but weave it in naturally. Don't say "I read on Glassdoor that..." Instead, reference specifics: "I noticed your team grew from 8 to 20 in the last year. What's been the biggest challenge with that growth?" or "I saw the company recently raised a Series C. How is the team thinking about scaling?" This shows you did the work without making it feel like a book report. Interviewers notice when a candidate clearly understands their business, and it puts you in a completely different category from everyone who just skimmed the About page.

### What if I can't find much information about a small or private company?

Small companies with limited online presence need creative research. Check the founders' personal LinkedIn activity and podcast appearances. Search for the company name in industry publications. Look at their job postings (even for other roles) to understand their priorities and tech stack. Search for them on Crunchbase for funding and investor information. And lean heavily on the former employee outreach strategy. For a 50-person company, even one conversation with a former team member will give you more insight than hours of web research. Also try the AI snapshot prompt with Perplexity, which pulls from current web sources and often finds information that ChatGPT's training data doesn't include.
