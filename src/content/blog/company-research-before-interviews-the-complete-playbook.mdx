# Company Research Before Interviews: The Complete Playbook

Most candidates "research" a company by skimming the About page and reading the mission statement. Then they walk into the interview and say, "I love your company's commitment to innovation."

The interviewer has heard that 47 times this week. It means nothing.

Real company research before interviews means walking in like you already work there. Knowing the challenges. Understanding the culture. Having opinions about their strategy. That's what separates "we should hire this person" from "next candidate, please."

Here's the complete playbook.

## Step 1: LinkedIn Deep Dive (Former Employees Are Your Best Source)

The careers page tells you what the company *wants* you to see. Former employees tell you what it's actually like.

### How to Find Former Employees

1. Go to the company's LinkedIn page
2. Click "People"
3. Filter by "Past Company" (this is the key filter most people miss)
4. Look for people who left 2-4 years ago

Why 2-4 years? They're recent enough to have relevant intel but far enough removed to speak candidly. Someone who left last month may still be emotional. Someone who left 6 years ago is too far removed.

### What to Ask Them

Reach out with a short, specific message. (Use the [former employee outreach template](/blog/linkedin-outreach-messages-that-actually-get-replies) if you need a starting point.)

Good questions to ask:
- "What was the team culture like day to day?"
- "What surprised you most about working there?"
- "If you were interviewing there today, what would you want to know?"
- "What type of person thrives in that environment?"

You'll learn more in one 15-minute conversation with a former employee than in 3 hours of Googling.

### Current Employees Matter Too

Search the company's People tab for people in your target department. Look at:
- **Job tenure patterns.** Are people staying 3+ years, or is there high turnover?
- **Career progression.** Are people getting promoted internally, or does everyone leave at the same level they joined?
- **Common backgrounds.** What skills and experiences do current team members share?

These patterns tell you what the company actually values, regardless of what the job posting says.

## Step 2: Glassdoor Reviews (Look for Patterns, Not Individual Complaints)

Every company has negative Glassdoor reviews. That's not the signal. The signal is patterns.

### How to Read Glassdoor Like a Pro

1. Sort by most recent
2. Read at least 20 reviews
3. Look for themes that show up 3+ times

If one person says "management is terrible," that's one person's bad experience. If 12 out of 20 reviews mention "lack of clear direction from leadership," that's a pattern worth paying attention to.

### What to Track

Make a simple list:

**Positives mentioned 3+ times:**
- Great benefits
- Strong team collaboration
- Flexible work arrangements

**Negatives mentioned 3+ times:**
- Slow decision-making
- Unclear promotion criteria
- Work-life balance challenges during Q4

### How to Use This in the Interview

You're not going to say "I read on Glassdoor that your management is bad." That's career suicide.

Instead, turn patterns into professional questions:

- Glassdoor pattern: "Unclear promotion criteria" becomes your question: "Can you walk me through how performance is evaluated and how promotion decisions are made?"
- Glassdoor pattern: "Slow decision-making" becomes: "How does the team typically move from idea to execution on new initiatives?"

You're addressing real concerns without revealing your source. Smart and strategic.

## Step 3: Social Media for Culture Signals

The company's Instagram, TikTok, and Twitter accounts tell you things the corporate website never will.

### What to Look For

- **Instagram:** Team events, office photos, employee spotlights. Do people look like they actually enjoy being there? Is the content polished corporate stock photos or authentic moments?
- **Twitter/X:** How does the company respond to criticism? What topics does leadership engage with? What's the tone?
- **YouTube:** Look for employee testimonials, day-in-the-life videos, and conference presentations by team members.

### Culture Red Flags on Social Media

- Every post is a product announcement, zero employee content
- Comments are turned off
- The account hasn't posted in months
- Employee spotlight language sounds copy-pasted across every feature

### Culture Green Flags

- Employees resharing company content with genuine personal commentary
- Behind-the-scenes content that feels unscripted
- Leadership posting about team wins and giving credit by name
- Active engagement with followers and honest responses to questions

## Step 4: Research Your Interviewers (Cultural Intelligence)

This is the step that separates good candidates from great ones. Research the people who will interview you.

### What to Look Up

For each interviewer, check their LinkedIn profile for:
- **Career trajectory.** Did they climb the ladder internally or join from outside? This tells you what they value: loyalty and institutional knowledge vs. fresh perspectives.
- **Content they've posted or shared.** Their posts reveal what they care about professionally.
- **Shared connections.** If you have mutual contacts, you can reference them naturally in conversation.
- **Education and background.** Not to make small talk about alma maters, but to understand their frame of reference.

### How This Changes Your Approach

If your interviewer spent 15 years climbing from individual contributor to VP at this company, they value longevity and growth. Frame your answers around commitment and long-term impact.

If they've hopped between startups every 2-3 years, they value adaptability and rapid impact. Frame your answers around quick wins and versatility.

This isn't manipulation. It's communication intelligence. You're adjusting your delivery to resonate with a specific audience. That's a professional skill.

## Step 5: Opening Question Intelligence

Here's something most candidates never think about: the interviewer's first question reveals their biggest concern about you.

If they open with "Tell me about your management experience," they're worried you might not be senior enough.

If they open with "Why are you leaving your current role?" they're worried about flight risk or red flags.

If they open with "What do you know about our company?" they're testing whether you actually care about this specific role or if you're applying everywhere.

### How to Prepare for This

Before the interview, ask yourself: if I were the hiring manager looking at my resume, what would concern me?

- Career gap? Prepare to address it confidently.
- Industry switch? Prepare to bridge the relevance.
- Short tenures? Prepare to explain the pattern.
- Overqualified? Prepare to explain why this role excites you.

Whatever their first question is, answer it head-on. No deflecting. No rambling. Address the concern, then pivot to your value.

For more on handling questions about gaps in your experience, read [how to answer questions about experience you don't have](/blog/how-to-answer-questions-about-experience-you-dont-have).

## Putting It All Together: Your Research Checklist

Before any interview, work through this checklist. Use our [career research tools](/tools) to speed up the process.

- [ ] Read 20+ Glassdoor reviews and noted patterns
- [ ] Searched LinkedIn for former employees (2-4 years gone)
- [ ] Reached out to at least 1 former employee
- [ ] Reviewed current team members' LinkedIn profiles for tenure and progression patterns
- [ ] Checked company social media for culture signals
- [ ] Researched each interviewer's LinkedIn profile
- [ ] Identified the likely "biggest concern" about your candidacy
- [ ] Prepared 3-5 questions based on your research

This takes 2-3 hours. That's it. And it's the difference between "I prepared" and "I showed up."

Once your research is done, pair it with [the 4 stories you need for any interview](/blog/four-stories-you-need-for-any-interview) so you can connect your experience directly to what you've learned about the company.

## Your Micro-Action for Today

Pick a company you're interviewing with (or want to interview with). Go to their LinkedIn page, click People, and filter by Past Company. Find one person who left 2-4 years ago. Send them a message today.

One conversation. That's your research head start. Do it now.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much time should I spend researching a company before an interview?

Plan for 2-3 hours of focused research for each company. Spread it across a few days so you can absorb what you find. Spend roughly 30 minutes on LinkedIn (current and former employees), 30 minutes on Glassdoor, 20 minutes on social media, 20 minutes on interviewer backgrounds, and the rest on the company's website, recent news, and earnings calls if they're public.

### Should I mention my research during the interview?

Absolutely, but weave it in naturally. Don't say "I read on Glassdoor that..." Instead, reference specifics: "I noticed your team grew from 8 to 20 in the last year. What's been the biggest challenge with that growth?" This shows you did the work without making it feel like a book report. Interviewers notice when a candidate clearly understands their business.

### What if I can't find much information about a small or private company?

Small companies with limited online presence require creative research. Check the founders' personal LinkedIn activity and podcast appearances. Search for the company name in industry publications. Look at their job postings, even for other roles, to understand their priorities. And lean heavily on the former employee outreach strategy. For a 50-person company, even one conversation with a former team member will give you more insight than hours of web research.
