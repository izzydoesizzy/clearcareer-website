Let's be honest. The word "networking" makes most people cringe.

It sounds transactional. Forced. Like you're supposed to walk into a room full of strangers, hand out business cards, and recite your resume at people until someone agrees to hire you.

That's not networking. That's torture. For everyone involved.

Real networking at events is simpler than you think. Meet people you find interesting. Learn about their work. Be curious. Follow up. That's it.

But there's a system behind doing it well. I've personally gotten into over $10,000 worth of conferences for free, scored job interviews from casual conversations, and helped dozens of coaching clients turn a single event into a pipeline of warm connections.

Here's the full playbook, broken into three phases: Before, During, and After.

## Phase 1: BEFORE the Event

The work you do before you walk through the door determines 80% of your results. Most people skip this entirely. Don't be most people.

### Step 1: Find Events Worth Attending

Start by making a list of 5 to 10 events in your industry. You're looking for conferences, summits, meetups, and panels where people in your target field actually show up.

Here's a quick way to build that list using AI:

1. Go to [Perplexity AI](https://www.perplexity.ai/) (free research tool)
2. Type this prompt: "Make a list of conferences and events related to [YOUR INDUSTRY] in [YOUR CITY]. Output the result as a 5 column table: Name, Location, Date, Description, Website URL."
3. Run it a few times with different keywords to catch everything

Now you've got a list. Look at each event and ask yourself:

- Who are the speakers? What are they talking about?
- Who are the sponsor companies? Are they hiring?
- What's the price? Can I afford it?

That last question leads to the most underrated career hack I know.

### Step 2: Volunteer for Free Access

If an event costs money you don't have, ask to volunteer. I cannot overstate how powerful this is.

I've personally saved over $10,000 in conference fees by sending a simple email. One of my coaching clients got into an $1,800 event for free. Another client, Raj, used his podcast to offer live event content in exchange for a ticket. Nicole did the same thing at a different conference.

The worst case? You get no response. The best case? Free access, VIP treatment, and a direct line to the event organizers, who are typically the most connected people in the room.

Here's the email template I give all my clients:

> Hi [ORGANIZER NAME],
>
> I hope you're having a nice week!
>
> I wanted to reach out because I just came across the upcoming [EVENT NAME] event in [CITY] on [DATE]. I'm a [PROFESSION] looking to learn from the industry leaders in [INDUSTRY/FIELD].
>
> I'm currently between jobs, which makes the admission price a barrier for me to attend. Knowing that, I would still love to be involved. Are there any opportunities to volunteer with the event?
>
> Thanks for your time! I'm excited to find ways to bring value to this incredible event! I look forward to hearing from you!
>
> [YOUR NAME]
> [JOB TITLE]
> [LINKEDIN URL]

That's it. Short, respectful, honest. And it works more often than you'd expect.

Here's why volunteering is pure career rocket fuel:

- **Free access** to sessions, talks, and networking areas
- **Backstage access** to speakers, panelists, and VIPs
- **Direct relationship with organizers**, who are typically super connectors in their industry
- **Natural conversation starters** because you're part of the team, not just another attendee

One of my coaching group members, Priya, pointed out something important: paid event staff often get restricted from networking areas like expo halls. Volunteers get much better access. If your goal is making connections, volunteering beats paid event work every time.

**My Techweek NYC story.** In 2016, I was between jobs and wanted to break into tech. I found a conference called Techweek in New York City. Couldn't afford the ticket, the flight, or the hotel. So I asked to volunteer, booked a $50 bus ticket, and crashed on a friend's couch.

I showed up to every single event. Did every crappy job nobody else wanted. The organizers noticed. By the end of the week, they asked me to ring the opening bell at the NASDAQ stock exchange on national television. In a Pikachu costume. On the Times Square Jumbotron.

Then they invited me to the VIP event at One World Trade Center.

All because I sent one email asking to volunteer.

### Step 3: Do Your Research

Once you've got your ticket (paid or free), dig into the details:

**Research the speakers.** Look them up on LinkedIn. Read their bios. Figure out 2 to 3 specific questions you'd want to ask each one. Not generic questions like "What advice do you have for someone starting out?" Specific ones like "I saw you led the rebrand at [Company]. What was the hardest part of getting internal buy-in?"

**Research the sponsors.** Why are they at the event? Are they hiring? Launching a new product? Looking for partnerships? The more you know about their objectives, the more relevant your conversations become.

**Create a target list.** Pick 3 to 5 people you want to connect with. Write down why each one is interesting to you and what you'd want to talk about.

I once went to an event called Discover Your Personal Brand. Before attending, I made a spreadsheet of every speaker with their social media handles, talk topics, and time slots. I emailed it to the organizers thinking they might find it useful. When I showed up, they'd printed 30 copies to hand out at the event. Instant recognition, just from doing research I needed for myself anyway.

Think about whether the research you're doing for yourself could also be a value-add to the event. If so, share it.

### Step 4: Prepare Your Introduction

You need a clear, concise way to introduce yourself. Not a monologue. Not your entire career history. A short statement that communicates who you are, what you do, and what you're looking for.

If you haven't built yours yet, check out the [identity statement formula](/blog/craft-your-elevator-pitch-the-identity-statement-formula). It gives you a repeatable structure you can adapt on the fly depending on who you're talking to.

But here's the twist: don't lead with it. More on that in Phase 2.

## Phase 2: DURING the Event

You've done the prep. You're at the event. Now what?

### Step 5: The 80/20 Listening Rule

This is the single biggest tip I have for in-person networking: make every conversation 80% about the other person and 20% about you.

Be curious. Ask questions. Dig in. Here are some conversation starters that actually work:

- "What are you excited about at this conference?"
- "What's a big project you're working on right now?"
- "Have you had any big wins this week you're proud of?"
- "What are some of the biggest challenges you're dealing with?"
- "Who else here do you think I should meet?"

These questions get people talking. And people love talking about themselves. Not in a vain way. They're already thinking about their work, their projects, their problems. You're giving them permission to share.

When they share a challenge, that's your opening. Not to pitch yourself, but to add value: "Oh, I've dealt with something similar. We used [tool/approach] and it made a huge difference. I can send you the link after."

Now you've got a reason to follow up. That's how organic connections happen.

### Step 6: Let Them Talk First, Then Curate Your Pitch

One of the biggest mistakes people make at events is dumping their entire skill set on someone before learning anything about them.

I coach people to do the opposite. Let them talk first. Get a little intel. Then curate your introduction based on what they said.

Sandra, one of my coaching clients, used to list every role she'd ever held because she was afraid of missing an opportunity by narrowing her focus. But that approach overwhelms people. They don't know what to do with a laundry list of your capabilities.

Instead, listen to what they do. When you find a thread that connects to your experience, pull on it. If they mention they're struggling with developer documentation, and you've done technical writing, that's what you lead with. You don't need to mention the other 12 things you've done.

Focus on the value that connects to their world.

### Step 7: Be Yourself (The Megan Story)

Megan, one of my coaching clients, spent years networking the "right" way. Rigid. Professional. Reciting her skill set like a robot. People's eyes glazed over every time.

Then she decided to just have fun with it.

She was at SickKids, one of her dream organizations. She got on the elevator and it smelled like pizza. She joked about it with the woman next to her. They laughed. They walked together. Five minutes later, the woman took off her jacket and everyone in the room lit up: "Hi, Nancy!" Turns out she was the VP of philanthropy at SickKids and the keynote speaker.

Megan made more headway with that pizza joke than any elevator pitch would have landed. As she put it: "She'll always remember me as the person in the elevator who totally joked with her, as opposed to the person who gave her my resume in verbal form."

The lesson? Just showing up, being present, being yourself. That's what makes you memorable. Not a rehearsed pitch delivered under pressure.

### Step 8: Book a Call from a Call

There's a philosophy in sales: always book a call from a call. The same applies to networking at events.

Before you end any good conversation, ask one of these:

- "Who are 3 other people you think I should connect with?"
- "What's an event you'd recommend I check out next?"
- "Would you be open to grabbing a coffee sometime to continue this conversation?"

Don't let a great conversation end without a next step. The next step doesn't need to be a job lead. It can be an introduction, a resource, or just a reason to stay in touch.

## Phase 3: AFTER the Event

This is where most people drop the ball. They have 10 great conversations at an event and then do nothing. The connection goes cold. The opportunity disappears.

Don't be that person.

### Step 9: Follow Up Within 24 Hours

Speed matters. Send your follow-ups within 24 hours while the conversation is still fresh in both your minds.

For LinkedIn connections, send a personalized note. Not the default "I'd like to add you to my professional network" message. Reference something specific from your conversation:

> "Great meeting you at [Event] yesterday! I really enjoyed our conversation about [specific topic]. Here's that [resource/link/article] I mentioned. Would love to stay connected."

For deeper conversations, send an email. Same principle: reference something specific, offer value, suggest a next step.

If you want templates for these follow-up messages, check out the [LinkedIn outreach guide](/blog/linkedin-outreach-messages-that-actually-get-replies). The same principles apply: personalize to the person, not just the company. Reference their work, not just your needs.

And whatever you do, [don't pitch-slap](/blog/stop-pitch-slapping-on-linkedin). A follow-up message that immediately asks for a job or a favor undoes all the goodwill you built at the event.

### Step 10: Create LinkedIn Content from the Event

This is the advanced move, and it's one most people never think of.

Turn the event into LinkedIn content. Here's how:

**Quote graphics.** Hop onto Canva and turn something a speaker said into a quote graphic. Post it on LinkedIn and tag them. Most people don't get immortalized into quotes. Being tagged in a quote on a LinkedIn post is a gift. You're doing something that benefits their image and personal brand.

**Event recap carousel.** Create a LinkedIn carousel with a quote from each panelist or speaker. Tag all of them. Tag the event. Tag the event hashtag. This gives every person you tag a reason to engage with your post, and it positions you as someone who was paying attention and adding value.

**Key takeaways post.** Write a simple post: "3 things I learned at [Event]" with specific takeaways. Tag the speakers whose ideas you're sharing.

This approach does three things at once:

1. It gives you a reason to connect with speakers and attendees (you're promoting their work)
2. It builds your personal brand as someone who's active and engaged in the industry
3. It creates a warm follow-up opportunity ("Hey, I featured your quote in my LinkedIn post, thought you'd want to see it!")

## The Complete Checklist

Here's everything in one place:

**Before:**
- [ ] Make a list of 5 to 10 relevant events
- [ ] Email organizers to volunteer (use the template above)
- [ ] Research speakers and sponsors
- [ ] Create a target list of 3 to 5 people to connect with
- [ ] Prepare your [identity statement](/blog/craft-your-elevator-pitch-the-identity-statement-formula)
- [ ] Create a shareable resource (speaker spreadsheet, attendee guide, etc.)

**During:**
- [ ] Apply the 80/20 rule (80% listening, 20% talking)
- [ ] Let them talk first, then curate your pitch
- [ ] Be yourself. Ditch the robot script.
- [ ] Book a call from every good conversation
- [ ] Collect names, topics discussed, and follow-up commitments

**After:**
- [ ] Follow up within 24 hours
- [ ] Send personalized LinkedIn connection requests referencing the conversation
- [ ] Create at least one piece of LinkedIn content from the event
- [ ] Schedule any coffee chats or calls you committed to
- [ ] Add new contacts to your networking tracker

## Your Micro-Action for Today

Find one event in your industry happening in the next 60 days. Just one.

Go to the website. Look up the speakers. Check if there's a volunteer option. If it costs money, send the volunteer email template above.

You don't need to do everything in this playbook at your first event. Just showing up with a plan puts you ahead of 90% of attendees who walk in without one.

One event. One email. Start there.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What if I'm an introvert and hate networking events?

Good news: this playbook is built for introverts. The 80/20 rule means you're mostly listening, not performing. Volunteering gives you a built-in role so you're not standing around wondering what to do. And the research phase means you walk in with specific people to find and specific questions to ask, so you're never stuck in the "so, what do you do?" loop. Start with smaller events (50 to 100 people) before working up to big conferences. And give yourself permission to leave early if you've had 3 to 4 good conversations. Quality over quantity, always.

### How many events should I attend per month during a job search?

Aim for 2 to 4 events per month. That's enough to build momentum without burning out. Mix it up: one larger conference or summit, one smaller meetup or panel, and one or two virtual events. The key isn't volume. It's consistency. Attending one event and following up with everyone you met is worth more than attending five events and following up with nobody. Block time in your calendar for both the event and the follow-up work the next day.

### What do I do if I didn't connect with anyone interesting at the event?

First, check your expectations. "Interesting" doesn't mean they can give you a job tomorrow. Some of the most valuable connections I've made were with fellow attendees, not speakers or VIPs. Second, create that LinkedIn content anyway. A recap post tagging speakers and the event still gives you visibility, even if you didn't have a deep conversation with anyone. Third, reach out to speakers after the event even if you didn't talk in person. A thoughtful message referencing their talk still works: "I was at your session on [topic]. Your point about [specific thing] really stuck with me because [reason]. Would love to connect." Most speakers are happy to hear their talk landed with someone.
