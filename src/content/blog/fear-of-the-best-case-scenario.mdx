One of my clients was about to post "open to work" on LinkedIn. She'd updated her profile. Written the post. Had her finger on the publish button.

And she froze.

Not because she was afraid nobody would respond. Because she was afraid people *would*.

Responses meant messages. Messages meant conversations. Conversations meant interviews. And her last interview? 45 minutes of rapid-fire grilling from a senior engineer who made her feel like she didn't belong in the room.

So her brain did what brains do. It ran a quick risk assessment and decided: safest option is to not put yourself out there at all.

She closed the laptop. Didn't post. Didn't tell anyone she was looking. Stayed stuck for three more weeks.

This is the fear of the best-case scenario. And nobody talks about it.

## Why Success Scares You More Than Failure

We talk about fear of failure constantly. Entire self-help shelves are dedicated to it. But fear of success? That gets almost no airtime.

Here's why it's so sneaky: it doesn't feel like fear of success. It feels like procrastination. It feels like "I'm not ready yet." It feels like waiting for the perfect resume, the perfect moment, the perfect LinkedIn headline before you take action.

But underneath all of that is a simple calculation your nervous system is running without your permission:

**If I try and fail, nothing changes. If I try and succeed, everything changes.**

New job. New team. New expectations. New performance reviews. New chances to be exposed as someone who doesn't belong. Your brain would rather keep you in familiar discomfort than unfamiliar success.

That's not laziness. That's your survival wiring doing exactly what it was designed to do: avoid the unknown.

The problem is, the unknown is where every good thing in your career lives.

## The One Bad Interview Problem

Back to my client. Her entire avoidance strategy was built on one data point: that brutal 45-minute interview.

One person. One company. One bad day.

She'd turned that single experience into a universal rule: "Interviews are where I get humiliated." Her brain filed it under "DANGER" and blocked every path that led back there.

Here's what I told her: that interview wasn't a prophecy. It was a data point. One data point doesn't make a pattern. One rude interviewer doesn't mean all interviewers are rude. One tough experience doesn't mean every future experience will be tough.

If you ate one bad meal at a restaurant, you wouldn't stop eating forever. You'd just pick a different restaurant.

The same logic applies to your job search. That bad interview says something about that person at that company on that day. It says nothing about your future.

## The Fear Doesn't Go Away Before You Act

Here's the part most people get wrong: they wait for the fear to go away before they do the thing.

That's backwards.

The fear doesn't go away before you act. It goes away *because* you act.

You post "open to work" and the world doesn't end. You do the phone screen and it goes okay. You tell your STAR story in the interview and the hiring manager nods along. Each small action shrinks the fear a little more.

Not because you stopped being afraid. Because you proved to your nervous system that the feared outcome didn't happen.

One of my clients put it better than I ever could:

> "You're gonna feel like I can't do this. But then when you start doing it, you're like, what, I CAN do this."

That's the whole game. You act despite the fear. Then the fear catches up and realizes you're fine.

## The Best-Case Scenario Fear Checklist

If you're stuck and can't figure out why, run through this list. See if any of these sound familiar:

- **"What if I get the interview and bomb it?"** Then you learn something and do better next time. One bad interview is not a career sentence.
- **"What if I get the job and can't do it?"** Impostor syndrome talking. They hired you because you can do it. You'll learn the rest on the job, just like everyone else did.
- **"What if my network sees me as desperate?"** Nobody thinks that. "Open to work" signals action, not desperation. The people judging are the ones too scared to post it themselves.
- **"What if I get an offer and it's not perfect?"** You can negotiate. You can counter. You can say no. An offer gives you options. No offers give you nothing.
- **"What if I leave my current situation and regret it?"** What if you stay and regret that more? The status quo has a cost too. You're just used to paying it.

Every item on this list is your brain trying to protect you from change. But you don't need protection from change. You need a plan for navigating it.

## How to Move When Fear of the Best-Case Scenario Has You Stuck

Here's what I want you to do. Not think about. Do.

### 1. Name the fear out loud

Say it. "I'm afraid that if I apply, I'll actually get an interview, and I'll freeze." Naming it takes away its power. Vague dread is paralyzing. Specific fear is manageable.

### 2. Shrink the action

Don't start with "get a new job." Start with "update one section of my LinkedIn profile." Then "comment on one post in my target industry." Then "send one message to one connection." Small actions build momentum. Momentum builds confidence. Confidence is the antidote to fear.

### 3. Prepare for the best case

Instead of bracing for the worst, plan for the best. What if someone does respond to your "open to work" post? What will you say? Draft a template now, while you're calm, so you're not scrambling when it happens. Having [four strong interview stories](/blog/four-stories-you-need-for-any-interview) ready turns "what if they ask me hard questions?" into "I'm ready for this."

### 4. Set a fear deadline

Give yourself a date. "By Friday at 5pm, I'm posting." Tell someone. A friend, a coach, your mom. External accountability turns "I'll do it eventually" into "I'm doing it Friday."

### 5. Celebrate the action, not the outcome

You posted? Win. You applied? Win. You showed up to the interview? Win. Don't wait for the offer to feel good about yourself. The action itself is the victory. Everything after that is a bonus.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is fear of success a real thing, or am I just procrastinating?

Fear of the best-case scenario is a well-documented psychological pattern. It shows up as avoidance, perfectionism, and "not feeling ready" even when you objectively are. The difference between procrastination and success-fear: procrastination is about the task being unpleasant. Fear of success is about the outcome being overwhelming. If you've been "almost ready" for weeks, it's probably not procrastination. Your nervous system is protecting you from change. The fix is the same either way: take one small action today.

### How do I stop one bad experience from defining my entire job search?

Separate the data from the story. The data is: one interviewer was tough at one company on one day. The story your brain wrote is: all interviews are dangerous and you'll be humiliated. Challenge the story by collecting counter-evidence. Think of interviews that went fine, conversations that felt natural, moments where you impressed someone. One data point is not a pattern. Collect more data by putting yourself out there again.

### What if I turn on "open to work" and nobody responds?

Most people see engagement within the first week, especially if they pair the badge with a post about what they're looking for. If response is slow, it's not a verdict on your worth. It's feedback on your visibility. Start commenting on posts in your target industry. Share a useful article. Connect with people at your target companies. The "open to work" badge works best as part of an active LinkedIn presence, not as a stand-alone signal.
