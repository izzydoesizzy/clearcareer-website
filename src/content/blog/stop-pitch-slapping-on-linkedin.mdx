You know that feeling when someone connects with you on LinkedIn and, within 30 seconds, they've sent a wall of text about their product, their service, or themselves?

That's a pitch slap.

And if you're doing this during your job search, you're burning bridges before you even build them.

## What Is Pitch-Slapping on LinkedIn?

Pitch-slapping is when you skip the relationship and go straight to the ask. You connect with a hiring manager and immediately send: "Hi, I'd love to be considered for the Senior PM role at your company. Here's my resume."

No context. No warmth. No reason for this person to care.

It's the LinkedIn equivalent of walking up to a stranger at a party and handing them your business card before saying hello.

Here's the hard truth: people help people they know, like, and trust. Pitch-slapping skips all three.

## Why the "Spray and Pray" Approach Fails

I see job seekers do this constantly. They send 50 identical messages to 50 hiring managers and wonder why nobody responds. The response rate on cold pitch slaps hovers around 2-3%. That's not networking. That's spam with a professional headshot.

The [hidden job market](/blog/project-manage-your-job-search-like-a-paid-project) runs on relationships. Real ones. The kind you can't manufacture in a single message.

## The Farming Analogy: You Can't Harvest What You Haven't Planted

Dr. Ivan Misner, founder of BNI (Business Network International), puts it best: networking is about farming, not hunting.

Hunting is reactive. You're hungry, you chase something, you either catch it or you don't. Farming is proactive. You plant seeds, tend them, water them, and eventually you have more than you could ever use.

Most job seekers are hunters. They only reach out when they need something. That's backwards.

The best networkers are farmers. They're planting seeds all year long. So when they need help, the harvest is already there.

## 5 Ways to Give Value Before You Ever Ask

Here's what I want you to do. Before you send any ask to anyone on LinkedIn, give first. Every single time. Here are five ways to do that.

### 1. Reference Their Work

Read something they posted. Read their company's latest blog. Watch their conference talk. Then send a message that proves you did.

"I saw your post about cross-functional collaboration last week. The part about setting shared OKRs across teams is exactly what we struggled with at my last company. Great insight."

This takes 5 minutes. It separates you from 99% of people in their inbox.

### 2. Share Their Content

See a post from someone you want to build a relationship with? Share it. Add your own perspective. Tag them. This puts you on their radar without asking for a single thing.

### 3. Send Relevant Articles

Find an article related to their industry, their role, or a challenge they've talked about. Send it with a short note: "Thought of you when I saw this. The section on [specific detail] seemed relevant to what you're building at [Company]."

You're showing that you pay attention. That's rare.

### 4. Make Introductions

Know two people who should meet? Introduce them. This is one of the highest-value things you can do in any professional network. It costs you nothing and creates goodwill with both parties.

"Hey Sarah, meet James. James, Sarah runs product at [Company] and has been doing incredible work on [specific thing]. Thought you two would have a lot to talk about."

### 5. Close the Loop

This is the one nobody does. If someone gave you advice, told you about a company, or made an introduction, follow up and tell them what happened.

"Hey, I wanted to circle back. I reached out to the contact you suggested at [Company] and we had a great conversation. They pointed me toward a role that's a strong fit. Thank you for that intro."

Closing the loop makes people want to help you again. And again. And again.

## How to Build This Into Your Weekly Routine

Don't overcomplicate this. Spend 15 minutes a day on LinkedIn doing one of these five things. That's it.

Monday: Comment on two posts from people in your target industry.
Tuesday: Share someone's content with your own take.
Wednesday: Send one article to a connection with a personal note.
Thursday: Make one introduction.
Friday: Close one loop.

In a month, you'll have dozens of warm connections. In three months, people will start reaching out to *you*.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many LinkedIn messages should I send per week during a job search?

Quality beats quantity every time. Aim for 5-10 thoughtful, personalized messages per week rather than 50 generic ones. Each message should reference something specific about the person or their work. A 30% response rate on 10 great messages beats a 2% response rate on 100 pitch slaps.

### Is it okay to reach out to hiring managers directly on LinkedIn?

Absolutely. Hiring managers expect to hear from candidates. The key is timing and approach. Give value first by engaging with their content for a week or two. Then send a short, specific message that shows you've done your research. Skip the resume attachment on the first message.

### How long should I wait after connecting before making an ask?

There's no magic number, but aim for at least 2-3 genuine interactions before you ask for anything. That could be comments on their posts, sharing their content, or sending a relevant article. The goal is to become a familiar name in their notifications before you show up in their inbox with a request.
