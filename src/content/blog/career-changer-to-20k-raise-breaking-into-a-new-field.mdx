## The Wall

One of my clients had spent a decade in education. She was good at it. Caring, organized, driven. But she was ready for something different, and she'd set her sights on customer success and UX.

The problem: after a year of applications and countless rejections, she was stuck. The job market didn't seem to recognize her skills. Every posting wanted "3-5 years of customer success experience," and no matter how she framed her teaching background, she kept getting filtered out.

By the time we started working together, she was overwhelmed. Not just by the rejections, but by the sheer volume of conflicting advice. Every blog post, every LinkedIn influencer, every well-meaning friend had a different opinion about what she should be doing. Rewrite your resume this way. No, that way. Use this template. No, use AI. Network more. Apply more. Apply less.

The noise was paralyzing.

## The Real Problem

Her resume wasn't bad. It was unfocused.

She'd been trying to do two things at once: show her education experience AND convince employers she could do customer success work. The result was a resume that didn't fully commit to either story. Hiring managers in customer success saw "educator" and moved on. The transferable skills were there, but they were buried.

The other issue was metrics. In education, you don't typically think about your work in terms of numbers. You think about students helped, lessons taught, programs run. All valuable. But the job market in customer success and UX wants to see impact quantified: retention rates, satisfaction scores, process improvements, cost savings.

She had those numbers. She just hadn't thought to count them.

## What We Changed

### 1. One story, told clearly

We stopped trying to straddle two worlds and committed fully to the customer success narrative. Every bullet on her resume answered one question: "Why is this person going to be great at customer success?"

Her teaching experience became:
- **"Managed 150+ stakeholder relationships annually"** (parent-teacher communication)
- **"Designed onboarding programs that reduced ramp time by 30%"** (new student orientation)
- **"Achieved 95% satisfaction rate across 4 consecutive program cycles"** (end-of-year surveys)

Same work. Different framing. If you want to learn how to do this for your own experience, read [Achievement Mining: How to Quantify Your Resume Bullets](/blog/achievement-mining-how-to-quantify-your-resume-bullets).

### 2. Reducing the noise

We threw out the conflicting advice and built one clear system:
- Target 10-15 companies, not 100
- Research each one deeply
- Reach out to people at those companies before applying
- Tailor every application to the specific role

Simple. Not easy. But simple.

### 3. Interview prep that built confidence

The weekly group coaching sessions did something her solo job search couldn't: they normalized the struggle. She saw other smart, experienced people dealing with the same rejections and setbacks. That alone cut through the isolation.

Then we worked on her interview approach. Instead of apologizing for not having "traditional" customer success experience, she led with the problems she'd solved and the results she'd delivered. The framing shifted from "I used to be a teacher" to "I've managed complex stakeholder relationships, built systems that scaled, and consistently delivered measurable outcomes."

For more on structuring your interview stories, check out [Four Stories You Need for Any Interview](/blog/four-stories-you-need-for-any-interview).

## The Result

After months of coaching, interview practice, and targeted applications, she accepted a role in customer success with:

- **$20,000 salary increase** over her previous position
- **$5,000-$10,000 annual bonus**
- **25% total pay raise**

The financial stability changed things for her family immediately. But the confidence shift was just as significant. She stopped seeing herself as "just a teacher trying to break in" and started owning her value as a professional with a decade of stakeholder management, program design, and outcome delivery.

## Why Career Changers Undervalue Themselves

If you're changing careers, here's what's probably happening: you're comparing yourself to people who have direct experience in your target field, and you're concluding that you're behind.

You're not behind. You're different. And different is valuable.

Companies don't just hire for technical skills. They hire for problem-solving, communication, relationship management, and the ability to learn quickly. Career changers bring perspectives that career lifers don't have. The key is framing your experience in terms your target industry understands.

Three principles for career changers:

1. **Translate, don't apologize.** Your experience is valuable. The work is making it legible to a new audience.
2. **Lead with outcomes, not job titles.** Nobody cares that you were a "Grade 5 Teacher." They care that you managed 150 stakeholder relationships and achieved 95% satisfaction.
3. **Get in the room differently.** When you're changing fields, online applications are the hardest path. Networking, informational interviews, and direct outreach get you conversations where you can tell your story. ## Your Micro-Action for Today

Take your 3 most recent job responsibilities. For each one, ask: "What happened because I did this? What would have happened if I didn't?"

The answers are your metrics. Write them down. If you're a career changer, these translated accomplishments are the bridge between your past experience and your next role.
