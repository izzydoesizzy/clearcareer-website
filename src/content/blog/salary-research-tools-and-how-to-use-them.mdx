"What are your salary expectations?"

Six words that make most people freeze. You either lowball yourself and leave $20K on the table, or throw out a number so high the recruiter goes silent.

Here's the fix: stop guessing. Use salary research tools to build a range backed by real data. When they ask your salary expectations, you want three data points, not one. A low, a mid, and a high, all supported by evidence you can cite out loud.

That's not negotiating aggressively. That's negotiating smart.

Let me walk you through the 5 salary research tools I recommend to every client, how to use each one, and how to combine them into a range that gives you confidence and leverage.

## 1. Glassdoor: Company-Specific Salary Data

**What it does:** Shows salary ranges reported by employees at specific companies, broken down by role, location, and experience level.

**Who it's for:** Anyone targeting specific companies. Glassdoor is strongest when you know where you're interviewing.

**Free vs. paid:** Free with an account (you'll need to leave a review or salary report to unlock full access).

**How to use it:**

1. Search for the exact job title at the specific company
2. Look at the base salary range, not just the average
3. Check the number of salary reports. Under 10 reports? Take it with a grain of salt
4. Note the bonus and stock ranges separately

**Izzy's take:** Glassdoor is your starting point, not your ending point. The data skews slightly low because people who are underpaid are more likely to report their salaries. Use it as your floor, not your ceiling.

## 2. Levels.fyi: Tech Compensation Data

**What it does:** Breaks down total compensation packages at tech companies, including base, stock, bonus, and sign-on. The data is incredibly granular.

**Who it's for:** Anyone in tech, engineering, product, design, or data roles. If you're targeting FAANG, Big Tech, or well-funded startups, this is your best resource.

**Free vs. paid:** Free for basic searches. Paid tier gives you more filters and comparisons.

**How to use it:**

1. Search by company and level (L3, L4, L5, etc.)
2. Look at total comp, not just base. Stock and bonus can double the base at senior levels
3. Filter by location to adjust for cost-of-living differences
4. Compare across companies at the same level to see the spread

**Izzy's take:** Levels.fyi is a game changer for tech compensation. The total comp breakdowns are the most accurate I've seen. If you're in tech and you're not using this, you're negotiating blind.

## 3. Payscale: Market Salary Ranges

**What it does:** Generates personalized salary reports based on your job title, experience, education, location, and skills. Gives you a percentile ranking.

**Who it's for:** Anyone. Works across industries, not just tech.

**Free vs. paid:** Free basic report. Paid version gives more detail and employer-specific data.

**How to use it:**

1. Complete their salary survey (takes about 5 minutes)
2. Review your percentile ranking within the role
3. Use the range to understand where you fall: 25th, 50th, or 75th percentile
4. Run multiple scenarios with different titles or locations to see how the range shifts

**Izzy's take:** Payscale is the most versatile tool on this list. It works for marketing managers, operations directors, and finance analysts just as well as it works for software engineers. The percentile ranking is especially useful when building your negotiation case.

## 4. Indeed Salary Search: Volume-Based Data

**What it does:** Aggregates salary data from job postings and employee reports. Shows averages by role and location.

**Who it's for:** Good for broad strokes. Best when you want to understand the general market for a role.

**Free vs. paid:** Completely free.

**How to use it:**

1. Search by job title and location
2. Look at the salary range, not just the average
3. Check whether the data comes from job postings or employee reports (job posting data tends to be more current)
4. Compare across similar job titles to account for title inflation

**Izzy's take:** Indeed has massive data volume, which is its strength and its weakness. The averages can be misleading because they blend entry-level and senior roles together. Use it as one data point among several, never as your only source.

## 5. LinkedIn Salary: Network-Driven Insights

**What it does:** Shows salary ranges based on data submitted by LinkedIn members. Includes base, bonus, and other compensation. Also shows how factors like education, industry, and company size affect pay.

**Who it's for:** Anyone with a LinkedIn account. Strongest for roles where LinkedIn has high member density (business, tech, marketing, sales).

**Free vs. paid:** Free with a LinkedIn account.

**How to use it:**

1. Search by job title and location
2. Use the filters to narrow by industry, company size, and years of experience
3. Pay attention to the bonus and equity breakdowns
4. Compare how compensation shifts between metro areas

**Izzy's take:** LinkedIn's data is solid because it comes from a massive professional network. The geographic comparisons are especially useful if you're considering relocation or remote roles with location-based pay bands.

## How to Triangulate Your Salary Range

Using one tool gives you a number. Using five tools gives you a range. Here's how to combine them.

**Step 1: Gather data from all five sources**

Search the same job title and location in each tool. Write down the low, median, and high from each one.

**Step 2: Throw out outliers**

If one source says $80K and the other four say $110K-$130K, the $80K is noise. Ignore it.

**Step 3: Build your range**

- **Low:** The 25th percentile across your sources. This is your walk-away number.
- **Mid:** The median across your sources. This is what you'd be happy accepting.
- **High:** The 75th percentile. This is your opening ask.

**Step 4: Anchor to the high**

When the recruiter asks your expectations, lead with the top of your range. "Based on my research, I'm targeting $135K-$150K for this role." Now the conversation starts at the top, not the bottom.

## Why a Range Works Better Than a Single Number

A single number is a trap. Too high and you scare them off. Too low and you've anchored yourself below market.

A range does three things:

1. **Shows you've done your homework.** Citing a range with sources signals professionalism
2. **Gives the company room to work.** They can come in at the low end and feel like they got a deal. You can accept at the low end and still be at market rate
3. **Keeps the door open.** A range is a conversation starter. A single number is an ultimatum

The sweet spot: your bottom number should be the lowest you'd happily accept. Your top number should be ambitious but defensible with data.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What if different salary tools show very different numbers for the same role?

That's normal and expected. Each tool uses different data sources, sample sizes, and methodologies. The key is triangulation: gather data from at least three sources, throw out extreme outliers, and build your range from the overlap. If Glassdoor says $110K-$130K and Levels.fyi says $120K-$145K, your realistic range is $115K-$140K. Differences tell you the market has variance, which is useful information during negotiation.

### Should I share my salary research with the recruiter during negotiation?

Yes, briefly. Cite your sources without over-explaining. Say something like: "Based on market data from Glassdoor, Payscale, and Levels.fyi, the range for this role in this market is $120K-$145K. Given my 8 years of experience and background in [specific skill], I'm targeting the upper end of that range." Data-backed asks are harder to dismiss than gut-feel numbers.

### How often should I research my market value, even if I'm not job searching?

At least once a year. Salaries shift with market conditions, and knowing your current market value gives you leverage during annual reviews, promotion conversations, and unexpected opportunities. Set a calendar reminder to spend 30 minutes updating your salary research every January. If you discover you're significantly below market, that's a conversation worth having with your manager.
