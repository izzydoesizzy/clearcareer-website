You got the offer. You're relieved. Excited. Maybe even a little giddy. And your first instinct is to say yes immediately before they change their mind.

Don't.

The first offer is never the final offer. Every recruiter, every hiring manager, every VP of talent knows this. They built room into the number. They expect you to negotiate. The only person who doesn't know this? You.

And that silence is costing you real money.

## Why People Don't Negotiate (and Why They're Wrong)

The #1 fear I hear from clients: "What if they pull the offer?"

In 200+ clients I've coached through salary negotiations, that has never happened. Not once. Zero times. Let me say that louder for the people in the back: **no one has ever lost an offer because they asked for more money.**

Here's what actually happens when you negotiate: the company respects you more. You've just demonstrated that you know your value, you can advocate for yourself, and you're willing to have hard conversations. Those are the exact skills they're hiring you for.

The real risk isn't negotiating. It's not negotiating. Because that first number? It follows you. Every raise, every bonus, every stock refresh is a percentage of your base. Start low, stay low.

## The ROI Math That Should Make You Uncomfortable

Let's do some math that most people avoid.

Say you're targeting a $200K salary. Your burn rate is $3,846 per week. If your job search takes 5 months at a salary that's $15K below what you could've negotiated, here's what that looks like:

- **5 months of search time at $75K opportunity cost = $31,250 in missed income**
- That's a brand new car
- Over 5 years, a $15K difference compounds to $75,000+ (before raises)
- Over 10 years? You're looking at $150,000+

One conversation. 15 minutes. Six figures of lifetime earnings on the table.

And you're going to skip it because you feel awkward?

That's a solvable problem.

## The Negotiation Script (Word for Word)

Here's what I want you to say when you get the offer. Practice it out loud. Seriously.

**Step 1: Express enthusiasm (buy yourself time)**

> "Thank you so much for this offer. I'm really excited about this role and about joining the team. I'd love to take a day or two to review the full package. Can I come back to you by [specific date]?"

This is non-negotiable. Never negotiate on the spot. You need time to think, research, and prepare.

**Step 2: Do your homework**

Use that time to research comparable salaries. Pull data from Glassdoor, Levels.fyi, Payscale, and LinkedIn Salary. Build your case with numbers, not feelings. Check out our full breakdown of [salary research tools](/blog/salary-research-tools-and-how-to-use-them) for how to do this right.

**Step 3: Make the ask**

> "I've done some research on the market rate for this role, and based on my experience and the scope of this position, I was hoping we could get closer to [your target number]. I'm basing that on [specific data point] and [specific data point]. Is there flexibility there?"

That's it. No apologies. No "I know this is a lot to ask." No rambling justifications. State the number. Cite the data. Ask the question.

**Step 4: Handle the pushback**

If they say "that's at the top of our range," try this:

> "I understand there may be constraints on base salary. Are there other parts of the package we could look at? Signing bonus, additional PTO, stock options, or a six-month salary review?"

You just opened five new doors. Most companies have more flexibility on these components than on base salary.

## Beyond Base Salary: What Else to Negotiate

Base salary gets all the attention, but the full package matters. Here's what's on the table:

- **Signing bonus**: One-time cash. Great for offsetting lost income from your job search
- **Stock or equity**: Can be worth more than salary at growth companies
- **PTO**: Extra vacation days cost the company nothing. Big quality-of-life win for you
- **Remote work flexibility**: Worth $5K-$15K in commute savings and time
- **Start date**: Need an extra two weeks? Ask for it
- **Professional development budget**: Conference tickets, courses, certifications
- **Salary review timeline**: Ask for a 6-month review instead of waiting 12 months

One of my clients negotiated $35K above the initial offer by combining a $10K base increase, a $15K signing bonus, and $10K in additional equity. The company didn't blink. They'd already decided they wanted her. The negotiation was just a formality.

That's the part most people miss. By the time you have an offer, they've already chosen you. You have leverage. Use it.

And if you're weighing a contract offer against a salaried role, the math changes dramatically. Read [Contract vs. Salary: The Math Nobody Does](/blog/contract-vs-salary-the-math-nobody-does) before you make any decisions.

## When NOT to Negotiate

I believe you should almost always negotiate. But there are a few exceptions:

- **The offer is already above your research-backed range.** If your data says $150K-$170K and they offer $175K, say thank you. You can still negotiate other parts of the package.
- **You've been explicitly told the offer is non-negotiable and it's a government or union role with fixed pay bands.** Even then, ask about signing bonuses or start dates.

That's a short list. For most people in most situations, negotiate.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can negotiating a job offer cause the company to rescind it?

In over 200 salary negotiations I've coached, not a single offer has been rescinded because a candidate asked for more. Companies expect you to negotiate. The offer includes built-in room for discussion. A professional, data-backed counteroffer shows confidence and self-advocacy. If a company pulled an offer over a polite negotiation, that tells you everything you need to know about working there.

### How much more should I ask for above the initial offer?

Aim for 10-20% above the initial offer, backed by market data. Pull salary ranges from Glassdoor, Levels.fyi, and Payscale to build your case. If the offer is $120K, a counteroffer of $132K-$144K is reasonable when supported by research. Even a 5% increase on a $150K salary adds up to $37,500+ over five years before raises.

### Should I negotiate if I'm just happy to have the offer?

Yes. Being grateful for the offer and negotiating it are not opposites. You can express genuine excitement about the role while also asking for fair compensation. The first offer is a starting point, not a ceiling. Skipping the negotiation leaves money on the table that follows you for the rest of your career through percentage-based raises, bonuses, and retirement contributions.
