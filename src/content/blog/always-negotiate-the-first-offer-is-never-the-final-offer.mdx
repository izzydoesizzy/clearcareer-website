You got the offer. You're relieved. Excited. Maybe even a little giddy. And your first instinct is to say yes immediately before they change their mind.

Don't.

The first offer is never the final offer. Every recruiter, every hiring manager, every VP of talent knows this. They built room into the number. They expect you to negotiate. The only person who doesn't know this? You.

And that silence is costing you real money.

## Why People Don't Negotiate (and Why They're Wrong)

The #1 fear I hear from clients: "What if they pull the offer?"

In 200+ clients I've coached through salary negotiations, that has never happened. Not once. Zero times. Let me say that louder for the people in the back: **no one has ever lost an offer because they asked for more money.**

What actually happens when you negotiate: the company respects you more. You've just demonstrated that you know your value, you can advocate for yourself, and you're willing to have hard conversations. Those are the exact skills they're hiring you for.

The real risk isn't negotiating. It's not negotiating. Because that first number follows you. Every raise, every bonus, every stock refresh is a percentage of your base. Start low, stay low.

My client Nicole told me something that stuck with me: "Before meeting you, I probably wouldn't have negotiated. I would have just said okay and kept it moving." She negotiated anyway. The result? A 22% salary increase over her previous highest salary. That's not a small bump. That's a different financial trajectory.

## The ROI Math That Should Make You Uncomfortable

Let's do some math that most people avoid.

Say you're targeting a $200K salary. Your burn rate is $3,846 per week. If your job search takes 5 months at a salary that's $15K below what you could've negotiated, here's what that looks like:

- **5 months of search time at $75K opportunity cost = $31,250 in missed income**
- That's a brand new car
- Over 5 years, a $15K difference compounds to $75,000+ (before raises)
- Over 10 years? You're looking at $150,000+

One conversation. 15 minutes. Six figures of lifetime earnings on the table.

And you're going to skip it because you feel awkward?

Salary negotiation is my favorite stage of coaching because it's so fun. It's 1 to 2 hours of prep and can result in $10,000, $15,000, $20,000, even $30,000 salary increases. That's an incredible return on a small investment of time.

## Phase 1: Research Your Market Value

Before you negotiate anything, you need data. Not feelings. Not vibes. Data.

The mistake most people make is picking a number out of thin air, or worse, anchoring to their previous salary. Your previous salary is irrelevant. What matters is the market rate for the role you're stepping into.

### The 5 Salary Research Tools I Recommend

**1. Glassdoor** (Company-specific salary data)
Search for the exact job title at the specific company. Look at the base salary range, not just the average. Check whether there are at least 10 salary reports. Under 10? Take it with a grain of salt. Glassdoor is your starting point, not your ending point. The data skews slightly low because people who are underpaid are more likely to report their salaries. Use it as your floor, not your ceiling.

**2. Levels.fyi** (Tech compensation breakdowns)
If you're in tech, engineering, product, design, or data, this is your best resource. Search by company and level. Look at total comp, not just base. Stock and bonus can double the base at senior levels. Filter by location to adjust for cost-of-living differences. The total comp breakdowns are the most accurate I've seen.

**3. Payscale** (Cross-industry market ranges)
The most versatile tool on this list. Works for marketing managers, operations directors, and finance analysts just as well as for software engineers. Complete their salary survey (5 minutes), review your percentile ranking within the role, and run multiple scenarios with different titles or locations to see how the range shifts.

**4. Indeed Salary Search** (Volume-based data)
Massive data volume, which is both its strength and weakness. The averages can be misleading because they blend entry-level and senior roles. Use it as one data point among several, never as your only source.

**5. LinkedIn Salary** (Network-driven insights)
Solid data from a massive professional network. The geographic comparisons are especially useful if you're considering relocation or remote roles with location-based pay bands. Use the filters to narrow by industry, company size, and years of experience.

### How to Triangulate Your Range

Using one tool gives you a number. Using five gives you a range.

**Step 1:** Search the same job title and location in each tool. Write down the low, median, and high from each.

**Step 2:** Throw out outliers. If one source says $80K and the other four say $110K-$130K, the $80K is noise.

**Step 3:** Build your range.
- **Low:** The 25th percentile across sources. This is your walk-away number.
- **Mid:** The median across sources. This is what you'd be happy accepting.
- **High:** The 75th percentile. This is your opening ask.

When my client Raj was preparing to negotiate an offer, he went and talked to multiple people at the company, got multiple figures from different employees, and built a confident range based on real data. That's superstar-level research. You don't always have to go that far, but the more data points you have, the stronger your position.

### Why a Range Beats a Single Number

A single number is a trap. Too high and you scare them off. Too low and you've anchored yourself below market.

A range does three things:
1. **Shows you've done your homework.** Citing a range with sources signals professionalism.
2. **Gives the company room to work.** They can come in at the low end and feel like they got a deal. You can accept at the low end and still be at market rate.
3. **Keeps the door open.** A range is a conversation starter. A single number is an ultimatum.

When the recruiter asks your expectations, lead with the top of your range: "Based on my research, I'm targeting $135K-$150K for this role." Now the conversation starts at the top, not the bottom.

## Phase 2: The Negotiation Mindset

Before we get into scripts, let's talk about how to think about this conversation. Because the mindset matters as much as the words.

### Collaborative, Not Adversarial

The #1 rule: keep it collaborative. You're on the same team. You both want this to work. Frame every ask as: "How can we make this work for both of us?"

Never use the word "however" in a salary negotiation. It immediately puts them on the other side. Say "and" instead. "I'm excited about this offer, and I'd love to explore whether there's flexibility on the base." See the difference? "However" creates opposition. "And" creates partnership.

### Anchor to Value, Not Personal Needs

Never say "I need this because my rent is this much" or "I have a family to support." That's not their problem. Their problem is finding the best person for the role.

The money they're paying you is reinvested into their own growth and success. Your job in the negotiation is to show them the return on that investment. What unique value do you bring? What specific results have you delivered? That's where you anchor.

As I coached Raj: "You being unemployed doesn't impact your value. The value that you bring is this is the market rate, and here's the specific unique experience that you're going to bring to this role. Anchor your ask on that, not on personal financial needs."

### The Long-Term Partner Frame

Frame yourself as a long-term partner, not just someone filling a seat. When they ask about salary, lead with something like: "I'm looking to build a long-term career here. I want to grow with this company."

That changes the dynamic from a transactional hire to an investment. Companies invest more in people they think will stay. If they believe you're there for 2+ years, they're more willing to stretch on compensation because they're not going to have to backfill the role in six months.

## Phase 3: The Negotiation Script (Word for Word)

**Step 1: Express enthusiasm (buy yourself time)**

> "Thank you so much for this offer. I'm really excited about this role and about joining the team. I'd love to take a day or two to review the full package. Can I come back to you by [specific date]?"

This is non-negotiable. Never negotiate on the spot. You need time to think, research, and prepare.

**Step 2: Do your homework**

Use that time to pull data from all five salary tools. Build your case with numbers, not feelings. Build your low/mid/high range.

**Step 3: Communicate your intention to accept**

Before making any ask, say something like: "I'm very excited about this role and I intend to accept. I just want to make sure we can align on a few things."

This is critical. It tells them you're not playing games. You're serious. You want to be there. You just want to make sure the terms work.

**Step 4: Make the ask**

> "I've done some research on the market rate for this role, and based on my experience and the scope of this position, I was hoping we could get closer to [your target number]. I'm basing that on [specific data point] and [specific data point]. Is there flexibility there?"

No apologies. No "I know this is a lot to ask." No rambling justifications. State the number. Cite the data. Ask the question. Then stop talking. Let the silence work for you.

## Phase 4: The Two-Option Framework

This is the approach I teach in coaching, and it works better than a straight counter-offer every time.

Instead of countering with a single number (which can feel like a rejection of their offer), you give them two options:

**Option A:** Higher salary, same benefits package.
**Option B:** Original salary they offered, plus enhanced benefits like signing bonus, extra vacation, professional development budget, or an earlier salary review date.

This works because you're not saying no. You're saying yes, and here are two ways we can make this work. The hiring manager can pick the one that works within their constraints. Sometimes they have rigid salary bands they can't break but they have discretionary budget for signing bonuses or professional development.

My client Zoe used this exact approach. She wrote an email with two options. The company came back with a hybrid of the two that was actually better than either option she proposed. Because they felt like they were collaborating on a solution rather than being pushed into a corner.

A single counter feels like a rejection of their offer. Two options feel like you're working together. Always frame it as excitement about the role first. Lead with how excited you are, then present the options as ways to make it work for both sides.

### How to Write the Two-Option Email

Here's the template:

> Subject: Excited About the Offer, a Couple of Options to Discuss
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> Thank you again for the offer. I'm genuinely excited about joining the team and contributing to [specific project or goal discussed in interview].
>
> I've given the compensation package careful thought and I'd love to explore two options:
>
> **Option A:** A base salary of [target number], with the benefits package as offered. I'm basing this on market data from [sources] and the value I'd bring in [specific area].
>
> **Option B:** The offered base salary of [their number], with [specific additional benefit: $X signing bonus / X additional PTO days / 6-month salary review instead of 12-month / $X professional development budget].
>
> I'm open to either direction and happy to discuss what works best on your end. Looking forward to making this work.
>
> Best,
> [Your name]

Short. Clean. Collaborative. Two paths forward. No ultimatums.

## Phase 5: Beyond Base Salary

Base salary gets all the attention, but the full package matters. Here's what else is on the table:

- **Signing bonus**: One-time cash. Great for offsetting lost income from a job search
- **Stock or equity**: Can be worth more than salary at growth companies
- **PTO**: Extra vacation days cost the company very little. Big quality-of-life win for you
- **Remote work flexibility**: Worth $5K-$15K in commute savings and time
- **Start date**: Need an extra two weeks? Ask for it
- **Professional development budget**: Conference tickets, courses, certifications
- **Salary review timeline**: Ask for a 6-month review instead of waiting 12 months

One of my clients negotiated $35K above the initial offer by combining a $10K base increase, a $15K signing bonus, and $10K in additional equity. The company didn't blink. They'd already decided they wanted her. The negotiation was just a formality.

That's the part most people miss. By the time you have an offer, they've already chosen you. You have leverage. Use it.

## Handling Pushback

If they say "that's at the top of our range," try this:

> "I understand there may be constraints on base salary. Are there other parts of the package we could look at? Signing bonus, additional PTO, stock options, or a six-month salary review?"

You just opened five new doors. Most companies have more flexibility on these components than on base salary.

If they say the offer is final, consider the Raj approach. When he was evaluating a role at EOG Resources in Houston, the salary was $65/hour versus his target of $75/hour. But the role checked every other box: right industry, right location, right type of work. My advice: focus on being the best candidate first. Let them want you. Then you're in a much better position to negotiate. Sometimes the right role at a slightly lower number still beats waiting for a perfect package that never shows up.

## When NOT to Negotiate

I believe you should almost always negotiate. But there are a few exceptions:

- **The offer is already above your research-backed range.** If your data says $150K-$170K and they offer $175K, say thank you. You can still negotiate other parts of the package.
- **It's a government or union role with fixed pay bands.** Even then, ask about signing bonuses or start dates.

That's a short list. For most people in most situations, negotiate. Asking 10-20% more than the first offer is not unreasonable when it's backed by research.

And if you're weighing a contract offer against a salaried role, the math changes dramatically. Read [Contract vs. Salary: The Math Nobody Does](/blog/contract-vs-salary-the-math-nobody-does) before you make any decisions.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can negotiating a job offer cause the company to rescind it?

In over 200 salary negotiations I've coached, not a single offer has been rescinded because a candidate asked for more. Companies expect you to negotiate. The offer includes built-in room for discussion. A professional, data-backed counteroffer shows confidence and self-advocacy. If a company pulled an offer over a polite negotiation, that tells you everything you need to know about working there.

### How much more should I ask for above the initial offer?

Aim for 10-20% above the initial offer, backed by market data. Pull salary ranges from Glassdoor, Levels.fyi, and Payscale to build your case. If the offer is $120K, a counteroffer of $132K-$144K is reasonable when supported by research. Even a 5% increase on a $150K salary adds up to $37,500+ over five years before raises. The two-option framework makes this easier because you're not just naming a higher number. You're giving them two collaborative paths forward.

### Should I negotiate if I'm just happy to have the offer?

Yes. Being grateful for the offer and negotiating it are not opposites. You can express genuine excitement about the role while also asking for fair compensation. The first offer is a starting point, not a ceiling. Skipping the negotiation leaves money on the table that follows you for the rest of your career through percentage-based raises, bonuses, and retirement contributions. Nicole almost didn't negotiate because she was just happy to have the offer. She negotiated anyway and landed a 22% salary jump. That's the difference between gratitude and leaving money behind.
