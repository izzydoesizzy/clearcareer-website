"Marketing Manager at XYZ Corp"

## Why Your LinkedIn Headline Matters So Much

LinkedIn gives you 220 characters for your headline. Most people use about 30 of them for their job title and call it done.

Here's what they're missing:

- **Recruiters search by keywords.** Your headline is one of the most heavily weighted fields in LinkedIn's search algorithm. If a recruiter searches "B2B SaaS Product Manager" and your headline just says "Product Manager," you're less likely to show up.
- **It's the first thing people see.** Before your summary, before your experience, before your photo. Your headline appears in search results, in the "People You May Know" sidebar, in comment threads, everywhere.
- **It frames how people perceive you.** A strong headline says "I know exactly what I do and I'm great at it." A generic one says "I exist and have a job."

Think of it this way: if your LinkedIn headline were printed on a billboard and 10,000 recruiters drove past it every day, would any of them pull over?

## The 3-Part LinkedIn Headline Formula

Here's the formula I use with every client. Simple, effective, and endlessly customizable.

**Target Role + Key Skills/Tools + Who You Help (or What You Deliver)**

That's it. Three parts. Let's break them down.

### Part 1: Target Role

This is the job title you WANT, not necessarily the one you have. If you're a Marketing Coordinator aiming for Marketing Manager roles, lead with "Marketing Manager."

Recruiters search for the role they're hiring for. If that title isn't in your headline, you're invisible.

### Part 2: Key Skills or Tools

What do you bring to the table that's specific and searchable? Think tools, methodologies, platforms, and specialties.

Not: "experienced in marketing"
Yes: "SEO, Paid Media, HubSpot"

Not: "data professional"
Yes: "SQL, Python, Tableau"

These keywords make you findable. They also signal your depth immediately.

### Part 3: Who You Help or What You Deliver

This is the part that turns your headline from a label into a value proposition. It answers: "So what?"

"Helping B2B SaaS companies reduce churn by 30%"
"Building data pipelines for healthcare companies"
"Driving revenue growth for e-commerce brands"

This part makes recruiters think: "That's exactly what we need."

## Common LinkedIn Headline Mistakes

### "Open to Opportunities"

This tells recruiters nothing about what you actually do. It's the equivalent of a billboard that says "Hire me for something." Replace it with your target role and skills.

### Using only your current job title

"Software Engineer at [Current Company]" is the default LinkedIn generates. It's the bare minimum. You're leaving 190 characters of searchable, persuasive space unused.

### Listing every skill you've ever had

"Marketing | Sales | Operations | Finance | Strategy | Leadership | Innovation | Transformation"

When you say you do everything, you're saying you specialize in nothing. Pick the 2-3 skills most relevant to your target role.

### Being cute instead of clear

"Growth Ninja | Marketing Wizard | Disruption Enthusiast"

Recruiters don't search for "ninja." They search for "Growth Marketing Manager." Be findable first, creative second.

## How to Write Your LinkedIn Headline in 10 Minutes

Here's what I want you to do right now:

1. **Pick your target role.** What job title do you want recruiters to find you for? Write it down.
2. **List your top 3 skills or tools.** The ones most relevant to that target role. What would a recruiter search for?
3. **Write your "so what."** Who do you help? What results do you deliver? What industry or company type do you serve?
4. **Combine them.** [Target Role] | [Skill 1, Skill 2, Skill 3] | [Who you help / What you deliver]
5. **Check the character count.** You get 220 characters. Use as many as you can.

Takes 10 minutes. The impact lasts for your entire search.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Should I include my current company name in my LinkedIn headline?

Only if the company name adds credibility or context. If you work at Google, Amazon, or a well-known brand in your industry, including it can help. Otherwise, use that space for keywords and your value proposition. Recruiters care more about what you can do than where you currently sit. Your company name already appears right below your headline anyway.

### How often should I update my LinkedIn headline?

Update it whenever your target role changes or when you're starting a new job search. If you're actively searching, optimize your headline for the specific role you want. Once you land a new position, update it again to reflect your new role and the value you're delivering. A quarterly review is a good habit even when you're not actively looking because it keeps your profile fresh and searchable.

### Can I have different headlines for different job targets?

LinkedIn only allows one headline at a time. If you're targeting two closely related roles (like "Marketing Manager" and "Brand Manager"), combine the key elements. Something like: "Marketing & Brand Manager | Content Strategy, Paid Media | Driving growth for consumer tech." If your targets are very different, pick the one you're most focused on and optimize for that. You can always update it as your priorities shift.
