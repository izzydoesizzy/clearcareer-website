Most job seekers treat LinkedIn like a job board. They scroll, they apply, they close the tab. They never post. They never comment. They never engage with anyone's content.

And then they wonder why nobody knows they exist.

LinkedIn content is not about becoming an influencer or building a personal brand empire. It's about visibility. When a hiring manager sees your application, what happens next? They Google you. They check your LinkedIn. And if your profile has zero activity, zero posts, zero engagement, you look like a ghost.

Compare that to someone who's been commenting thoughtful things on industry posts, sharing their perspective on trends, and posting about their work. Who would you rather interview?

Content is a cheat code for the hidden job market. Jenna, my wife, posted about what she does, what she loves about her work, and what she's looking for. People she'd volunteered with and worked with saw it. They told others. A company created a role specifically for her. She was the only person they interviewed.

You don't need 10,000 followers. You need the right 50 people to know your name.

Let's build a content strategy that makes that happen.

## Before You Post: Make Sure Your Profile Is Ready

Content drives traffic to your profile. If your profile is a mess, that traffic bounces.

Before you start posting or commenting, make sure your [LinkedIn profile is optimized](/blog/linkedin-profile-optimization-the-full-audit). Your headline should use the 3-part formula (Target Role + Skills + Value). Your summary should have all 6 elements. Your keywords should match the roles you're targeting.

Content is the engine. Your profile is the landing page. Both need to work.

## The Commenting Strategy: Get Known Before You Post

Posting gets all the attention, but commenting is the real workhorse. It's faster, easier, lower-risk, and surprisingly effective.

Here's why: when you comment on someone's post, every person who reads that post sees your name, your headline, and your photo. If your comment is good, people click on your profile. If you do this consistently with the right people, they start recognizing your name before you ever send a DM.

### The Four-Level Commenting Matrix

Not all comments are created equal. Here's how I break it down:

**Level 1: Like.** The bare minimum. Forgettable. Better than nothing, but barely.

**Level 2: Generic comment.** "Great post!" or "Thanks for sharing." Slightly better. Still forgettable. Nobody remembers who wrote "Great post!"

**Level 3: Add your own perspective.** This is where it gets interesting. "I saw this same thing when I was at [company]. What worked for us was..." Now you're contributing to the conversation. People remember this. You're demonstrating expertise without being asked.

**Level 4: Connect people and share resources.** The gold standard. "This reminds me of what @person was saying about X. Have you two connected?" or "Here's a resource that goes deeper on this point." Now you're a connector. You're adding value to multiple people at once.

The strategy: operate at levels 3 and 4. Every single time.

### Building Your Commenting Routine

Here's the system I teach my clients:

**Step 1: Identify 10-15 target accounts.** These should be people at your target companies, in your industry, or in roles you want. Think hiring managers, team leads, industry voices, and recruiters who post regularly.

**Step 2: Set up a tracking spreadsheet.** Create a simple spreadsheet with their profile URLs. Add a column that generates their "Recent Activity" URL (just add "/recent-activity/all/" to the end of their profile URL). In the morning, right-click and open all tabs at once. Now you've got everyone's latest posts right in front of you.

**Step 3: Spend 15-20 minutes each morning commenting.** Not liking. Not writing "great post." Adding your perspective. Sharing a related experience. Asking a thoughtful question. Connecting their idea to something else you've seen.

**Step 4: Do this consistently for 2-3 weeks.** By the time you reach out with a DM or connection request, they already know your name. Raj, one of my clients, started commenting on the CEOs of his target companies' posts. His plan: three months of consistent engagement so these CEOs notice him. Once the familiarity is built, his direct messages land differently because they're not coming from a stranger.

This works because most LinkedIn posts get very few comments. If a company's posts are getting zero engagement and you drop a thoughtful comment, you immediately stand out. One of my clients noticed a job posting from Contact Monkey that had only four comments. She wrote one insightful comment that showed she understood what the role required. That alone got the hiring manager's attention before the application even landed.

### What to Comment (With Examples)

**Instead of:** "Great insights!"
**Write:** "This matches what I saw at [Company] when we rolled out a similar program. The biggest challenge was getting buy-in from middle management. Did you face that too?"

**Instead of:** "Thanks for sharing."
**Write:** "Point 3 about [specific thing] is underrated. I've been experimenting with that approach for the last 6 months, and the results have been [specific outcome]."

**Instead of:** "Love this!"
**Write:** "This connects to something @[Name] posted last week about [topic]. Between the two posts, I'm starting to see a pattern in how [industry] is shifting toward [trend]."

Notice the pattern: specificity, personal experience, and questions that continue the conversation.

## The Posting Strategy: 2-3 Posts Per Week

Once your commenting routine is running, start posting. Not every day. Two to three times per week is plenty.

### What to Post About

You don't need to be a thought leader. You need to share what you know, what you've done, and what you're learning. Here are five post types that work for job seekers:

**1. Lessons from your work.** What did you learn in your last role that others could benefit from? "In 3 years managing enterprise accounts, I learned that the #1 predictor of renewal wasn't product satisfaction. It was how quickly we responded to their first support ticket."

**2. Industry observations.** What trends are you seeing? What's changing? "I've noticed that 8 out of 10 job postings for [role] now require [skill]. Two years ago, that number was 3 out of 10. Here's what that tells me about where this field is heading."

**3. Tools and resources you find useful.** Share something that helped you and explain why. "I've been using [tool] to [specific task], and it's cut my [process] time by 40%. Here's how I set it up."

**4. Behind-the-scenes of your job search.** Be real about the process. "Week 6 of my job search. Here's what I've learned so far..." People relate to vulnerability when it comes with insight.

**5. Your open-to-work announcement.** This is the big one. More on this below.

### Formatting Rules for LinkedIn Posts

LinkedIn's algorithm has specific preferences. Follow these:

**Never include links in the body of your post.** LinkedIn wants to keep users on the platform. If your post has a link, the algorithm deprioritizes it and shows it to fewer people. Always say "link in the comments" and drop the link there.

**Short lines. White space. Hook in the first line.** No walls of text. Break up your writing. One idea per line. Leave space between paragraphs. The first line is everything because LinkedIn truncates posts after about 3 lines. If your first line doesn't make someone click "see more," the rest doesn't matter.

**Photos are optional.** Some posts work better with photos, some without. If you use a photo, make it personal. A picture of you, not a stock image. People connect with you, not your credentials.

**Post Tuesday through Thursday.** LinkedIn engagement peaks mid-week. Monday posts get buried. Friday posts get forgotten.

## The Open-to-Work Post: Your Highest-Leverage Content

If you post one thing during your entire job search, make it this.

An open-to-work announcement post activates your entire network at once. People you haven't spoken to in years will see it, share it, and think of you when they hear about relevant roles.

### The 5 Elements of a Perfect Open-to-Work Post

1. **Clarity.** State exactly what roles you're seeking. Right at the top. No ambiguity. Anyone reading should know immediately what you're looking for.

2. **Personal brand.** Highlight your strongest skills and what sets you apart. Don't be generic. Be specific about your expertise and your track record.

3. **Results.** Quantify your past achievements. Numbers stand out in a feed full of text. "Drove $4.2M in new revenue" is 10x more powerful than "experienced in sales."

4. **A story.** Share something real about your journey, a challenge you overcame, a project you're proud of, a lesson you learned. Stories make you human and memorable.

5. **A call to action.** Tell people exactly what you want them to do. "If you know of [type of role] opportunities, I'd love to hear about them. DM me or tag me." Simple and direct.

### Real Results From Open-to-Work Posts

The tangible outcomes: a coffee chat with a contact in his industry, a recruiter scheduling a call, multiple people reposting and saying they'd keep him in mind.

He said: "People are now aware in my LinkedIn community that I am looking for a job and this is what I want. So when the opportunity comes, they will keep me in mind."

Maya posted her open-to-work announcement during a live group call using the framework above. Within five minutes, she had a recruiter message, a new connection request, and 11 likes. Five minutes.

Tyler David posted and landed multiple interview opportunities directly from the post. She started a new job shortly after.

You've got to put yourself out there. If people don't know you're looking, they assume you're happy and settled. Tell them what you want, what you're looking for, how you can help, what successes you've had. Then watch your network go to work for you.

## Using Content to Start Conversations With Target Companies

Content isn't just about broadcasting. It's a conversation starter that leads to real opportunities.

Here's the full sequence:

**Week 1-2:** Comment on posts from people at your target companies. Level 3 and 4 comments. Add your perspective. Ask smart questions. Get your name in front of them.

**Week 2-3:** Post your own content. Share insights from your industry. Reference their company's work where it's genuine. Show that you think about the problems they're solving.

**Week 3-4:** Send a connection request or DM. By now, they've seen your name. They've read your comments. They may have checked your profile. Your message isn't cold anymore. It's warm.

This is the difference between [pitch-slapping someone](/blog/stop-pitch-slapping-on-linkedin) and building an actual relationship. Content creates familiarity. Familiarity creates trust. Trust creates opportunities.

## Track, Measure, Iterate

Don't just post and hope. Track what works.

**Check weekly:**
- How many profile views are you getting? (Up or down from last week?)
- Which posts got the most engagement? What did they have in common?
- Are your search appearances increasing? Are the right job titles showing up?
- Are people reaching out to you in DMs?

**Adjust based on data:**
- If a certain topic gets 3x more engagement, write more about that topic
- If text posts outperform photo posts, lean into text
- If morning posts outperform afternoon posts, post in the morning
- If your profile views are up but you're not getting DMs, your profile might need work

Treat your content like a marketing experiment. The job seekers who get the most traction are the ones who pay attention to what the data is telling them.

## The Weekly Content Calendar

Here's a simple schedule to follow. Customize it based on your energy and availability:

**Monday:** Spend 15 minutes commenting on your 10-15 target accounts. No posting today. Just engagement.

**Tuesday:** Comment for 15 minutes. Publish one post (industry insight, lesson learned, or tool recommendation).

**Wednesday:** Comment for 15 minutes. Engage with anyone who commented on yesterday's post.

**Thursday:** Comment for 15 minutes. Publish one post (personal story, behind-the-scenes of your search, or a question for your network).

**Friday:** Comment for 15 minutes. Review your analytics from the week. What worked? What didn't?

Total time: about 20-25 minutes per day, plus 30-45 minutes for writing two posts per week. That's roughly 3 hours per week. Less time than most people spend scrolling without a strategy.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many LinkedIn followers do I need before posting is worth it?

Zero. You don't need followers to benefit from posting. Your posts are shown to your connections first, and most job seekers already have 200-500+ connections. That's more than enough for your open-to-work post to reach the right people. Maya had a modest network and got a recruiter message within 5 minutes of posting. Focus on the quality of your content and the specificity of your targeting, not follower count.

### What if I post and nobody engages?

First, check your formatting. Walls of text get scrolled past. Short lines and a strong opening hook make a big difference. Second, make sure you're engaging with others first. The algorithm rewards people who are active on the platform, not just people who broadcast. Third, give it time. Most people quit after 2-3 posts with low engagement. That's too early. Commit to 2-3 posts per week for 4 weeks before you evaluate. Raj's most popular post came after weeks of consistent activity.

### I'm worried about my current employer seeing that I'm looking for a job. Should I still post?

If you're employed and searching discreetly, you can still benefit from a content strategy without announcing your search. Post industry insights, lessons learned, and professional observations. These build your visibility and personal brand without signaling that you're job hunting. Use LinkedIn's recruiter-only "Open to Work" signal (not the green banner). Focus heavily on the commenting strategy, which builds relationships without drawing attention. When you're ready to make the leap, you'll already have a warm network that knows your name.
