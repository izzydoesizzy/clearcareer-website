Most people run their job search like a hobby. A little browsing here, a half-hearted application there, maybe a LinkedIn scroll before bed.

Then they wonder why nothing is moving.

I've coached hundreds of job seekers, and the pattern is always the same. The ones who land offers fastest treat their search like a paid project. They set weekly targets. They track their pipeline. They batch their tasks. They show up consistently, not heroically.

Marcus came to me after applying to 600 jobs with zero traction. We flipped his approach from reactive to targeted. He applied to four or five jobs total, and landed an offer with a $10K salary bump. His words: "I slowed my role as far as quantity and started working on quality and being proactive with reaching out to companies and hiring managers. That's when things started turning around rapidly."

This playbook gives you the exact system.

## Step 1: Stop Being Reactive (And Start Being Proactive)

Here's the default job search:

1. Open LinkedIn or Indeed
2. Scroll through postings for an hour
3. Find something that sort of fits
4. Spend two hours tailoring your resume
5. Hit submit
6. Wait three weeks
7. Get ghosted
8. Repeat

That's a reactive search. You're waiting for employers to post something, then competing against 700 other applicants for the same role. Out of the 654 people who apply for a typical position, only 14 get interviews. Only 1 gets hired.

A proactive search flips the script entirely.

Instead of asking "what's posted?", you ask: "Where do I actually want to work?" Then you go find the people at those companies, build relationships, and create opportunities before a job is ever listed.

As I tell my clients: "This is you designing your career and your life rather than just kind of seeing where you fit in the job market. Proactive and not reactive."

### How to build your target company list

1. **Brainstorm 20 companies you genuinely care about.** Not companies with open roles. Companies whose products you use, whose missions excite you, whose teams you'd love to join. When you have genuine interest, enthusiasm and networking come naturally.

2. **Expand with AI.** Open your AI assistant (ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity, or similar) and type: "I'm looking for a new job in [your field]. Make a list of [industry] companies in [your city/region]." Put the results into a three-column table: company name, description, URL.

3. **Use LinkedIn's "People Also Viewed."** Follow your target companies on LinkedIn. The sidebar shows similar companies. Add the good ones to your list.

4. **Search for events.** Ask AI: "What are the top upcoming events in [your city] related to [your field]?" Volunteer at the ones that matter. Volunteering is like VIP access. You meet speakers, organizers, and people who are hyperconnected in your industry.

Your target list should hit 30-40 companies. This is your universe. Everything else in your search flows from here.

For a deeper dive on how to research and approach target companies, read the [target company list playbook](/blog/build-your-target-company-list-with-ai).

## Step 2: Set Weekly Targets (That You'll Actually Hit)

Raj asked me during a coaching call:

<Client>I've given myself a metric of 10 outreach emails every day. Is that adequate, or should that be bumped up to 20?</Client>

<Coach>I'd rather you have a consistent, achievable, smaller goal than a big, unattainable goal that you're going to break. Even if it's three a day, not ten a day. Is ten a day achievable for you? If not, do three a day. That's okay.</Coach>

This is straight from Atomic Habits. Consistency beats ambition every time. One small action every day compounds into massive results. I learned conversational Mandarin by listening to a podcast for 20 minutes a day. The same principle applies to your job search.

### Your minimum viable weekly cadence

- **5-10 outreach messages** (LinkedIn DMs, emails to hiring managers, informational interview requests)
- **2-3 tailored applications** (not spray-and-pray, but targeted applications to companies on your list)
- **1-2 informational interviews** (20-minute conversations with people doing the work you want to do)
- **3-5 LinkedIn comments at Level 3 or 4** (add your perspective, share resources, connect people)

That's it. You can do this in 2-3 focused hours per day. Not eight.

### The golden rule: actionable over passive, consistent over ambitious

Spend most of your time doing something that moves the needle: outreach, applications, connecting with someone, attending an event. Something where you're creating contact with another human.

Tweaking your resume for the 20th time? That's passive. Researching company websites for four hours? Passive. Sending five emails to hiring managers? Actionable.

Raj put it perfectly: "Spend the majority of your time on anything that adds opportunities to your funnel."

## Step 3: Build Your Pipeline Tracker

You need to see your entire job search at a glance. No more "I think I applied there... maybe?"

### The simple spreadsheet method

Create a Google Sheet with these columns:

| Company | Role | Contact | Status | Next Action | Date | Notes |
|---------|------|---------|--------|-------------|------|-------|
| Acme Corp | Product Manager | Sarah J. (HM) | Applied | Follow up email | Apr 2 | Met at conference |
| TechCo | PM Lead | Mike R. | Networking | Info interview | Mar 30 | Referred by Nicole |
| HealthFirst | Senior PM | -- | Researching | Find HM on LinkedIn | Mar 28 | Great mission fit |

The "Status" column tracks where each opportunity sits in your funnel:

1. **Researching** - On your target list, doing homework
2. **Networking** - Connecting with people at the company
3. **Applied** - Submitted an application
4. **Interviewing** - In active interview rounds
5. **Offer** - Negotiating or evaluating

### The CRM method (for power users)

Streak CRM plugs directly into Gmail and lets you track opens, set up mail merge with personal blurbs for each contact, and manage your pipeline visually. You can see which emails got opened 30 times (they forwarded it to their team) and which ones got zero opens (time to try a different contact).

Spending 20-30 minutes making a list of contacts and a personal blurb for each, then using mail merge, saves hours of manual emailing.

### The critical rule: always keep the funnel open

Tess shared during a coaching call that she'd stopped networking once she had active interviews going. I told her: "It's a lot harder if interviews don't pan out and you have to go all the way back to the beginning, versus always having something in the pipeline."

Even when things are going well, keep adding to the top of your funnel. A networking conversation here. A LinkedIn comment there. The smallest possible step forward.

## Step 4: Batch Your Tasks

Context-switching kills productivity. If you bounce between research, writing, networking, and applying all in the same morning, you'll feel busy but get almost nothing done.

Instead, batch similar tasks together.

### Sample weekly schedule

**Monday: Planning and Research Day**
- Review your pipeline tracker
- Update statuses and next actions
- Research 3-5 new companies from your target list
- Queue up people to connect with this week

**Tuesday: Outreach Day**
- Send your weekly batch of LinkedIn DMs and emails
- Follow up on last week's messages (5-7 days after initial contact)
- Comment on 3-5 posts from people at target companies

**Wednesday: Application Day**
- Tailor and submit 2-3 applications from your target list
- Use AI to customize cover letters and identify objections between job descriptions and your resume

**Thursday: Networking Day**
- Attend a virtual or in-person event, or schedule informational interviews
- Follow up from recent conversations with a thank-you or resource share

**Friday: Review and Prep**
- Review the week: what worked, what didn't, what to adjust
- Prep for any upcoming interviews
- Set next week's targets

This structure takes 3-4 hours per day. Not eight. If you try to fill eight hours a day with job searching, you'll burn out in a week. Split your time between deep work, outreach, and weekly planning.

## Step 5: Build Accountability That Sticks

Here's what I've learned from coaching: the people who succeed aren't the most talented or the most connected. They're the most consistent. And consistency comes from accountability.

Anil, one of our community members, described it perfectly: "My clock starts from Tuesday to Tuesday. I start every Tuesday with a good energy talking to all folks on the call. And by the end of the week I'm so burnt out with the rejections. And then I show up for the call again on Tuesday and I get that motivation to keep on working."

That weekly rhythm is everything.

### Three levels of accountability

**Level 1: The Accountability Buddy**
Find one person who's also job searching. Set up a weekly 15-minute check-in. Share your goals for the week, report on last week's progress. Simple and effective.

**Level 2: The Job Search Group**
Reach out to 2-3 friends or former colleagues. Create a group chat or Slack channel. Establish a regular check-in day. Set weekly goals and report progress. When someone has a win, the whole group wins.

**Level 3: Body Doubling (Working Alongside Others)**
Andrew in our group struggled with motivation until he discovered body doubling: working alongside someone, even silently, creates enough accountability to stay focused.

Tools that make this easy:

- **FocusMate**: You get assigned a partner to work alongside on camera for 25-50 minutes. No talking besides saying hi and what you're working on. If you don't show up, you lose points. Free plan gives you 5 sessions per week.
- **Flown**: A global co-working community. You work in small breakout rooms for 50 minutes, tell the group what you're doing, and at the end say how it went. Strangers from all over the world encourage you. Nicole from our community used it and said she knocked out all her work in a two-hour deep dive.
- **Coffee shop forcing function**: Andrew got a coffee subscription that forced him out of the house every day. When he worked from home, Netflix won. When he worked at the shop, he got things done.

The pattern: external structure beats willpower. Every time.

## Step 6: Adopt the Snowplow Mindset

I was shoveling my driveway one morning and it hit me. There are four ways to clear snow:

1. **The big clunky shovel** (doing it yourself with whatever tools you've got)
2. **Four neighbors helping** (community, accountability partners)
3. **A snow plow** (hiring a coach)
4. **Sitting inside for five hours deciding which shovel to buy** (paralysis)

Even with the worst shovel, you can clear the driveway in 45 minutes. The person who sits inside researching the "best" approach for five hours still has a snow-covered driveway.

Stop overthinking. Start shoveling.

The people who do this consistently for four to six weeks almost always tell me that opportunities start coming to them instead of them having to chase everything.

## The Mindset Shift Behind All of This

There's a deeper shift happening when you run your search like a project. You stop seeing yourself as someone begging for a job. You start seeing yourself as someone building something.

You're not a needle in a haystack. You're a professional with a clear target, a system, and the discipline to work it consistently.

When you have genuine interest in the companies you're pursuing, that energy comes through in every email, every conversation, every interview. You can't fake authenticity. And when employers feel your passion, everything changes.

We call it job hunting. But the most successful job seekers will tell you it's much more like farming. Plant seeds. Water the seeds. Grow your network. Harvest the fruit.

The harvest doesn't come from one frantic afternoon of mass-applying. It comes from weeks of consistent, targeted effort.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many hours per day should I spend job searching?

Three to four focused hours beats eight unfocused hours every time. Split your time between deep work (research, applications) and active outreach (emails, networking, events). The key word is "actionable." If most of your time goes toward connecting with real people and adding opportunities to your pipeline, you're doing it right. If most of your time goes toward browsing job boards and polishing your resume, you're spinning your wheels. And take weekends off. Burnout is real.

### Is it worth using a CRM tool, or is a spreadsheet enough?

A spreadsheet works great for most people, especially at the start. If you're tracking 30-40 companies, Google Sheets handles it fine. A CRM like Streak becomes useful when you're running higher-volume outreach and want to track email opens, automate follow-ups, or use mail merge. Start with what's simple. Upgrade if you need to. The tool matters less than actually using it consistently.

### What if I don't have anyone to be accountable to?

You have more options than you think. FocusMate pairs you with a stranger for focused work sessions for free. Flown gives you a global co-working community with built-in encouragement. Even posting your weekly goals in a LinkedIn post creates public accountability. The point isn't finding the perfect partner. It's creating any external structure that makes it harder to skip your daily work. Start with one body doubling session this week and see how it feels.
