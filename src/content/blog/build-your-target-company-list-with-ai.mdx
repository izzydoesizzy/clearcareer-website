Most job seekers do their search backwards. They open LinkedIn, scroll job boards for hours, occasionally find something that sort of fits, and apply alongside 700 other people. Then they wonder why nothing's working.

I've worked with people who applied to 600 jobs over 12 months and got zero traction. Then we flip the approach, and they land interviews within weeks.

The difference? They stopped waiting for the right job to appear. They started targeting the right companies first.

Here's the full system I walk my coaching clients through. By the end, you'll have a list of 50+ companies you're genuinely excited about, organized in a Google Sheet, with the decision-makers already identified on LinkedIn.

## Why "Target Companies" Beats "Target Jobs"

Here's the philosophy behind everything you're about to do.

If you target a job posting, you're competing with hundreds (sometimes thousands) of applicants. Everyone sees the same listing. Everyone sends the same type of application.

But if you target the company rather than a specific job at the company, something shifts. You find people on the teams you're interested in. You start connecting with those people. And then you find opportunities that might not exist yet.

People who build relationships before a job is posted show employers something powerful: they don't just need a paycheck. They actually want to work here. They're reaching out because they're interested in the mission, the team, the work.

That's the approach we're building toward. Apply to posted jobs, yes. But also get your foot in the door before anything gets posted. The people who do both consistently are the ones who land offers faster.

## Step 2: Use AI to Expand Your List to 50+

Now we bring in AI. Here's the exact workflow I demo in coaching sessions. These prompts work in ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or any AI assistant.

### The Company Prospecting Prompt

Open your AI assistant and paste something like this:

<PromptBlock id="company-prospecting" label="Company Prospecting Prompt">
I'm looking for a new role as a [your target title] in [your industry or interest area]. Create a prospective company list of organizations I should target. Include: company name, URL, brief description, and location. Format as a table.
</PromptBlock>

That's it. The AI will generate a list of 15-20 companies.

Now expand it:

<PromptBlock id="expand-list" label="Expand List">
Expand this list by 20 more.
</PromptBlock>

And again:

<PromptBlock id="exclude-subset" label="Exclude and Refine">
Now show me others you haven't mentioned that are not [subset you want to exclude, e.g., "health focused"].
</PromptBlock>

And then get specific:

<PromptBlock id="city-specific" label="City-Specific Search">
Show me companies like these in [your city].
</PromptBlock>

<PromptBlock id="nested-orgs" label="Nested Organizations">
Show me companies like these that are housed within larger organizations, like venture studios within IBM or innovation labs within banks.
</PromptBlock>

I did this live with Nax during a coaching call. We started with "venture studios in the U.S." and within 10 minutes had a spreadsheet with over 50 companies, organized and ready to research.

### The Iterative Refinement Method

This is where it gets powerful. I walked Priya through this process step by step:

1. I asked Priya about her interests: marketing, communications, community building, product demand generation
2. We defined her target roles: community manager, product marketing manager
3. I told the AI to expand the list to 30 more, output as a four-column table: name, description, location, website
4. Priya checked off the ones that excited her most
5. We fed those back in: "Here are my top 10 picks. Expand this list with more companies similar to these."

Every round gets more targeted. You're teaching the AI what you actually want by showing it what resonates.

The goal is to shift from scarcity to abundance. From "I'm not finding the right jobs" to "I have 50 different companies I can start reaching out to." That mindset shift alone changes how you show up in your search.

## Step 3: Export to Google Sheets with a Tracking System

Once your list hits 40-50 companies, it's time to organize.

Here's the setup I use with every client:

1. Open a new Google Sheet (type `sheets.new` in your browser)
2. Create these columns: **Checkbox** | **Company Name** | **URL** | **Description** | **Location** | **Status** | **Notes**
3. Add a checkbox column on the far left and label it "Interested"
4. Paste your ChatGPT table into the sheet

Now go through the list. Check the boxes for the ones that genuinely excite you. The ones where you think, "Oh yeah, I'd love to work there."

Uncheck anything that feels like a stretch or just doesn't spark anything.

Take your checked companies, copy them back into your AI assistant, and say: "Here are the ones I'm most interested in. Find patterns and give me 20 more companies like these."

Run this cycle two or three times. Your list will get sharper every round.

### The Status Column

Track where you are with each company:

- **Researching**: You're learning about them
- **People Found**: You've identified contacts on LinkedIn
- **Reached Out**: You've sent a message or email
- **Conversation**: You've had a reply or call
- **Applied**: You've submitted an application

This turns your target list from a static document into a living pipeline.

## Step 4: The LinkedIn People Finder Method

You have your list. Now you need to find the right people at each company. Not HR. Not recruiters. The people who actually do the work you want to do, or manage the team you want to join.

Here's the 4-step method I teach:

### Step 1: Search for the Company on LinkedIn

Type the company name in the LinkedIn search bar. Make sure you select the actual company page, not a community group or showcase page.

### Step 2: Click "See All Employees on LinkedIn"

This gives you the full list of people who work there.

### Step 3: Use Filters to Find the Right People

Click "All Filters" and narrow down by:

- **Title**: Search for the job title you want, or the title of the person who would manage that role. If you want a marketing coordinator position, search for "Marketing Manager" or "Director of Marketing."
- **Keywords**: Search by department: "Engineering," "Marketing," "Human Resources." This helps you find people on the team you'd join.
- **Connections**: Filter by 2nd connections to find warm introduction paths. If someone you know is connected to someone at the company, that's your way in.

### Step 4: Identify the Right Person

You're looking for someone with decision-making power. That usually means someone 1-2 levels above the role you're targeting.

For small companies (under 10 people), you can reach out directly to the CEO. For larger organizations, aim for team leads, directors, or senior managers in your target department.

Save this person's name to your spreadsheet. Add their LinkedIn URL to a new column.

## Step 5: Boolean Search Techniques for Smarter Discovery

Most people don't know you can run advanced searches on LinkedIn and Indeed using Boolean operators. These are simple keywords that let you combine, exclude, and refine your search results.

The three operators you need:

- **AND**: Both terms must appear. "Program Manager AND energy" returns results that include both keywords.
- **OR**: Either term can appear. "Product Manager OR Program Manager" catches both titles.
- **NOT**: Exclude a term. "Marketing Manager NOT Amazon" removes Amazon listings from your results.

I taught this to Raj when he was searching for program manager roles in oil and gas. We set up: `program manager AND energy AND "oil and gas"`.

For my wife's search, she was looking at nonprofit roles in reproductive health, but nurse practitioner jobs kept flooding the results. We added `NOT nurse` and the results became immediately relevant.

You can also use Boolean search on LinkedIn posts (not just job listings) to find people actively talking about hiring. Comment on those posts when engagement is low, and you'll get directly in front of hiring managers instead of competing with hundreds of applicants.

Set up saved searches with alerts on both LinkedIn and Indeed. Any time something matches, you'll get a notification. It's like having a scout working for you around the clock.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many companies should be on my target list?

Start with 50. That sounds like a lot, but with AI, you can get there in under an hour. The point isn't to apply to all 50. It's to shift your mindset from scarcity to abundance. When you have 50 options, you stop feeling desperate about any single one. You also start noticing patterns in what excites you, which sharpens your whole search. From those 50, you'll probably narrow down to 15-20 that you actively pursue through networking and outreach.

### Should I only target companies that have open roles?

No. That's the whole point of this approach. If you only target companies with open postings, you're back to competing with everyone else. Target companies you're excited about regardless of whether they're hiring right now. Build relationships with people there. When a role opens (or before it even gets posted), you'll already be a known quantity. I've seen clients land roles that were created for them because they'd already proven their interest and value through networking.

### What if I don't know what industry I want to work in?

Start with your interests, not your experience. That's what I did with Allison. She thought she should be targeting consulting firms because that's where she'd worked. But when we explored wellness companies like MyFitnessPal and Peloton, she lit up. The energy difference was obvious to everyone on the call. Use the brainstorming questions from Step 1. Build lists across two or three different directions. See which one generates the most genuine excitement. Your energy will tell you where to go.
