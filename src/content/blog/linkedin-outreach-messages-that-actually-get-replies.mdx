Your LinkedIn outreach messages aren't getting replies. I know because I've seen the messages. They're too long, too generic, and they ask for too much too fast.

I sat with Raj during a coaching session and asked him to show me his actual outreach messages. Here's what he was sending after someone accepted his connection:

> "Hey, thanks so much for accepting my LinkedIn connection, really appreciate. I was wondering, would you be open to a 20 to 30 minute virtual chat?"

And here was his connection request note:

> "I'm eager to expand my professional network and connect with like-minded individuals. I believe our mutual interests and experiences could lead to valuable insights and opportunities."

He was getting some accepts. But the conversion to actual conversations was low. Because those messages could have been written by anyone, sent to anyone, about anything. There's nothing about the other person in them. Nothing specific. Nothing that signals "I actually looked at your profile and chose you for a reason."

I pulled up my own LinkedIn inbox during that call and walked him through the connection requests I reject. Every single one started with "I." They talked about themselves. None of them mentioned anything about me. No reason for reaching out. No specificity.

That's the problem. And it's fixable.

## The Golden Rule: Give Before You Ask

Before we get to templates, let's talk about the principle that makes all of them work.

Every effective outreach message follows the same pattern: give first, ask second. Lead with something about them, not about you. Reference something specific they did, wrote, or accomplished. Show that you did the work of actually learning about this person.

This is the opposite of [pitch slapping](/blog/stop-pitch-slapping-on-linkedin), which is when someone you've never met slaps you with a request before they've given you any reason to care.

Here's a phrase to delete from your vocabulary: "I'm impressed by your experience." I get this constantly. Every time I see it, I know it's a template. It's been used so many times by cold spammers and bots that it's lost all meaning.

Anil, one of my clients, made one tiny change that transformed his response rate. He swapped "impressed" for "inspired." Instead of "I'm impressed by your profile," he wrote, "I was inspired by your transition from [specific company] to [specific company]."

The results were immediate. An RBC recruiter who gets 50 connection requests per day, and never responds to any of them, accepted Anil's request and told him directly: "I accepted because your message showed you actually looked at my profile and had genuine interest in my path."

One word. Massive difference.

## The CHARM Framework: Your Outreach Blueprint

I created the CHARM framework to give every outreach message a structure that feels authentic and lands consistently. Five elements:

**C - Celebrate.** Mention something positive, inspiring, or interesting you learned about them, their team, or their company. This is your opening. It shows you did your homework.

**H - Highlight.** Highlight a specific role you're interested in, or express interest in their specific team. Don't be vague. "Your product team" is better than "your company." "The senior PM role on the marketplace team" is better still.

**A - Advocate.** Share a super concise summary of your experience and achievements. One sentence. One metric. That's it. You're not pitching. You're establishing credibility in 15 words.

**R - Reveal.** Share a unique insight using your industry expertise or research you've done on their company. This is permissionless value: you're giving them something useful without being asked. A competitor analysis. A trend you've noticed. A connection to something they're working on.

**M - Meet.** Offer a short, casual call to discuss industry trends and your insights. Not "pick your brain." Not "get advice." A conversation between two professionals.

Don't force it. Find people who genuinely inspire you and who you'd actually want to meet. That's where the real connections happen.

You don't need to use all five elements in every message. But the framework ensures you're always leading with them, not with you.

## Template 1: Cold Outreach to Hiring Managers

This is the one everyone needs. You've found a job posting. You know who the hiring manager is. You want to stand out from the 300 other applicants.

**The Template:**

> Hi [Name],
>
> I saw [Company] is hiring for the [Role Title] position and I'm genuinely excited about it. [One specific sentence about why, referencing something real about the company or team].
>
> I've spent [X years] doing [relevant experience], and most recently [one specific accomplishment with a number]. I'd love to learn more about what your team is focused on right now.
>
> Would you be open to a quick 15-minute conversation this week or next?

**Why it works:** Short. Leads with genuine interest in their company, not in yourself. Includes one concrete result so they know you're credible. Asks for 15 minutes, not a job.

**How to customize it:** The second sentence is everything. Don't write "I love your company's mission." Write "I saw your team just launched [specific product], and the approach to [specific detail] is exactly the kind of work I want to be doing." Show you did the homework. Use [our research tools](/tools) to find those specifics fast.

**CHARM breakdown:** Celebrate (excited about their specific work) + Highlight (specific role) + Advocate (one accomplishment with a number) + Meet (15-minute call).

## Template 2: Informational Interview Requests

Informational interviews are the single most underused tool in job searching. One 20-minute conversation can tell you more about a company than 5 hours of Googling.

But the way you ask determines whether you get one.

**The Template:**

> Hi [Name],
>
> I've been researching [Industry/Company] and your background caught my attention, specifically your work on [specific project, post, or career move].
>
> I'm exploring roles in [area] and would love to hear about your experience at [Company]. Not looking for any favors, just trying to learn from people doing the work I want to do.
>
> Would you have 20 minutes for a quick call in the next couple of weeks?

**Why it works:** You're leading with a specific compliment. You're being upfront that this isn't a sneaky job ask. And "20 minutes in the next couple of weeks" is easy to say yes to.

**How to customize it:** Replace the bracket in the second sentence with something *specific*. Their LinkedIn post about leadership. Their career change from finance to tech. Their promotion trajectory. The more specific, the higher your response rate.

**Critical framing:** Ask for advice, not a job. Raj asked me about this: how do you signal you're looking without creating pressure? The answer is to ask for low-pressure advice: "I'd love your advice on companies you'd look at outside of [their company], or types of positions you'd target if you were on the market today. Where do you see movement happening?"

You're asking them to share their perspective. That's flattering and easy. Compare that to "Can you get me a job?" which creates pressure, unease, and often silence.

People who feel valued give more. People who feel pressured pull away.

## Template 3: Micro-Asks (The Coffee Chat Alternative)

Here's something most people don't consider: sometimes a 20-minute call is too big an ask. People want to help. They don't always have the time or energy for a call with someone they barely know.

The micro-ask solves this.

**The Template:**

> Hi [Name],
>
> Quick question: I'm researching [Industry/Area] and I'm curious about your experience at [Company]. What are some of the competitors you considered before you started working there?
>
> Just looking for a quick answer, nothing more. Really appreciate any insight.

**Why it works:** It can be answered in one line. No scheduling required. No Zoom link. No 20-minute commitment. The person reads it, types two sentences, and moves on. The barrier is so low that most people will respond.

**The follow-up that builds the relationship:** Once they respond, you use the information. Then you follow up: "Hey, I looked into [Company they mentioned] and you were right. Their approach to [specific thing] is really interesting. Thanks for pointing me there."

Now you've closed the loop. You've shown that their input mattered. And suddenly you've got an ongoing conversation going with someone at a target company, without ever asking for a 20-minute coffee chat.

Ask specific questions that are not vague and easy to answer in one or two lines. Make it specific enough to them and their personal experience that they feel valued.

## Template 4: Reaching Out to Former Employees for Company Intel

This is the secret weapon. People who used to work at your target company will tell you things that current employees can't. They'll be honest about culture, management, and what the company is actually like behind the careers page.

**The Template:**

> Hi [Name],
>
> I noticed you spent [X years] at [Company] and I'm currently interviewing there for a [Role] position. I've been doing my research and would love to get a more candid perspective from someone who knows the company well.
>
> Would you be open to a brief chat? I'd really appreciate hearing about your experience, especially around [team culture / management style / growth opportunities / one specific thing].
>
> Happy to work around your schedule.

**Why it works:** Former employees are more likely to be candid. By saying "more candid perspective," you're signaling that you want real talk, not PR. And specifying what you want to know makes it easy for them to say yes because they can picture the conversation.

**How to find them:** On LinkedIn, search the company name, click "People," then filter by "Past Company." Look for people who left 2-4 years ago. They're close enough to have relevant intel but far enough removed to speak freely. For a deeper dive on research techniques, check out the [company research playbook](/blog/company-research-before-interviews-the-complete-playbook).

## Template 5: Post-Event Follow-Ups

You went to a networking event, a webinar, or a conference. You exchanged LinkedIn connections. Now what? Most people do nothing. The follow-up is where the relationship actually starts.

**The Template:**

> Hi [Name],
>
> Great meeting you at [Event] yesterday. I really enjoyed our conversation about [specific topic you discussed].
>
> [One sentence connecting their interest to something useful: an article, a person, a resource]. Thought you'd find it interesting.
>
> Would love to stay connected and continue the conversation.

**Why it works:** You're following up within 24 hours while they still remember you. You're referencing a specific detail from your conversation, which proves you were actually listening. And you're giving them something, not asking for something.

**How to customize it:** The "something useful" is the key. An article related to what you discussed. A podcast episode. A person they should know. A tool you mentioned. Lead with value and the relationship builds itself.

## The Permissionless Value Principle

One of the most powerful concepts in outreach is what I call permissionless value. Most people ask "How can I add value to you?" after an informational interview. That's well-intentioned. But you're asking them to do the work of figuring out how you can help them.

Don't ask how you can help. Just help.

During a conversation, pay attention. What are they working on? What challenges did they mention? What excited them? Then after the call, send them something relevant without being asked.

Sharing news about their industry. Providing feedback on something they published. Celebrating their achievements publicly. Making an introduction to someone who could help them. If they mentioned they're looking for a product manager to speak at an event, and you know someone, make that introduction. That's a huge value add.

Here are 5 ways to deliver permissionless value after any conversation:

1. **Share a relevant article or resource** they might not have seen
2. **Introduce them to someone** in your network who could help with something they mentioned
3. **Comment on and share their LinkedIn posts** to boost their visibility
4. **Send a thoughtful follow-up** referencing something specific they said, with your own insight added
5. **Flag a job posting or opportunity** that might interest someone they know (not just themselves)

The people who are best at networking aren't the most charming or the most connected. They're the most consistent at adding value without being asked.

## The Rules That Apply to Every Template

Across all five templates, these rules are non-negotiable:

1. **Keep it under 100 words.** Long messages don't get read. They get archived.
2. **One ask per message.** Don't ask for a call AND ask them to review your resume AND ask if they know of any openings. Pick one.
3. **Make it about them first.** Lead with something specific about their work, their company, or their background. Then bridge to your ask.
4. **Start with "you," not "I."** The first word of your message should reference them. Not you. Not your job search. Them.
5. **Follow up once.** If you don't hear back in 5-7 days, send one short follow-up. After that, move on. No one owes you a reply.
6. **Send Tuesday through Thursday.** Messages sent Monday morning get buried. Messages sent Friday afternoon get forgotten.
7. **Never say "I'm impressed."** Say "I was inspired." Be specific about what inspired you and why.

## What to Do When You Get a Reply

They responded. Now don't blow it.

Confirm the meeting within 24 hours. Send a calendar invite. Prepare 3-4 specific questions (not "tell me about your role," but "I noticed your team launched X last quarter. What was the biggest challenge in that rollout?").

After the call, send a thank-you note within 24 hours. Reference something specific from the conversation. Close the loop. Then look for an opportunity to deliver permissionless value: an article, an introduction, a resource.

This is how you build a network that actually works for you. Not by blasting 200 messages into the void, but by having 10 real conversations with the right people.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What's a good response rate for LinkedIn outreach messages?

For personalized, well-crafted messages, aim for 25-40%. If you're getting below 15%, your messages are probably too generic or too long. The biggest factor is specificity. Messages that reference something concrete about the person's work get 3-4x more replies than generic templates sent without customization. Anil went from low response rates to getting accepted by a recruiter who ignores 50 requests per day, just by swapping one word and being specific about what inspired him.

### Should I send a connection request with a note or message people directly?

Send a connection request with a short note first. Keep the note under 300 characters (LinkedIn's limit for connection notes). Use the CHARM framework in miniature: "Hi [Name], I was inspired by [specific thing about them]. I'm exploring [area] and would love to connect." Once they accept, follow up with your full message within 48 hours. The connection note is your foot in the door. The follow-up message is the conversation starter.

### How many follow-up messages is too many?

One follow-up is the sweet spot. Send it 5-7 business days after your initial message. Keep it short: "Hi [Name], just floating this back to the top of your inbox. No worries if the timing doesn't work." After one follow-up with no response, move on. Two or more follow-ups crosses the line from persistent to pushy. The people who are best at networking aren't the ones who chase. They're the ones who plant seeds consistently and water them patiently. Someone you meet today might think of you when a role opens six months from now.
