You just got laid off.

Maybe you saw it coming. Maybe you didn't. Either way, the floor just dropped out from under you and your brain is doing that thing where it cycles between "I'm fine" and "everything is on fire" every 45 seconds.

Take a breath. You're going to be okay. But what you do in the next 7 days matters a lot more than you think.

## The Quicksand Rule

Getting laid off feels like quicksand. Your instinct is to thrash around. Apply to 100 jobs. Rewrite your resume at 2 AM. Say yes to every coffee chat. Move fast. Do everything.

But quicksand doesn't work that way. The more you thrash, the deeper you sink.

Here's what actually works:

1. **Stop moving.** Pause. Get your bearings.
2. **Discard the excess weight.** Let go of the shame, the panic, and the "I should have seen this coming."
3. **Call for help.** Reach out to your network, your support system, and the people who've been where you are.

That's the framework for your first week. Let's walk through it day by day.

## Day 1: Feel the Feelings (Then Set a Deadline)

Today is not the day for strategy. Today is the day you feel it.

Angry? Valid. Scared? Makes sense. Relieved? That's okay too. Whatever you're feeling, don't stuff it down. Call a friend. Go for a long walk. Cry if you need to. Write down every thought rattling around your head.

But here's the key: set a deadline on the emotional spiral. Give yourself today and tonight. Feel all of it. Tomorrow, we start moving forward.

This isn't about being tough or "bouncing back." It's about giving yourself permission to be human while also recognizing that extended wallowing becomes its own kind of quicksand.

One day to process. Then we get to work.

## Day 2-3: Update LinkedIn and Tell Your Network

This is where most people freeze. They don't want anyone to know. They feel embarrassed. So they go silent for weeks, applying to jobs in isolation while their inbox fills with nothing.

That silence is expensive.

### Update your LinkedIn headline

Don't write "Open to Opportunities." That's passive and vague. Instead, use a headline that says what you do and who you help. Something like:

**"Senior Product Manager | B2B SaaS | Built products used by 2M+ users"**

Then turn on the "Open to Work" setting (visible to recruiters only if you prefer).

For more on this, check out [the LinkedIn headline formula that actually gets recruiters to click](/blog/linkedin-headline-formula-that-gets-recruiters-to-click).

### Tell your network

Write a LinkedIn post. Keep it honest and specific:

> "After 4 years at [Company], my role was eliminated in a restructuring. I'm now looking for my next Senior Product Manager role in B2B SaaS, ideally at a growth-stage company in health tech or fintech. If you know of any openings or people I should connect with, I'd really appreciate the intro."

Be specific about what you want. "I'm looking for a job" helps no one. Name the role, the industry, and the type of company. Make it easy for people to help you.

Send direct messages to 15-20 people who know your work best. Former managers, close colleagues, mentors. Tell them what happened and what you're looking for. Most of them will want to help. Let them.

## Day 4-5: Audit Your Finances (The Burn Rate Math)

Nobody wants to do this part. But knowing your numbers is the difference between making decisions from clarity and making them from panic.

Here's the exercise:

### Calculate your monthly burn rate

Add up your non-negotiable monthly expenses: rent/mortgage, utilities, groceries, insurance, debt payments, subscriptions. That's your burn rate.

### Calculate your runway

Savings + severance + unemployment benefits, divided by your monthly burn rate. That's how many months you have.

If your runway is 3 months, you have time to be strategic. If it's 6+ months, you have room to be selective. If it's less than 2 months, you need to move fast and consider bridge income while you search.

### Understand what a prolonged search costs you

Here's the math that wakes people up. If your target salary is $200,000 per year, that's $3,846 per week. Every week your search drags on costs you nearly $4,000 in lost income.

A search that takes 12 weeks instead of 8? That's $15,384 in lost salary. A search that takes 6 months instead of 3? That's nearly $50,000 left on the table.

This isn't about panicking. It's about understanding the ROI of running a focused, strategic search instead of a slow, scattered one. The investment in doing this right, whether that's time, coaching, or tools, pays for itself many times over when it shortens your search by even a few weeks.

## Day 6-7: Start Your Job Search System

Now we build the machine.

### Set up your tracker

Create a spreadsheet or use a tool to track every company, every application, every conversation. Columns: Company, Role, Date Applied, Contact Person, Status, Next Step, Follow-up Date.

You need to see your entire pipeline at a glance. No more "I think I applied there... maybe?"

### Build your target company list

Identify 30-40 companies where you'd genuinely want to work. Don't just scrape job boards. Think about industries you're drawn to, companies whose products you use, organizations whose missions you care about.

### Prepare your core materials

Get your resume into fighting shape. This is not a biography of your career. It's an ad for what you can do for the next employer. Every bullet should show results, not responsibilities. ([Here's how to think about your resume as an ad](/blog/resume-is-an-ad-not-a-biography).)

Update your LinkedIn profile. Refresh your summary. Make sure your experience section mirrors your resume's strongest points.

### Create a daily routine

Job searching without structure is a recipe for burnout. Set a schedule:

- **Morning (2-3 hours):** Research, customize, and send targeted applications
- **Midday (1 hour):** Networking outreach, coffee chats, LinkedIn engagement
- **Afternoon (1-2 hours):** Interview prep, skill building, follow-ups

Treat it like a job. Because right now, getting your next job IS your job.

## What Not to Do This Week

A few traps to avoid:

- **Don't mass-apply to everything.** The panic instinct is to send 50 resumes. Resist it. A week of targeted setup saves you months of wasted effort.
- **Don't take the first thing that comes along.** Unless your financial runway is under 30 days, give yourself permission to be strategic. A bad-fit role is a 6-month detour.
- **Don't isolate.** The shame spiral is real, but hiding from your network is the opposite of what works. The people who land fastest are the ones who ask for help early.
- **Don't skip the finance audit.** Knowing your numbers reduces anxiety. Uncertainty is scarier than bad numbers.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How long does it take to find a new job after a layoff?

The average job search takes 3-6 months, but a focused, strategic search can cut that significantly. My clients who follow a structured approach typically land offers in 5-9 weeks. The biggest factor isn't the job market, it's how targeted your search is. Scattered applications extend your timeline. A clear target, strong materials, and active networking compress it.

### Should I take a lower-paying job just to have income?

It depends on your financial runway. If you have less than 30 days of expenses covered, bridge income makes sense while you search for the right role. If you have 3+ months of runway, resist the urge to grab the first offer. A role that's $30K below your market rate costs you far more over 2-3 years than a few extra weeks of job searching. Know your numbers before you decide.

### Should I mention being laid off in interviews?

Yes, briefly and without apologizing. Layoffs happen to talented people constantly, and hiring managers know this. Say something like: "My role was eliminated when the company restructured its [department]. I'm now focused on finding a role where I can apply my experience in [specific skill] to [specific type of challenge]." Keep it factual, forward-looking, and under 30 seconds. Then move to what you bring to the table.
