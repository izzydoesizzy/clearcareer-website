# ADHD Job Search Cheat Sheet

**The quick-reference survival guide for job seekers whose brains work differently. From someone who gets it.**

> **Disclaimer:** This guide shares practical strategies for managing ADHD tendencies during job search. It is not a diagnostic tool or medical advice. If you suspect you have ADHD and haven't been assessed, talk to your doctor.

This is the one-pager (okay, two-pager) you tape to your wall. No fluff. No theory. Just what works.

**Time estimate:** 5 minutes to read, then reference daily
**What you'll need:** A pen, a sticky note, and the willingness to try one thing differently

---

## The ADHD Tax in Job Search

You know the ADHD tax. The late fees, the forgotten appointments, the half-finished projects. In job search, the ADHD tax looks like this:

- **You research a company for 3 hours and forget to actually apply**
- You start customizing a cover letter, get bored at paragraph two, and submit it half-done
- You have 47 browser tabs open, all job boards, none of them actionable
- You hyperfocus on perfecting your resume for a week and send zero applications
- You miss interview prep because time blindness hit again
- You ghost a recruiter not out of disinterest but because the email sat in your inbox until the shame of not replying became bigger than the task itself

If you felt personally attacked by that list, this guide is for you.

I'm Izzy Piyale-Sheard, founder of ClearCareer. I have ADHD. I've built an entire career coaching business around helping people land jobs, and I still have to fight my own brain every single day to stay on task. Everything in this guide comes from personal experience plus what I've seen work for hundreds of job seekers whose brains operate the same way.

## 5 Immediate Hacks (Start Today)

### Hack 1: The 2-Minute Application Start

Your brain resists starting. Not doing. Starting.

So make starting stupidly small. Open the job posting. Copy the company name into your tracking sheet. That's it. You've started. Now your brain is engaged, and 80% of the time, momentum carries you through the rest.

If it doesn't carry you through? Fine. You still logged the opportunity. Come back later.

**The rule:** Never try to "sit down and apply to jobs." Instead, sit down and copy one company name into your tracker. The rest often follows.

### Hack 2: The Body Double

You will get more done in one hour at a coffee shop sitting across from a stranger than in eight hours alone at your kitchen table.

Body doubling works because your brain borrows focus from the social environment. The presence of another person doing work creates a gentle external accountability that ADHD brains crave.

**Options:**
- Work from a coffee shop or library
- Join a virtual co-working session (Focusmate is free)
- Ask a friend to sit with you while you both work on your own things
- Put on a "study with me" livestream as a minimum-viable body double

### Hack 3: The Timer, Not the To-Do List

To-do lists are where ADHD job searches go to die. You write 15 items, feel overwhelmed, and do none of them.

Replace the list with a timer.

Set a 25-minute timer (Pomodoro technique). Pick one thing. Work on only that thing until the timer stops. Then take a 5-minute break. Repeat.

You don't need to finish. You need to focus for 25 minutes. That's achievable. And 3 Pomodoros (75 minutes of focused work) will accomplish more than a full day of unfocused anxiety.

### Hack 4: The Outbox, Not the Inbox

Stop starting your day by checking email and LinkedIn. That's reactive mode. ADHD brains in reactive mode get pulled in 15 directions before 10 AM.

Start your day with one outbound action:
- Send one application
- Send one networking message
- Follow up with one contact

Outbox first. Inbox second. Offense before defense.

### Hack 5: The Shame Eraser

That recruiter you ghosted. That application you started and never finished. That networking event you RSVP'd to and didn't attend.

Do this: send the message anyway. Apply anyway. It's not too late.

```
"Hi [Name], I apologize for the delayed response. I'm very interested
in this opportunity and would love to continue the conversation if
the role is still available."
```

That's it. No elaborate excuse. No shame spiral. Just re-engage. Most people don't care about the delay. They care about the follow-through.

## Your Daily Cheat Sheet

Print this. Stick it on your wall. Follow it.

**Morning (pick your peak focus window, whenever that is):**
1. One outbound action (application, message, or follow-up)
2. One 25-minute Pomodoro on your highest-priority task
3. Check email and LinkedIn (with a 15-minute timer)

**Afternoon (or whenever your energy dips):**
1. Low-effort tasks: update tracker, save job postings, organize files
2. One networking conversation (text, call, or coffee)
3. Body movement: walk, gym, stretch, anything

**Evening:**
1. Write tomorrow's ONE priority on a sticky note
2. Put the sticky note where you'll see it first thing
3. Done. Close the laptop. Job search is over for today.

## The One Thing That Changes Everything

If you only do one thing from this cheat sheet, do this:

**Write down your ONE most important task for tomorrow. Just one. Put it where you'll see it before anything else.**

ADHD brains don't fail because of lack of ability. They fail because of lack of clarity about what to do next. When you wake up and the first thing you see is "Apply to [Company] [Role]," you skip the 45-minute decision paralysis about what to work on.

One task. Every night. On paper. Visible.

That's the whole system.

## Key Takeaway

Job searching with ADHD is not harder because you're less capable. It's harder because the job search process was designed by and for neurotypical brains. Linear processes, sustained focus, long-term planning, delayed rewards: all of these are executive function demands that ADHD makes genuinely difficult. Stop blaming yourself. Start building systems that work with your brain instead of against it. The five hacks above are your starting point. Pick one. Try it today.
