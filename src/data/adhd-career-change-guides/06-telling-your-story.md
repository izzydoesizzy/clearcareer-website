# Telling Your Story

**How to explain a non-linear career without apologizing for it.**

You have had five jobs in three industries. Or ten jobs in ten years. Or a career that looks like a pinball machine from the outside. Every interview, every networking conversation, someone asks you to "walk me through your background" and you panic. Because the truth sounds chaotic and the polished version sounds fake.

Here is the thing: non-linear careers are increasingly common. And with the right framing, yours becomes an asset, not a liability. This guide helps you build a career narrative that is honest, coherent, and positions your varied experience as a deliberate advantage.

**Time:** 45-60 minutes. **What you'll need:** Your resume and the willingness to stop apologizing for your career path.

---

## Why Non-Linear Careers Need a Narrative

When a hiring manager reads your resume, they are doing one thing: building a story in their head about who you are. If your career is linear (same industry, progressive titles, clear trajectory), the story writes itself. If your career is non-linear, the hiring manager will write their own story. And it usually sounds like: "This person cannot commit" or "What is going on here?"

Your job is to give them a better story. One that is true, that connects the dots, and that makes your variety look intentional.

You are not lying. You are editing.

## Step 2: Reframe "Scattered" as "Versatile"

The language you use matters. Here is a translation table:

| What You Think | What You Say |
|---------------|-------------|
| "I've jumped around a lot" | "I've built experience across multiple industries" |
| "I couldn't figure out what I wanted" | "I deliberately explored different paths before committing" |
| "I got bored and left" | "I'm driven by challenge and growth, and I moved when I had maximized my learning" |
| "My career is all over the place" | "My background gives me a cross-functional perspective that specialists don't have" |
| "I changed industries three times" | "I've successfully transitioned between industries, which means I learn fast and adapt quickly" |
| "I have a lot of short stints" | "I've worked in high-impact project-based roles" |

You are not spinning. You are choosing to tell the true version of the story that serves you rather than the true version that undermines you. Both are true. Pick the one that gets you hired.

## Step 3: Build Your Career Narrative

Use one of these three frameworks to structure your story.

### Framework 1: The Evolution Story

"I started in [FIRST FIELD], where I developed [SKILL]. That led me to [SECOND FIELD], where I discovered I was really good at [SKILL/STRENGTH]. Now I'm bringing all of that to [TARGET FIELD], because [REASON IT ALL CONNECTS]."

**Example:** "I started in hospitality management, where I got really good at high-pressure operations and team leadership. That led me into tech project management, where I discovered I love building systems from scratch. Now I'm bringing that operational and systems-building experience to product management, because the best PMs are the ones who understand both the people side and the process side."

### Framework 2: The Problem-Solver Story

"Throughout my career, I've been drawn to [TYPE OF PROBLEM]. In [FIELD 1], that looked like [EXAMPLE]. In [FIELD 2], it looked like [EXAMPLE]. Now I want to solve that same kind of problem at [TARGET COMPANY/INDUSTRY]."

**Example:** "I've always been drawn to communication problems, specifically helping people understand complex things. In journalism, that meant translating policy for general audiences. In tech, that meant building documentation and training programs. Now I'm moving into UX writing, because it is the same challenge in a new medium: making complex systems feel simple to users."

### Framework 3: The Skills Collector Story

"I've intentionally built a toolkit that crosses traditional career boundaries. From [FIELD 1] I bring [SKILL]. From [FIELD 2] I bring [SKILL]. From [FIELD 3] I bring [SKILL]. That combination is exactly what [TARGET ROLE] needs."

**Example:** "From my time in sales, I bring an understanding of what customers actually need and how to communicate value. From my marketing work, I bring data literacy and campaign strategy. From my recent work in customer success, I bring retention thinking and long-term relationship management. Product marketing needs all three, and most people in the field only have one or two."

## Step 4: The Disclosure Framework

If you have ADHD and you are wondering whether to disclose it in professional settings, here is a practical framework.

### When to disclose (potentially)

- **To a direct manager after you are hired,** if you need specific accommodations (flexible hours, written instructions, noise-canceling environment)
- **In ADHD-friendly workplaces** that explicitly value neurodiversity (check their careers page and ERGs)
- **When it explains a strength:** "I have ADHD, which is actually why I'm so good at crisis management. My brain lights up under pressure."

### When not to disclose

- **During interviews** at companies where you do not know the culture. Bias is real and common.
- **On your resume or cover letter.** Never.
- **To explain gaps or short tenures.** Use professional framing instead (see the reframes in Step 2).
- **When you are unsure.** Default to not disclosing. You can always disclose later. You cannot un-disclose.

### How to disclose (when you choose to)

Keep it brief, matter-of-fact, and strength-forward:

"I have ADHD, which means my brain works a bit differently. In practice, [SPECIFIC THING: I do my best deep work in the morning, I prefer written instructions, I work in sprints rather than steady-state]. I've built systems that work really well for me. I wanted you to know so we can set things up for success."

Do not over-explain. Do not apologize. Do not make it a big deal. Treat it like telling someone you are left-handed.

## Step 5: Practice Scripts

### "Tell me about yourself" (Career change version)

```
"I'm a [YOUR IDENTITY/ROLE DESCRIPTION] with [X YEARS] across [INDUSTRIES/FUNCTIONS]. The thread through my career has been [YOUR THREAD FROM STEP 1]. Most recently at [RECENT COMPANY], I [KEY ACHIEVEMENT]. I'm now focused on [TARGET FIELD/ROLE] because [REASON THAT CONNECTS YOUR THREAD TO THIS OPPORTUNITY]."
```

### "Why the career change?"

```
"Honestly, [TARGET FIELD] is where my strongest skills have the most impact. My background in [PREVIOUS FIELD] gave me [SPECIFIC SKILLS], and I've realized those skills are exactly what [TARGET FIELD] needs. Rather than starting over, I'm building on [X YEARS] of experience in a new direction."
```

### "I see you've had a few different roles..."

```
"Yes, and that's been deliberate. Each role taught me something I use every day. [ROLE 1] taught me [SKILL]. [ROLE 2] taught me [SKILL]. That breadth is actually my competitive advantage, because I can [SPECIFIC VALUE YOU BRING THAT SPECIALISTS CANNOT]."
```

### "Where do you see yourself in 5 years?"

This question terrifies career changers and ADHD adults alike. Here is the honest-but-professional answer:

```
"I want to be deep in [TARGET FIELD], having built real expertise and delivered measurable results. I'm not the kind of person who plans a rigid 5-year trajectory. I follow impact and growth. But the direction is clear: I want to be excellent at [SPECIFIC THING] and leading [TYPE OF WORK/TEAM]."
```

## The 60-Second Version

You need a version of your story that takes 60 seconds to tell. For networking events, elevator rides, and the first minute of phone screens.

Template:

"I'm [NAME]. I spent [X YEARS] in [FIELD(S)], where I [ONE KEY THING YOU DID]. Now I'm moving into [TARGET FIELD] because [SIMPLE, HONEST REASON]. What connects it all is [YOUR THREAD]. I'm looking for [TYPE OF ROLE] where I can [VALUE YOU BRING]."

Practice it out loud 5 times. Time yourself. Trim anything over 60 seconds.

---

## Key Takeaway

Your career is not a mess. It is a story that has not been edited yet. Find the thread, reframe the narrative, practice the delivery, and walk into every conversation with confidence instead of apology.

You did not wander. You explored. And now you know more than most people in the room.
