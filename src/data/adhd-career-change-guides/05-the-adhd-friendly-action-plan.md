# The ADHD-Friendly Action Plan

**Forget 90-day plans. Your brain needs 2-week sprints.**

You know what happens with 90-day career change plans? You spend a weekend building a beautiful spreadsheet. You color-code everything. You feel amazing. By Day 8, you have not looked at it once. By Day 30, you cannot remember where you saved it.

This is not a motivation problem. It is a time horizon problem. ADHD brains cannot sustain motivation toward a goal that is 90 days away. The reward is too distant, the steps are too abstract, and there are too many opportunities to get distracted between now and then.

The fix: 2-week sprints. Short enough to see the finish line. Long enough to make real progress. At the end of each sprint, you celebrate, reassess, and plan the next one.

**Time:** 30 minutes to plan your first sprint. **What you'll need:** A timer, something to write with, and one clear priority.

---

## Why 2-Week Sprints Work for ADHD

**The deadline is always close.** Two weeks from now is close enough to create urgency, which is one of the four ADHD activation triggers.

**The scope is small.** You are not planning your entire career change. You are planning what you will accomplish in the next 14 days. That is it.

**The reward is frequent.** Every two weeks, you finish something. You cross it off. You feel the dopamine hit of completion. Then you plan the next sprint.

**Course corrections are cheap.** If a sprint goes sideways, you have only lost two weeks, not three months. You adjust and try again.

## How to Plan a Sprint

### Step 1: Pick ONE Priority

Not three. Not five. One. The single most important thing you can accomplish in the next two weeks that moves your career change forward.

Examples of good sprint priorities:
- Complete an online course in [target skill]
- Have 3 informational interviews with people in [target industry]
- Build a portfolio project that demonstrates [skill]
- Update resume and LinkedIn for [target role]
- Apply to 5 specific jobs with customized applications
- Research 10 companies in [target industry]

Examples of bad sprint priorities:
- "Work on career change" (too vague)
- "Get a new job" (too big)
- "Figure out what I want to do" (not actionable)
- "Complete certification, update resume, network with 20 people, and apply to 15 jobs" (too many things)

**Your Sprint 1 priority:** ________________

### Step 2: Break It Into Daily Actions

Take your one priority and break it into specific daily actions. Each action should take 15-45 minutes. Not longer. ADHD brains work better in short, intense bursts.

| Day | Action | Time Needed | Done? |
|-----|--------|-------------|-------|
| Day 1 | ________________ | _____ min | [ ] |
| Day 2 | ________________ | _____ min | [ ] |
| Day 3 | ________________ | _____ min | [ ] |
| Day 4 | ________________ | _____ min | [ ] |
| Day 5 | ________________ | _____ min | [ ] |
| Day 6 | ________________ | _____ min | [ ] |
| Day 7 | REST | 0 | [ ] |
| Day 8 | ________________ | _____ min | [ ] |
| Day 9 | ________________ | _____ min | [ ] |
| Day 10 | ________________ | _____ min | [ ] |
| Day 11 | ________________ | _____ min | [ ] |
| Day 12 | ________________ | _____ min | [ ] |
| Day 13 | ________________ | _____ min | [ ] |
| Day 14 | REVIEW + CELEBRATE | 30 min | [ ] |

Notice Day 7 is rest. Non-negotiable. ADHD brains burn out fast when they are "on" every day. Plan the rest day or your brain will take one without your permission (usually on the day you needed to be productive).

### Step 3: Set Your Start Trigger

An ADHD brain needs a launch trigger. Not "I'll work on this when I have time." You will never have time. You need a specific cue that tells your brain "it is time to do the thing."

Pick one:
- [ ] Right after my morning coffee, before I check my phone
- [ ] Immediately after lunch, for 30 minutes
- [ ] At [SPECIFIC TIME], at [SPECIFIC LOCATION]
- [ ] After I [EXISTING HABIT], I do 15 minutes of sprint work

The best trigger is one attached to something you already do every day. This is called habit stacking, and it is one of the most effective ADHD strategies for building new routines.

**My start trigger:** After I ________________, I will ________________ for _____ minutes.

## The Body Doubling Hack

Body doubling is working alongside another person, even if they are doing something completely different. It works for ADHD brains because the presence of another person creates just enough social accountability to keep you on task.

### Options for body doubling:

**In person:** Work at a coffee shop, library, or coworking space. Or ask a friend if you can sit in their living room and work.

**Virtual:** Use Focusmate (focusmate.com), which pairs you with a stranger for 25 or 50-minute work sessions on video. You tell each other what you are going to work on, then you work silently, then you check in at the end.

**Asynchronous:** Find an accountability partner. Text each other at the start and end of each work session. "Starting my sprint work now." "Done. Finished [task]."

**The ADHD job search buddy system:** Find someone else who is also job searching or career changing. Meet once a week (video or in-person) for 60 minutes. First 10 minutes: share what you accomplished last week. Next 40 minutes: work together in silence. Last 10 minutes: commit to next week's actions.

## Avoiding the Rabbit Hole

ADHD career changers lose weeks to rabbit holes. You sit down to research companies in your target industry and three hours later you are watching YouTube videos about the history of that industry, reading someone's blog about their career change, and signing up for a certification program you do not need.

### The Rabbit Hole Prevention Protocol

**Before you start working, write down your ONE task for this session.** Put it on a sticky note in front of you.

**Set a timer.** 25 minutes (Pomodoro). When the timer goes off, stop. Check: are you still doing the thing on the sticky note? If yes, reset the timer. If no, pull yourself back.

**Keep a "parking lot" notepad next to you.** When an interesting tangent appears (and it will), write it on the parking lot and go back to your task. You can explore the parking lot later. Not now.

**Close every browser tab except the one you need.** Use an extension like OneTab to save all your open tabs for later. Your brain cannot resist open tabs. Remove the temptation.

**The "Is This Moving Me Forward?" test.** Every 25 minutes, ask yourself: "Is what I am doing right now directly connected to my sprint priority?" Yes = keep going. No = stop and redirect.

## The Sprint Review (Day 14)

At the end of each sprint, spend 30 minutes on this review:

**1. What did I accomplish?**
List everything you finished, even if it was not on the original plan.

**2. What did I not finish, and why?**
Be specific. "I ran out of time" is not specific enough. "I spent 3 hours on Tuesday researching certifications I do not need" is specific and useful.

**3. What surprised me?**
What was easier than expected? Harder? What did you learn about yourself?

**4. What is my energy level?**
Rate 1-10. If you are below a 5, your next sprint should be lighter. If you are at 8+, you can push harder.

**5. What is my ONE priority for the next sprint?**
Based on what you accomplished and what is left, what is the single most important next step?

## The Sprint Roadmap

You do not need to plan all your sprints in advance. But it helps to have a rough sense of the sequence. Here is a typical career change sprint sequence:

| Sprint | Focus | Outcome |
|--------|-------|---------|
| 1 | Research and validation | 3-5 informational interviews completed, industry understanding |
| 2 | Skill gap assessment | Know exactly what you need to learn, have a plan |
| 3 | Skill building (part 1) | Complete first course/project/certification module |
| 4 | Skill building (part 2) | Complete second learning milestone |
| 5 | Materials preparation | Resume, LinkedIn, and portfolio updated for target career |
| 6 | Active search begins | 5-10 quality applications submitted, networking active |
| 7-10 | Search and interview | Ongoing applications, interviews, refinement |

Each sprint builds on the last. If a sprint needs to be repeated (you did not finish), that is fine. Repeat it. There is no penalty for taking an extra two weeks.

## When You Fall Off the Plan

You will. Every ADHD adult does. A sprint will go sideways. You will lose a week to a hyperfocus tangent or a low-energy crash or life getting in the way.

When that happens:

1. **Do not shame yourself.** Shame makes ADHD worse. It does not motivate you. It paralyzes you.
2. **Do not restart from scratch.** Pick up where you left off, not from the beginning.
3. **Shrink the next sprint.** If a full sprint feels overwhelming, do a 1-week mini-sprint instead.
4. **Phone a friend.** Tell your accountability partner or body double. Say "I fell off. Help me get back on." That is it.
5. **Celebrate showing up again.** The fact that you are restarting instead of quitting is the skill. That IS the thing.

---

## Key Takeaway

You do not need a master plan. You need a system that works with your brain instead of against it. Two-week sprints, one priority at a time, body doubling, rabbit hole prevention, and regular reviews.

Plan Sprint 1 right now. Set your start trigger. Start tomorrow.
