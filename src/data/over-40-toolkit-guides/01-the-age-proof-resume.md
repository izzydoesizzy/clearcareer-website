# The Age-Proof Resume

**20+ years of experience is an asset. Your resume needs to present it as one.**

If you have been working for 20 or more years, your resume has a unique challenge: too much history. Listing every role since 1998 dates you instantly. But cutting too aggressively erases the depth that makes you valuable.

This guide walks you through the specific techniques for presenting a long career without triggering age bias in recruiters, ATS systems, or hiring managers.

**Time:** 2-3 hours to rework your resume. **What you'll need:** Your current resume, 5-10 job postings for roles you are targeting, and a willingness to let go of some history.

---

## The Last 10-15 Years Rule

Your resume should emphasize the last 10-15 years of your career. Everything before that gets compressed or removed entirely.

### Why This Works

- Hiring managers care most about recent experience
- ATS systems weight recent roles more heavily
- Detailed experience from 20+ years ago signals your age without adding value
- The skills and tools from early career roles may be obsolete

### How to Apply It

**Last 10 years:** Full detail. Bullets with achievements, metrics, and impact.
**10-15 years ago:** Abbreviated. Company, title, dates, and 1-2 bullet points.
**15+ years ago:** Either omit entirely, or group under "Earlier Career" with titles and companies only (no dates).

### The "Earlier Career" Section

At the bottom of your experience section:

```
EARLIER CAREER
Senior Analyst, [Company Name]
Project Coordinator, [Company Name]
```

No dates. No bullets. Just enough to show career progression without exposing your timeline.

---

## Removing Age Signals

Recruiters and AI screening tools use subtle cues to estimate age. Here is what to remove or modify.

| Age Signal | What to Do |
|-----------|-----------|
| Graduation year on degrees | Remove it. Just list the degree and institution. |
| "20+ years of experience" in summary | Replace with "extensive experience" or a specific number under 15 |
| Dates before 2010 on your resume | Compress or remove (Earlier Career section) |
| Outdated technology (Lotus Notes, ColdFusion, Windows XP) | Remove unless directly relevant |
| Objective statement | Replace with a Professional Summary (objective statements are outdated format) |
| References available upon request | Remove (this has not been standard since the 1990s) |
| Full home address | Use city and province/state only |
| Two-page resume for 10 years of experience | One page unless you have 15+ years of relevant detail |
| AOL or Hotmail email address | Get a Gmail address |

## ATS Optimization for Experienced Professionals

Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS) parse your resume for keywords, formatting, and structure. Experienced professionals often have resumes that are well-written but ATS-unfriendly.

### Format Rules

- **Use a single-column layout.** Two-column designs confuse many ATS parsers.
- **Standard section headers:** "Professional Experience," "Education," "Skills." Do not get creative with "Where I've Made an Impact" or "My Journey."
- **No headers or footers.** ATS often cannot read content in headers/footers.
- **No tables for core content.** ATS struggles with tables. Use them sparingly.
- **Save as PDF or .docx** depending on the application system's preference. When in doubt, .docx is more ATS-friendly.
- **Standard fonts:** Arial, Calibri, Garamond, or similar. Avoid decorative fonts.

### Keyword Optimization

1. Pull 5-10 job postings for your target role.
2. List every skill, tool, and qualification that appears in 3+ postings.
3. Make sure each of those keywords appears somewhere on your resume (if you genuinely have the skill).
4. Use the exact language from the posting. If they say "stakeholder management," do not write "working with stakeholders."

### Skills Section

Add a dedicated Skills section near the top of your resume:

```
SKILLS
Project Management: Agile, Scrum, Waterfall, JIRA, Asana, MS Project
Data Analysis: Excel (advanced), Tableau, Power BI, SQL
Leadership: Team building, coaching, performance management, change management
Industry: [Relevant industry certifications, regulations, or domain knowledge]
```

This section serves two purposes: it feeds the ATS the keywords it needs, and it shows hiring managers at a glance that you have current, relevant tools in your toolkit.

## Achievement Bullets for Experienced Professionals

At your career stage, you have bigger achievements than junior candidates. Use them. But format them for maximum impact.

### The CAR Format

**C**hallenge: What was the situation or problem?
**A**ction: What did you do?
**R**esult: What was the measurable outcome?

### Examples at Different Seniority Levels

**Manager Level:**
- Led a team of 12 through a system migration that reduced processing time by 35% and saved $400K annually
- Designed and implemented a new onboarding program that decreased new hire ramp-up time from 90 to 45 days

**Director Level:**
- Built the customer success function from scratch, growing the team from 2 to 18 and improving retention from 72% to 91% over 3 years
- Managed a $5M operational budget, identifying $800K in efficiency savings through process automation

**VP/Executive Level:**
- Drove the strategic pivot from on-premise to SaaS, resulting in 40% ARR growth and $15M in new revenue within 18 months
- Led the integration of a $50M acquisition, achieving full operational integration in 6 months while retaining 95% of key talent

### What to Emphasize at Your Level

- **Scale:** How large were the teams, budgets, or projects you managed?
- **Impact:** What changed because of your work? Revenue, cost, efficiency, retention, satisfaction?
- **Strategy:** Did you set direction, not just execute? Show strategic thinking.
- **Leadership:** How did you develop people and build teams?
- **Transformation:** Did you change something from one state to a better state?

## The Professional Summary

Your summary is the first thing a human reads (after your name and title). For experienced professionals, it needs to signal "senior, current, and relevant."

### Template

```
[ROLE DESCRIPTOR] with [X] years of experience driving [OUTCOME TYPE] in [INDUSTRY/CONTEXT]. Track record of [TOP ACHIEVEMENT with number]. Known for [2-3 KEY STRENGTHS]. Currently focused on [WHAT YOU WANT TO DO NEXT / WHAT YOU BRING].
```

### Example

```
Operations executive with 15+ years of experience scaling teams and systems in high-growth technology environments. Led the build-out of a 200-person customer operations function that achieved 95% satisfaction scores and $40M in managed revenue. Known for translating complex operational challenges into scalable solutions. Seeking to bring enterprise-scale operational expertise to a growth-stage company.
```

Notice: no dates. No "20+ years." The experience speaks for itself through the scale and specificity of the achievements.

## The Two-Page Question

**One page:** If you can tell your story in one page without sacrificing key achievements, do it. One page is always easier to read.

**Two pages:** If you have 15+ years of relevant experience and cannot fit it on one page without losing critical achievements, two pages is acceptable. But the first page must stand alone. If someone only reads page one, they should still want to call you.

**Three pages:** Almost never. The exception is academic CVs or very senior executive roles with extensive board and advisory experience.

## Quick Checklist

- [ ] Graduation dates removed from education section
- [ ] Experience detailed for last 10-15 years only
- [ ] Older roles compressed into "Earlier Career" section
- [ ] No outdated technology listed
- [ ] Skills section includes current tools and methodologies
- [ ] ATS-friendly format (single column, standard headers, no tables)
- [ ] Keywords from target job postings included
- [ ] Achievement bullets show scale, impact, and leadership
- [ ] Professional summary positions you as senior and current
- [ ] Email address is professional and current

---

## Key Takeaway

Your age is not the problem. How your resume presents your age can be. Strip the signals, lead with recent achievements, show that your tools and methods are current, and let the depth of your experience be an advantage rather than a liability.

Twenty years of experience means twenty years of solved problems, built teams, and delivered results. Make sure your resume says that.
