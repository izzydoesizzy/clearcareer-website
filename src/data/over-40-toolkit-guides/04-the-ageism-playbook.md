# The Ageism Playbook

**It is real. Here is how to navigate it without pretending it does not exist.**

Age discrimination in hiring is illegal in most jurisdictions. It is also pervasive. A 2023 AARP study found that 78% of workers aged 40-65 have seen or experienced age discrimination in the workplace. A Resume Builder survey found that 36% of hiring managers admitted to age bias in their hiring decisions. An audit study published in the American Economic Review found that older applicants received 26% fewer callbacks than younger applicants with identical qualifications.

You cannot eliminate ageism from the job market. But you can learn to spot it, work around it, and protect yourself when you encounter it.

**Time:** 20 minutes to read. Reference as needed throughout your search. **What you'll need:** Awareness and a strategy.

---

## The 99% Stat

Here is a number that should make you angry, then strategic: approximately 99% of age discrimination claims filed with employment standards agencies are dismissed or settled for small amounts. The bar for proving age discrimination in hiring is extremely high. Companies know this.

This does not mean you have no protections. It means your primary strategy should be proactive (making ageism irrelevant) rather than reactive (filing complaints after the fact). Prevention beats litigation.

## Legal Protections (Know Your Rights)

### In Canada
- **Canadian Human Rights Act** protects against age discrimination in federally regulated workplaces
- **Provincial human rights codes** (e.g., Ontario Human Rights Code) protect against age discrimination in employment
- Age is a protected ground in all provinces and territories
- Employers cannot ask your age, date of birth, or graduation year in interviews

### In the United States
- **Age Discrimination in Employment Act (ADEA)** protects workers 40 and older
- Applies to employers with 20+ employees
- State laws may provide additional protections
- Cannot mandate retirement at a specific age (with narrow exceptions)

### What Employers Cannot Do
- Include age requirements in job postings (unless a bona fide occupational requirement)
- Ask your age or date of birth in an interview
- Use language like "digital native," "recent graduate," or "young and hungry" in postings (these are proxies for age)
- Make hiring decisions based on age or perceived age
- Offer lower salaries based on how many "working years" you have left

### What Is Hard to Prove
- That age was the reason you were not hired (they will cite other reasons)
- That "culture fit" was code for "too old"
- That the "energy" comments in feedback were about age
- That the preference for a "less experienced" candidate was age-based

## Spotting Age Bias: The Code Words

Interviewers and recruiters rarely say "you're too old." They use coded language.

| What They Say | What They Might Mean |
|--------------|---------------------|
| "We're looking for someone earlier in their career" | We want someone younger and cheaper |
| "You might be overqualified" | You're too experienced for what we want to pay |
| "We have a very young, energetic team" | You might not fit in |
| "Are you comfortable with fast-paced environments?" | Can you keep up? |
| "Culture fit" (with no further explanation) | You are not like the rest of our team (which is younger) |
| "We need someone who can grow with the role" | We want someone who will stay for 10+ years |
| "Digital native" in the posting | Under 35 preferred |
| "How do you feel about working with new technology?" | Are you tech-savvy enough? |
| "This is a high-energy environment" | Are you too old for this? |

Not every instance of these phrases is age bias. Context matters. But if you hear multiple coded phrases in one conversation, trust your instinct.

## Age-Friendly Companies: How to Find Them

Some companies actively value experienced professionals. Here is how to find them.

### Green Flags

- Job postings that mention "diverse experience" or "non-traditional backgrounds"
- Leadership team includes people visibly over 50
- Employee Resource Groups (ERGs) for experienced professionals or multi-generational workforce
- Glassdoor reviews from employees 40+ that are positive
- The company has won "age-friendly employer" awards (AARP, for example)
- Flexible work arrangements (tends to correlate with age-inclusive culture)
- They do not use "ninja," "rockstar," or "digital native" in postings

### Red Flags

- Career page photos only show people in their 20s
- Job postings emphasize "cultural fit" without defining it
- Glassdoor reviews mention age-related concerns
- High turnover among older employees
- Mandatory social events centered around drinking culture
- Office is designed exclusively for 25-year-olds (ping pong, beer taps, no private offices)

### Where Age-Friendly Companies Tend to Cluster

- Government and public sector
- Professional services (accounting, consulting, law)
- Healthcare
- Financial services
- Manufacturing and engineering
- Education
- Established tech companies (as opposed to early-stage startups)
- Nonprofits

## The Proactive Strategy

Instead of fighting ageism after the fact, reduce its impact before it starts.

### 1. Control the First Impression

Your resume, LinkedIn, and initial conversations should present you as current, energetic, and forward-looking. Not by hiding your age, but by emphasizing your relevance.

- Lead with recent experience and current tools
- Use modern resume formatting
- Keep LinkedIn active with current commentary
- Reference recent achievements, not ancient history

### 2. Network Past the Gatekeepers

Entry-level recruiters and ATS systems are the most likely points of age bias. Bypass them.

- Use referrals (referred candidates bypass the initial screen)
- Reach out directly to hiring managers
- Attend industry events where you meet decision-makers
- Use executive recruiters who specialize in senior placements

### 3. Reframe Your Experience as an Asset

In every conversation, frame your years of experience in terms of what they enable:

- Pattern recognition ("I've seen this problem before, in three different contexts")
- Network depth ("I can make introductions others can't")
- Calm under pressure ("I've navigated [X] downturns and I know what works")
- Teaching and mentoring ("I develop people, which multiplies the team's output")
- Institutional knowledge ("I bring context that takes years to build")

### 4. Stay Visibly Current

Nothing defeats age assumptions faster than demonstrating fluency with current tools, trends, and methods.

- Use AI tools and mention it casually
- Reference current industry trends with specificity
- Maintain an active, modern LinkedIn presence
- Continue learning visibly (courses, certifications, conference talks)

## When You Encounter Ageism

### In a Job Posting
Document it (screenshot). If it is egregious ("seeking recent graduates" for a senior role), you can report it to the job board. Most boards have policies against discriminatory language.

### In an Interview
Note the comments. Stay professional. After the interview, document what was said, by whom, and when. If you want to pursue it, contact an employment lawyer for a free consultation.

### After a Rejection
If you have strong evidence of age bias (you were told you were overqualified despite perfect qualifications, the person hired was significantly younger with less experience, you heard coded language in the interview), consult an employment lawyer. Most offer free initial consultations.

### Emotionally
Ageism stings. It makes you feel invisible and devalued. Acknowledge the frustration. Talk to peers who understand. Then channel the energy into your proactive strategy. The best revenge against ageism is getting hired somewhere that values what you bring.

---

## Key Takeaway

Age discrimination is real, common, and difficult to fight through legal channels. Your best strategy is a proactive one: present yourself as current and relevant, network past the gatekeepers, reframe your experience as a competitive advantage, and target companies that value what experienced professionals bring.

You cannot control every hiring manager's bias. You can control how you show up. Show up as someone who is impossible to overlook.
