# Second Act Exploration

**Full-time employment is not your only option. Here are four others.**

By 40, you have something most people do not: decades of deep expertise, a network of professional relationships, and the credibility that comes from having done the work. These assets are worth more than a single salary. They can fuel an entirely different kind of career.

This guide explores four alternative career models: consulting, fractional leadership, portfolio careers, and starting a business. Not everyone should pursue these. But everyone over 40 should at least know they exist.

**Time:** 30 minutes to read. Longer if you want to explore one model in depth. **What you'll need:** Curiosity and an honest assessment of your risk tolerance.

---

## Model 1: Consulting

### What It Is
Selling your expertise to multiple clients on a project or retainer basis instead of working for one employer.

### Who It Is Best For
- Subject matter experts with 15+ years in a specific domain
- People who enjoy variety and autonomy
- Strong networkers who can generate their own business
- People who prefer project-based work over ongoing operations

### How It Works

**Solo consulting:** You are the consultant. You find clients, do the work, and get paid directly. Low overhead, high margins, full control.

**Consulting firm:** You join an existing consulting firm (boutique or major). They find clients and you do the work. Less autonomy, more stability.

**The economics:**
- Solo consultants typically charge 2-3x their equivalent hourly employee rate
- A $150K/year employee ($75/hour) might charge $150-$225/hour as a consultant
- Working 30 billable hours/week for 48 weeks = $216K-$324K/year
- Reality check: you will not bill 30 hours every week, especially at first. Plan for 50-70% utilization in year one.

### Getting Started
1. Define your consulting niche (the more specific, the better)
2. Package your expertise into 2-3 service offerings
3. Tell your network you are consulting (most first clients come from existing relationships)
4. Set your rate (start at 2x your hourly employee equivalent and adjust)
5. Create a simple website (even a LinkedIn profile with "Open to consulting" works)
6. Deliver outstanding work for your first 2-3 clients (referrals drive the business)

### Risks
- Income is inconsistent, especially in year one
- You are responsible for your own benefits, taxes, and retirement
- Business development never stops
- Isolation (you work alone much of the time)

## Model 2: Fractional Leadership

### What It Is
Serving as a part-time executive (CFO, CMO, CTO, COO, CHRO) for companies that need senior leadership but cannot afford or do not need a full-time hire.

### Who It Is Best For
- Senior leaders (Director+ level) with broad functional expertise
- People who enjoy strategic work more than day-to-day management
- Experienced professionals who want to work with multiple companies
- People who value flexibility and variety

### How It Works

**The model:** You work 1-2 days per week at each company. Most fractional leaders serve 2-4 companies simultaneously.

**The economics:**
- Fractional executives typically earn $5,000-$15,000 per month per client
- With 3 clients: $15,000-$45,000/month ($180K-$540K/year)
- Engagements typically last 6-18 months
- You provide strategic direction, build teams, and set systems. You do not manage daily operations.

**Who hires fractional leaders:**
- Startups that need C-level expertise but cannot afford a full-time C-suite
- Small businesses (10-100 employees) going through growth or transition
- Companies in between full-time hires
- Private equity portfolio companies that need temporary leadership

### Getting Started
1. Define your fractional title (Fractional CFO, Fractional CMO, etc.)
2. Update your LinkedIn headline and About section to include "Fractional [Title]"
3. Join fractional leadership communities and marketplaces (Toptal, On Deck, Chief of Staff Network, various Slack groups)
4. Reach out to startup founders, PE firms, and small business owners in your network
5. Create a simple one-page overview of what you offer, how you work, and your rate

### Risks
- Requires executive-level experience and credibility
- Client acquisition is slow at first
- Context-switching between multiple companies is mentally demanding
- Scope creep (clients want full-time effort at part-time rates)

## Model 3: Portfolio Career

### What It Is
Combining multiple income streams from different types of work: some consulting, some teaching, some writing, some board work, some part-time employment.

### Who It Is Best For
- People with diverse interests who get bored doing one thing
- People who want maximum flexibility and variety
- Semi-retired professionals who want to stay active
- People who value time freedom over income maximization

### How It Works

**Example portfolio:**
- Consulting for 2 clients (20 hours/week): $8,000/month
- Teaching one university course per semester: $1,500/month (averaged)
- Advisory board seat: $2,000/month
- Writing (blog, newsletter, or freelance): $500/month
- Total: $12,000/month with significant schedule flexibility

**The key:** no single income stream needs to replace your full-time salary. The combination does.

### Building Your Portfolio
1. Start with your highest-value skill (usually consulting or fractional work) as your anchor income
2. Add one adjacent activity that interests you (teaching, writing, speaking, advising)
3. Grow each stream gradually. Do not try to launch 5 income streams at once.
4. Evaluate every 6 months: which streams energize you and which drain you? Adjust.

### Risks
- Multiple income streams require multiple sets of relationships and commitments
- Benefits and retirement are entirely self-funded
- Tax complexity increases significantly
- It can feel scattered if you do not have a unifying theme

## Model 4: Starting a Business at 40+

### What It Is
Building a company based on your expertise, network, and domain knowledge.

### Why 40+ Is Actually the Best Time

The data contradicts the Silicon Valley narrative that entrepreneurs must be young:
- The average age of a successful startup founder is 45 (MIT study)
- Founders in their 50s are almost 2x more likely to build a high-growth company than founders in their 20s
- Domain expertise, network, and judgment (all of which increase with age) are the strongest predictors of startup success

### Business Models That Fit Experienced Professionals

| Model | Description | Capital Needed | Risk Level |
|-------|-------------|---------------|-----------|
| **Service business** | Consulting/agency built around your expertise | Low ($0-$5K) | Low |
| **Digital product** | Online course, template library, SaaS tool | Low-Medium ($2K-$20K) | Medium |
| **Franchise** | Proven business model you operate locally | High ($50K-$500K) | Medium |
| **E-commerce** | Physical or digital products sold online | Medium ($5K-$50K) | Medium |
| **Advisory/coaching** | 1:1 or group coaching in your domain | Low ($0-$5K) | Low |
| **Acquisition** | Buy an existing small business and improve it | High ($100K+) | Medium-High |

### The Lowest-Risk Path

1. Start as a side project while still employed
2. Validate demand before investing money (can you get 3 paying clients?)
3. Build to replacement income before leaving your job
4. Reinvest profits, not savings
5. Give yourself 12-18 months to reach profitability

### Risks
- Financial risk, especially for capital-intensive models
- Loneliness and uncertainty
- No guaranteed income, benefits, or structure
- The learning curve for sales, marketing, and operations is steep

## The Decision Framework

| Factor | Full-Time Employee | Consulting | Fractional | Portfolio | Business |
|--------|-------------------|-----------|-----------|-----------|---------|
| Income stability | High | Medium | Medium | Low-Medium | Low (initially) |
| Income ceiling | Medium | High | High | Medium | Very High |
| Flexibility | Low | High | Medium-High | Very High | Medium |
| Benefits | Included | Self-funded | Self-funded | Self-funded | Self-funded |
| Social structure | Built-in | Create your own | Partial | Create your own | Create your own |
| Risk level | Low | Medium | Medium | Medium | High |
| Autonomy | Low-Medium | High | High | Very High | Very High |

### Which Path Fits You?

**Choose full-time employment if:** You value stability, benefits, and social structure. You want to focus on the work, not the business side.

**Choose consulting if:** You have deep expertise, a strong network, and the ability to sell yourself. You want higher income and more autonomy.

**Choose fractional if:** You are at the executive level and want to work strategically across multiple companies without the politics of a full-time C-suite role.

**Choose a portfolio career if:** You value variety and flexibility above all else. You have multiple interests and income does not need to be maximized.

**Choose starting a business if:** You have an idea, the financial runway, the risk tolerance, and the energy to build something from scratch.

---

## Key Takeaway

After 40, you have options that 25-year-olds do not. Your experience, network, and reputation are valuable assets that can be deployed in multiple ways. Full-time employment is one option, not the only option.

Explore the models. Test the ones that appeal to you. And remember: the best "second act" is the one designed around how you actually want to live, not just how you have always worked.
