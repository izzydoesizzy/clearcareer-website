# Non-Compete Audit Checklist

Figure out which restrictive covenants in your employment contract actually apply to you.

> **Disclaimer:** This is general legal education, not legal advice. Enforceability depends on your specific clause language, circumstances, and province. Do not disregard any clause without consulting an employment lawyer first.

You don't need to do everything today. Work through this at your own pace.

**Time estimate:** 1 hour to audit your agreements, 30-60 minutes with a lawyer if needed
**What you'll need:** Every document you signed when hired, promoted, or given equity, plus your severance/termination agreement

**Top 3 actions from this guide:**
1. Find your original employment contract and any amendments you signed during your tenure.
2. Identify which restrictive clauses you have: non-compete, non-solicit, NDA, IP assignment, or garden leave.
3. Check if Ontario's non-compete ban applies to you. If you're not C-suite in Ontario, your non-compete is likely unenforceable.

---

## The Big News: Ontario Banned Non-Competes

As of October 25, 2021, non-competition clauses are **banned** in Ontario for most employees under the Working for Workers Act.

**Exceptions (non-competes may still apply):**
- C-suite executives (CEO, CFO, COO, etc.)
- Sale of business (non-compete was part of an acquisition)

**Note:** Employees with fiduciary duties (typically senior officers and directors) may also face non-compete restrictions under common law, separate from the statutory ban.

Contracts signed before October 2021 are not covered by the ban but may still be unenforceable under common-law reasonableness tests. Some legal debate exists on transitional cases, so consult a lawyer if unsure.

**Action:** If you work in Ontario and aren't C-suite, your non-compete is likely unenforceable. Get a lawyer to confirm for your specific contract.

## Province-by-Province Enforceability

| Province | Non-Competes | Key Detail |
|----------|-------------|------------|
| **Ontario** | Banned for most employees | Only enforceable for C-suite or sale of business |
| **BC** | Enforceable if reasonable | Courts require narrow scope, 6-12 months typical, geographic limits |
| **Alberta** | Enforceable if reasonable | Similar to BC, courts scrutinize broad clauses |
| **Quebec** | Enforceable if reasonable | Must be written, limited in time/scope, protect legitimate interests |
| **Federally Regulated** | Varies | No specific statute. Common-law reasonableness applies. |
| **Other provinces** | Generally enforceable if reasonable | All provinces apply a reasonableness test |

**"Reasonable" means:**
- **Time:** 6-12 months is typically reasonable. 2+ years often gets struck down.
- **Geography:** City or region is reasonable. "Worldwide" is frequently challenged.
- **Activity:** Specific competitors or client segments is reasonable. "Any business in the industry" is too broad.

**Action:** Identify your province and check the table above for the general enforceability landscape.

## Step 1: Find Your Agreements

Gather every document you signed. Check for:

- [ ] Original employment agreement / offer letter
- [ ] Amendments or addendums signed later
- [ ] Equity / stock option agreements (often contain separate restrictive covenants)
- [ ] Severance / termination agreement (may add new restrictions as a condition of the package)
- [ ] Independent contractor agreements (if any period was contractor status)

If you don't have copies, request them from your employer. In most cases, employers will provide copies of signed agreements. If they refuse, consult a lawyer.

**Action:** Collect all agreements into one folder before moving to Step 2.

---

## Step 2: Identify and Mark Each Clause

Go through your agreements and flag these five types:

**Non-Competition:** You can't work for a competitor for X months after leaving. Typical scope: 6-24 months, within a geographic area, in a specific industry. Banned in Ontario for most employees.

**Non-Solicitation:** You can't actively recruit former colleagues or contact former clients for X months. Typically 12-24 months. More likely to be enforced than non-competes because they're narrower. Important: non-solicits prevent your outreach, not someone else contacting you.

**Confidentiality / NDA:** You can't disclose or use confidential information, trade secrets, or proprietary knowledge. Usually indefinite. Almost always enforceable.

**IP Assignment:** Anything you created during employment belongs to the employer. Work created on company time with company tools is theirs. Side projects on your own time, with your own tools, unrelated to the employer's business, are typically yours (but check your specific agreement). Check your specific IP assignment clause. Some contracts claim broader rights than standard.

**Garden Leave:** During the notice period, you remain employed and paid but don't work. You can't start a new job until it ends.

**Action:** Highlight every restrictive clause in your agreements and note which type each one is.

---

## Step 3: Assess Your Risk

For each restrictive clause, evaluate these factors:

| Factor | Low Risk | Medium Risk | High Risk |
|--------|----------|-------------|-----------|
| Enforcement history | Employer has never enforced | Unknown | Employer has enforced against others |
| Specificity | Clause is vague or overly broad | Moderate | Narrow and well-drafted |
| Your role | Junior/mid-level | Senior | Executive with trade secret access |
| Relationship | Amicable departure | Neutral | Contentious departure |
| Consideration | No extra compensation for signing | Standard in offer | Specific bonus/payment for signing |

Low risk across the board? You're probably fine. Monitor but don't stress.

High risk on multiple factors? Get a lawyer's opinion before joining a competitor or soliciting former clients.

**Action:** Score each clause using this table. Any clause with 2+ high-risk factors warrants legal advice.

## Step 4: Negotiate Clauses During Severance

If you're negotiating severance, you can ask for modifications:

```
Full release:
"I'd like the non-competition clause waived as part of the
separation agreement."

Narrow the scope:
"Can we limit the non-solicit to direct clients I personally
managed, rather than all company clients?"

Shorten the duration:
"Can we reduce the restriction from 24 months to 12 months?"

Carve-out:
"I'd like a carve-out that allows me to work for [specific company]
or in [specific sector]."
```

Employers often agree because enforcing restrictive covenants is expensive and uncertain. Trading a narrower clause for a signed release is usually a good deal for them.

**Action:** Add restrictive covenant modifications to your severance negotiation checklist.

---

## Step 5: Know What You Can and Can't Do

### You CAN:
- Accept a job at a competitor (if your non-compete is unenforceable or waived)
- Accept inbound contact from former colleagues (non-solicit restricts your outreach, not theirs)
- Use general skills, knowledge, and experience from your career
- Keep your LinkedIn connections (they're your relationships). Note: courts have not fully settled whether LinkedIn connections built during employment belong to the employee. Avoid downloading your connection list for competitive use.
- Discuss salary, working conditions, and general industry knowledge

### You CANNOT:
- Share trade secrets, proprietary data, or confidential information
- Copy client lists, pricing databases, or internal documents
- Actively recruit former team members during the non-solicit period
- Use proprietary tools, processes, or templates you didn't create
- Represent yourself as still affiliated with your former employer

**Action:** Review this list before your first day at a new employer.

---

## When to Get Legal Advice

Consult an employment lawyer if:
- Your non-compete was well-drafted and specific
- You're considering joining a direct competitor
- You have access to significant trade secrets or client relationships
- Your departure was contentious
- Your new employer asks you to sign a rep that you have no restrictive covenants

A 30-60 minute consultation costs $200-$500 and can save you from an expensive legal fight.

**Action:** If any of the above apply, book a consultation before accepting your next role.

---

## Key Takeaway

In Ontario, non-competes have been legislatively restricted for most employees since 2021. In other provinces, courts apply a fact-specific reasonableness test. Non-solicits and confidentiality agreements generally carry more weight than non-competes. Know what you signed, understand the landscape in your province, and consult an employment lawyer before making decisions about your next role.
