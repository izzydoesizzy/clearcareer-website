# Non-Compete Audit Checklist

**Understanding restrictive covenants in your employment contract.**

> **Important:** This guide provides general educational information about non-compete and restrictive covenant law in Canada. It is not legal advice. Enforceability depends on the specific language of your clause, your circumstances, and your province. **Do not disregard any clause in your contract without consulting an employment lawyer first.** Courts apply fact-specific tests and outcomes are not predictable from general rules alone.

Many employment contracts include restrictive covenants: non-competes, non-solicits, confidentiality agreements, and IP assignments. After a layoff, you need to know which ones actually apply to you and which ones you can safely disregard.

---

## The Big News: Ontario Banned Non-Competes

As of October 25, 2021, non-competition clauses are **banned** in Ontario for most employees under the Working for Workers Act. If you work in Ontario and you're not a C-suite executive, your non-compete may not be enforceable under this legislation. Have an employment lawyer confirm this for your specific situation.

**Exceptions (non-competes may still apply):**
- C-suite executives (CEO, CFO, COO, etc.)
- Sale of business (if the non-compete was part of a business acquisition)
- Certain professionals with fiduciary duties

**If you signed a non-compete before October 2021 in Ontario:** It's still likely unenforceable. The legislation applies regardless of when the contract was signed, though there's some legal debate on transitional cases. Consult a lawyer if unsure.

---

## Province-by-Province: Non-Compete Enforceability

| Province | Non-Competes | Notes |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| **Ontario** | Banned for most employees (since Oct 2021) | Only enforceable for C-suite or sale of business |
| **BC** | Enforceable if reasonable | Courts require narrow scope, reasonable duration (6-12 months typical), and geographic limits |
| **Alberta** | Enforceable if reasonable | Similar to BC. Courts scrutinize broadly-worded clauses |
| **Quebec** | Enforceable if reasonable | Must be in writing, limited in time and scope, and necessary to protect legitimate interests |
| **Federal** | Varies | Depends on the specific federal labour regulations |
| **Other provinces** | Generally enforceable if reasonable | Courts in all provinces apply a reasonableness test |

**"Reasonable" means:**
- Time limit: 6-12 months is typically reasonable. 2+ years is often struck down.
- Geographic scope: City or region is reasonable. "Worldwide" scope is frequently challenged and often not upheld.
- Activity scope: Specific competitors or client segments is reasonable. "Any business in the industry" is too broad.

## Step 1: Find Your Agreements

Gather every document you signed when you were hired, promoted, or received equity. Look for:

- [ ] Original employment agreement / offer letter
- [ ] Any amendments or addendums signed later
- [ ] Equity / stock option agreements (often contain separate restrictive covenants)
- [ ] Severance / termination agreement (may contain new restrictions as a condition of the package)
- [ ] Independent contractor agreements (if any period was contractor status)

If you don't have copies, you can request them from your employer. They're required to provide them.

---

## Step 2: Identify the Clauses

Mark each clause in your agreements:

### Non-Competition Clause
- **What it says:** You can't work for a competitor or in a competing business for X months after leaving
- **Typical scope:** 6-24 months, within a geographic area, in a specific industry
- **Enforceability:** Banned in Ontario (most employees). Enforceable elsewhere if reasonable.

### Non-Solicitation Clause
- **What it says:** You can't actively recruit former colleagues or contact former clients/customers for X months
- **Typical scope:** 12-24 months
- **Enforceability:** More likely to be enforced than non-competes because they're narrower in scope
- **Important distinction:** Non-solicits prevent you from *actively reaching out to* specific people. They don't prevent those people from contacting *you* or responding to job postings.

### Confidentiality / NDA
- **What it says:** You can't disclose or use confidential information, trade secrets, or proprietary knowledge
- **Typical scope:** Usually indefinite (no time limit)
- **Enforceability:** Almost always enforceable. Don't share trade secrets, client lists, pricing data, or proprietary processes.

### IP Assignment
- **What it says:** Anything you created during employment belongs to the employer
- **Typical scope:** Work created during employment, using company resources, or related to the company's business
- **Enforceability:** Standard and enforceable. Anything you built on company time with company tools is theirs.
- **Exception:** Side projects created on your own time, with your own tools, unrelated to the employer's business, are typically yours. But check your specific agreement.

### Garden Leave
- **What it says:** During the notice period, you remain employed (and paid) but are not required to work
- **What it means:** You can't start a new job until the garden leave period ends, but you're still getting paid
- **How it helps you:** It's essentially paid severance without calling it that

---

## Step 3: Assess Your Risk

For each restrictive clause, evaluate:

| Factor | Low Risk | Medium Risk | High Risk |
|--------|----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Enforcement history** | Employer has never enforced | Unknown | Employer has enforced against others |
| **Specificity** | Clause is vague or overly broad | Moderate specificity | Narrow and well-drafted |
| **Your role** | Junior/mid-level | Senior | Executive with access to trade secrets |
| **Relationship** | Amicable departure | Neutral | Contentious departure |
| **Consideration** | No extra compensation for the clause | Standard in offer | Specific bonus/payment for signing |

**Low risk across the board?** You're probably fine to proceed as planned. Monitor but don't stress.

**High risk on multiple factors?** Get a lawyer's opinion before starting at a competitor or soliciting former clients.

---

## Step 4: Negotiate Clauses During Severance

If you're negotiating severance, you can ask for:

1. **Full release from non-compete obligations**
   > "I'd like the non-competition clause waived as part of the separation agreement."

2. **Narrowing the scope**
   > "Can we limit the non-solicit to direct clients I personally managed, rather than all company clients?"

3. **Shortening the duration**
   > "Can we reduce the restriction from 24 months to 12 months?"

4. **Explicit carve-outs**
   > "I'd like a carve-out that allows me to work for [specific company] or in [specific sector]."

Employers often agree to these requests because enforcing restrictive covenants is expensive and uncertain. Trading a narrower clause for a signed release is usually a good deal for them.

---

## Step 5: What You Can and Can't Do

### You CAN:
- Accept a job at a competitor (if your non-compete is unenforceable or has been waived)
- Accept inbound contact from former colleagues (non-solicit restricts *your* outreach, not theirs)
- Use general skills, knowledge, and experience gained during employment
- Take your personal LinkedIn connections with you (they're your relationships)
- Discuss your salary, working conditions, and general industry knowledge

### You CANNOT:
- Share trade secrets, proprietary data, or confidential information
- Copy client lists, pricing databases, or internal documents
- Actively recruit former team members (during non-solicit period)
- Use proprietary tools, processes, or templates you didn't create
- Represent yourself as still affiliated with your former employer

---

## When to Get Legal Advice

Consult a lawyer if:
- Your non-compete was well-drafted and specific
- You're considering joining a direct competitor
- You have access to significant trade secrets or client relationships
- Your departure was contentious
- Your new employer asks you to sign a representation that you have no restrictive covenants

A 30-60 minute consultation costs $200-$500 and can save you from an expensive legal fight.

---

## Key Takeaway

Restrictive covenants are a complex area of law. In Ontario, non-competes have been legislatively restricted for most employees since 2021. In other provinces, courts apply a reasonableness test that is highly fact-specific. Non-solicits and confidentiality agreements generally carry more weight. Know what you signed, understand the general landscape, and consult an employment lawyer before making decisions about your next role. Do not disregard any clause without legal advice.
