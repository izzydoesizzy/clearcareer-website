# Salary Negotiation Scripts

Word-for-word scripts for negotiating your next offer, by email and by phone.

> **Disclaimer:** These scripts are starting points you can adapt. Negotiation outcomes depend on the employer, market conditions, and individual factors. No specific outcome is guaranteed.

You don't need to do everything today. Work through this at your own pace.

You may not need this guide for weeks or months. Read it now so you're ready when the offer comes. Bookmark it.

If you're feeling pressure to just take whatever comes, that's normal. These scripts exist so you don't have to wing it when you're stressed.

**Time estimate:** 20 minutes to read, then reference as needed
**What you'll need:** Market salary data for your target role (Glassdoor, Robert Half, Indeed, Hays), your walk-away number, and your target number

**Top 3 actions from this guide:**
1. Never name a number first. Deflect with "What's the range you've budgeted for this position?"
2. When you get a verbal offer, ask for it in writing before responding.
3. Use the 4-step counter-offer framework: express enthusiasm, identify the gap, make a specific ask, then offer flexibility.

---

## Rule 1: Never Name a Number First

The person who names a number first loses leverage. When asked "What are your salary expectations?" use one of these:

```
Deflect (early in process):
"I'm flexible on compensation and more focused on finding the right fit
at this stage. I'd love to learn more about the role before we discuss
numbers. What's the range you've budgeted for this position?"

If they insist:
"Based on my research for similar roles in [city], the market range is
$[X] to $[Y]. I'd expect something in that range, but I'm open to
discussing the full package once we're further along."

If you must give a number:
"Based on my experience and the scope of this role, I'd be targeting
$[target, 10-15% above your minimum]. But I'm open to discussing the
total package including benefits, equity, and flexibility."
```

**Action:** Decide your walk-away number and target number before any compensation conversation.

---

## Rule 2: Get the Offer in Writing First

When you receive a verbal offer:

```
"Thank you so much. I'm really excited about this opportunity. Could you
send me the full offer details in writing so I can review everything
carefully? I want to give it the attention it deserves."
```

This buys you time and ensures you have the complete picture.

**Action:** Never respond to a verbal offer on the spot. Always ask for it in writing.

## The Counter-Offer Framework

Four steps. Use them in order.

**Step 1: Express Enthusiasm**

```
"I really appreciate this offer, and I'm genuinely excited about the
role and the team. This is exactly the kind of work I want to be doing."
```

**Step 2: Identify the Gap**

```
"After reviewing the full package, there are a couple of areas I'd
like to discuss to make sure we're aligned."
```

**Step 3: Make Your Ask (Be Specific)**

```
"Based on the scope of the role and my [X years of experience in Y],
I was hoping we could get closer to $[target]. I believe that reflects
the value I'd bring, especially given [specific value you add]."
```

**Step 4: Offer Flexibility**

```
"I understand there may be constraints on base salary. I'm open to
discussing other ways to bridge the gap -- signing bonus, additional
vacation, equity, or a performance review at 6 months with a defined
path to $[target]."
```

**Action:** Practice saying these four steps out loud before any negotiation call.

## Counter-Offer by Email

```
Subject: Re: Offer for [Role Title]

Hi [Hiring Manager],

Thank you for the offer. I'm very excited about joining [Company]
and contributing to [specific project or goal you discussed].

I've reviewed the package carefully and would like to discuss a
couple of points:

Base salary: The offer of $[X] is below my expectations based on
market research for comparable roles in [city]. I was hoping we
could get closer to $[target], which reflects the scope of the role
and the experience I'd bring. [Optional: "I'm basing this on data
from Glassdoor/Robert Half/Indeed/Hays."]

[Optional second ask]: I'd also like to discuss [signing bonus /
additional vacation / equity / remote flexibility / professional
development budget].

I want to make this work for both sides. I'm flexible and open to
creative solutions. Would you be available for a quick call to discuss?

Looking forward to working together.

Best,
[Your name]
```

**Action:** Customize this template for your specific offer before sending.

## Counter-Offer by Phone

```
You: "Thanks so much for sending the offer over. I've reviewed
everything and I'm really excited about the opportunity. There are
just a couple of things I'd like to discuss."

Them: "Sure, go ahead."

You: "The base salary of $[X] is a bit below what I was expecting
based on my research and experience. I was hoping we could get closer
to $[target]. Given my background in [specific experience] and the
scope of this role, I believe that's a fair number. Is there
flexibility there?"

[If yes:] "That's great. I appreciate you working with me on that."

[If the number is firm:] "I understand. Would you be open to
discussing other parts of the package? A signing bonus of $[X], or
an additional week of vacation? I want to make sure the total package
reflects the value I'll bring."

[If everything is firm:] "I understand there are constraints. If we
can agree to a 6-month performance review with a defined salary
adjustment to $[target] based on hitting [specific goals], I'd be
comfortable accepting at the current number."
```

**Action:** Write down your talking points and keep them visible during the call.

## Scripts by Scenario

**Below market offer:**
```
"I'm very interested in this role. The offer of $[X] is below the
market range I'm seeing for similar roles in [city], which is $[range].
Could we discuss getting closer to $[mid-point of range]?"
```

**You have another offer (only use if true -- never fabricate a competing offer).** Employers talk. If you're caught bluffing, the offer will likely be rescinded.
```
"I want to be transparent. I've received another offer at $[X]. I'd
prefer to join [Company] because [genuine reason], but I need the
compensation to be competitive. Is there room to match or come close?"
```

**Equity negotiation (startups/tech):**
```
"I understand the base salary may be constrained at this stage. Would
you be open to discussing equity? I'd be interested in [X shares / X%
/ additional RSUs] as part of the package, with a standard 4-year
vesting schedule."
```

**Remote work negotiation:**
```
"I noticed the role is listed as hybrid. I've been working remotely
for [X years] and am highly effective in that setup. Would you be
open to fully remote, or at minimum, a 1-day-in-office schedule?"
```

**Action:** Identify which scenario matches your situation and rehearse the matching script.

## Common Objections and Responses

| What They Say | What You Say |
|--------------|-------------|
| "This is our standard offer for this level." | "I understand. My experience goes beyond the standard profile for this level. Given [specific experience], I believe an adjustment is warranted." |
| "We can't go higher on base salary." | "I understand. Can we explore a signing bonus, additional equity, or a performance-based increase at 6 months?" |
| "We need to be fair to other team members." | "I respect that. I'm not asking for anything outside market range. I'm confident the value I'll bring justifies this." |
| "We'll review comp after your first year." | "I appreciate that. Could we formalize it with a guaranteed review at 6 months with a defined salary target of $[X] contingent on performance?" |
| "Take it or leave it." | "I understand. Can I have 48 hours to make my final decision?" (Then decide based on your walk-away number.) |

**Action:** Read through these objections and practice your responses before any negotiation.

## Negotiating Beyond Salary

If base salary is truly fixed, negotiate these instead:

| Item | Script |
|------|--------|
| Signing bonus | "Would a signing bonus of $[3-10K or 5-10% of base salary] be possible to bridge the gap?" |
| Vacation | "I currently have [X] weeks. Could we match that or add an additional week?" |
| Start date | "Could I start [2 weeks later] to wrap up personal commitments?" |
| Remote work | "Would [2-3 days remote] be available for this role?" |
| Professional development | "Is there a budget I could access for [specific certification]?" |
| Title | "Would you be open to [Senior/Lead/Director]? It better reflects the scope." |
| Performance review timing | "Could we schedule a formal review at 6 months with a defined compensation path?" |

**Action:** Rank these items by what matters most to you. Lead with your top priority.

## After You Agree

1. **Get it in writing.** If you're currently employed elsewhere, do not give notice until you have a signed offer letter.
2. **Read the fine print.** Check non-compete, non-solicit, IP assignment, and probation clauses.
3. **Confirm start date, benefits start date, and any conditions** (background check, references).
4. **Say thank you.** "I'm thrilled to accept. Looking forward to getting started."

**Action:** Do not sign anything until you've read every clause, especially restrictive covenants (see Guide 09).

## Canadian Pay Transparency

In some provinces (BC, PEI, others pending), employers must disclose salary ranges in job postings. If a range is listed, use it as your anchor. Reference the posted range directly in your negotiation: "I noticed the posted range is $X to $Y. Based on my experience, I believe I'm at the upper end of that range."

---

## Key Takeaway

Negotiation is not confrontation. It's a conversation about fair value. Employers expect you to negotiate. The fact that you do signals confidence and professionalism. These scripts give you the exact words so you don't have to improvise under pressure.
