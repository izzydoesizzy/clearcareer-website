# Salary Negotiation Scripts

**Word-for-word scripts for negotiating your next offer. By email and by phone.**

> **Note:** These scripts are starting points you can adapt to your situation. Negotiation outcomes depend on the employer, market conditions, and many individual factors. No specific outcome is guaranteed.

After a layoff, the temptation is to accept the first offer that comes along. The offer you accept sets your compensation baseline going forward. Starting salary differences compound over time. These scripts give you the exact words to use.

---

## Rule 1: Never Name a Number First

The person who names a number first loses negotiation leverage. When asked "What are your salary expectations?" you have three options:

### Script: Deflecting the Salary Question (Early in Process)

> "I'm flexible on compensation and more focused on finding the right fit at this stage. I'd love to learn more about the role and team before we discuss numbers. What's the range you've budgeted for this position?"

### Script: If They Insist

> "Based on my research for similar roles in [city], the market range is $[X] to $[Y]. I'd expect something in that range, but I'm open to discussing the full compensation package once we're further along."

### Script: If You Have to Give a Number

> "Based on my experience and the scope of this role, I'd be targeting $[your target number, which should be 10-15% above your minimum]. But I'm open to discussing the total package, including benefits, equity, and flexibility."

---

## Rule 2: Always Get the Offer in Writing Before Responding

When you receive a verbal offer:

> "Thank you so much. I'm really excited about this opportunity. Could you send me the full offer details in writing so I can review everything carefully? I want to give it the attention it deserves."

This buys you time and ensures you have the complete picture before responding.

## The Counter-Offer Framework

### Step 1: Express Enthusiasm (Don't Negotiate From a Cold Start)

> "I really appreciate this offer, and I'm genuinely excited about the role and the team. This is exactly the kind of work I want to be doing."

### Step 2: Identify the Gap

> "After reviewing the full package, there are a couple of areas I'd like to discuss to make sure we're aligned."

### Step 3: Make Your Ask (Be Specific)

> "Based on the scope of the role and my [X years of experience in Y], I was hoping we could get closer to $[target]. I believe that reflects the value I'd bring to the team, especially given [specific value you add]."

### Step 4: Offer Flexibility

> "I understand there may be constraints on base salary. I'm open to discussing other ways to bridge the gap, whether that's a signing bonus, additional vacation, equity, or a performance review at 6 months with a defined path to $[target]."

## Script: Counter-Offer by Email

**Subject: Re: Offer for [Role Title]**

Hi [Hiring Manager],

Thank you for the offer. I'm very excited about joining [Company] and contributing to [specific project or goal you discussed].

I've reviewed the package carefully, and I'd like to discuss a couple of points:

**Base salary:** The offer of $[X] is below my expectations based on market research for comparable roles in [city]. I was hoping we could get closer to $[target], which I believe reflects the scope of the role and the experience I'd bring. [Optional: "I'm basing this on data from [source: Glassdoor, Robert Half, Indeed, Hays]."]

**[Optional second ask]:** I'd also like to discuss [signing bonus / additional vacation / equity / remote flexibility / professional development budget].

I want to make this work for both sides. I'm flexible and open to creative solutions. Would you be available for a quick call to discuss?

Looking forward to working together.

Best,
[Your name]

## Script: Counter-Offer by Phone

> **You:** "Thanks so much for sending the offer over. I've had a chance to review everything, and I'm really excited about the opportunity. There are just a couple of things I'd like to discuss."
>
> **Them:** "Sure, go ahead."
>
> **You:** "The base salary of $[X] is a bit below what I was expecting based on my research and experience. I was hoping we could get closer to $[target]. Given my background in [specific experience] and the scope of this role, I believe that's a fair number. Is there flexibility there?"
>
> **[If they say yes:]** "That's great. I appreciate you working with me on that."
>
> **[If they say the number is firm:]** "I understand. Would you be open to discussing other parts of the package? For example, a signing bonus of $[X], or an additional week of vacation? I want to make sure the total package reflects the value I'll bring."
>
> **[If they say everything is firm:]** "I understand there are constraints. If we can agree to a 6-month performance review with a defined salary adjustment to $[target] based on hitting [specific goals], I'd be comfortable accepting at the current number."

## Scripts by Scenario

### Scenario: They Offer Below Market

> "I'm very interested in this role. The offer of $[X] is below the market range I'm seeing for similar roles in [city], which is $[range]. Could we discuss getting closer to $[mid-point of range]?"

### Scenario: You Have Another Offer

> "I want to be transparent. I've received another offer that's at $[X]. I'd prefer to join [Company] because [genuine reason], but I need the compensation to be competitive. Is there room to match or come close to that number?"

**Only use this if you actually have another offer.** Bluffing about competing offers is risky and can backfire.

### Scenario: Internal Transfer / Promotion (Re-Entry)

> "I'm excited about this new role. Given the expanded scope and responsibilities compared to my previous position, I'd like to discuss a compensation adjustment to $[target] to reflect the change in level."

### Scenario: Equity Negotiation (Startups/Tech)

> "I understand the base salary may be constrained at this stage. Would you be open to discussing equity? I'd be interested in [X shares / X% / additional RSUs] as part of the package, with a standard 4-year vesting schedule."

### Scenario: Remote Work Negotiation

> "I noticed the role is listed as hybrid. I've been working remotely for [X years] and am highly effective in that setup. Would you be open to a fully remote arrangement, or at minimum, a 1-day-in-office schedule?"

## Common Objections and Responses

| What They Say | What You Say |
|--------------|-------------|
| "This is our standard offer for this level." | "I understand. My experience goes beyond the standard profile for this level. Given [specific experience], I believe an adjustment is warranted." |
| "We can't go higher on base salary." | "I understand. Can we explore a signing bonus, additional equity, or a performance-based increase at 6 months?" |
| "We need to be fair to other team members." | "I respect that. I'm not asking for anything outside of market range. I'm confident the value I'll bring justifies this investment." |
| "We'll review comp after your first year." | "I appreciate that. Could we formalize that with a guaranteed review at 6 months with a defined salary target of $[X] contingent on performance?" |
| "Take it or leave it." | "I understand. Can I have 48 hours to make my final decision?" (Then decide based on your walk-away number.) |

## Negotiating Beyond Salary

If base salary is truly fixed, negotiate these:

| Item | How to Ask |
|------|-----------|
| Signing bonus | "Would a signing bonus of $[5-15K] be possible to help bridge the gap?" |
| Vacation | "I currently have [X] weeks. Could we match that or add an additional week?" |
| Start date | "Could I start [2 weeks later than proposed] to wrap up some personal commitments?" |
| Remote work | "Would [2-3 days remote] be available for this role?" |
| Professional development | "Is there a professional development budget I could access for [specific certification]?" |
| Title | "Would you be open to [Senior/Lead/Director] as the title? It better reflects the scope." |
| Performance review timing | "Could we schedule a formal review at 6 months with a defined compensation adjustment path?" |

## After You Agree

Once you reach agreement:

1. **Get it in writing.** Do not give notice at your current job (if applicable) until you have a signed offer letter.
2. **Read the fine print.** Check the non-compete, non-solicit, IP assignment, and probation clauses.
3. **Confirm the start date, benefits start date, and any conditions** (background check, references).
4. **Say thank you.** A simple email: "I'm thrilled to accept. Looking forward to getting started."

---

## Key Takeaway

Negotiation is not confrontation. It's a conversation about fair value. Employers expect you to negotiate. The fact that you negotiate signals confidence, self-awareness, and professionalism. The scripts in this guide give you the exact words so you don't have to improvise under pressure.
