# Reference Strategy Guide

**How to secure strong references after a layoff, even if your departure wasn't on great terms.**

> **Note:** This guide provides general career advice. Reference practices vary by industry and employer. Legal claims mentioned (such as defamation) are general information, not legal advice. Consult a lawyer for guidance specific to your situation.

References can make or break a job offer. A lukewarm reference kills momentum. A strong one seals the deal. After a layoff, you need to be proactive and strategic about who speaks for you.

---

## Step 1: Build Your Reference List (Before You Need It)

You need 3-5 references. Aim for a mix:

| Type | Who | Why |
|------|-----|-----|
| **Direct manager** | Your most recent boss (or the one before) | Validates your day-to-day performance |
| **Senior leader** | Someone 2+ levels above you | Validates your strategic impact |
| **Peer/colleague** | Someone you worked closely with | Validates collaboration and character |
| **External stakeholder** | Client, vendor, or partner | Validates your professional reputation |
| **Former manager** | A boss from a previous job | Provides a longer track record |

---

## Step 2: Ask the Right Way

### Script: Asking a Former Manager

> "Hi [Name], I hope you're doing well. I wanted to let you know that I'm currently exploring new opportunities after a restructuring at [Company]. I really valued working with you, and I was wondering if you'd be comfortable serving as a professional reference for me. I'd be happy to share what kinds of roles I'm targeting so you can speak to the most relevant parts of our work together."

### Script: Asking a Colleague

> "Hey [Name], I'm starting my job search and wanted to ask if you'd be willing to be a reference. I always appreciated how we worked together on [specific project], and I think you could speak to [specific skill or quality]. Let me know if you're comfortable with that."

### Script: If Your Manager Was Part of the Problem

If your departure was contentious or your manager wouldn't give a good reference:

> Skip them. Use your manager's manager, a peer, or a stakeholder from a different department instead. You are not obligated to provide your most recent direct supervisor as a reference.

---

## Step 3: Prep Your References

Don't just ask and walk away. Set your references up for success.

**Send each reference:**
1. The job description or role summary for positions you're applying to
2. 3-4 bullet points about what you'd like them to emphasize
3. A reminder of specific projects or accomplishments you worked on together

### Template: Reference Prep Email

> Hi [Name],
>
> Thank you for agreeing to be a reference. I wanted to share a quick update on my search and a few things that might be helpful if you get a call.
>
> I'm targeting [type of role] in [industry/function]. The key things I'm hoping you can speak to are:
>
> - My work on [specific project] and the results we achieved
> - My ability to [specific skill: lead teams, manage stakeholders, solve problems]
> - My character and how I show up as a colleague
>
> Here's a link to one of the roles I'm in the running for: [job posting URL]
>
> If there's anything else you'd find helpful, let me know. I really appreciate your support.

## Step 4: Negotiate References Into Your Severance

This is one of the most underused severance negotiation strategies. When negotiating your package, explicitly ask for:

1. **A written reference letter** from your manager or HR
2. **Agreed-upon reference language** that your company will use if contacted
3. **A commitment that the company will not provide negative references**

### Script for Severance Negotiation

> "As part of the severance agreement, I'd like to include a written reference letter from [manager name] and a commitment that any reference inquiries to the company will be handled with the agreed-upon language we discuss."

Most companies will agree to this because it protects them too (limits their liability for what's said in reference checks).

---

## Step 5: LinkedIn Recommendations

LinkedIn recommendations are visible, permanent, and controllable. They're not a substitute for phone references, but they strengthen your profile and provide social proof.

### Script: Requesting a LinkedIn Recommendation

> "Hi [Name], I'm updating my LinkedIn as part of my job search. Would you be open to writing a short recommendation? Even 2-3 sentences about our work on [project] or my strengths in [area] would be really helpful. Happy to return the favour anytime."

### What Makes a Good LinkedIn Recommendation

A good recommendation is:
- **Specific:** Mentions a project, skill, or result (not just "great to work with")
- **Short:** 3-5 sentences. Nobody reads long ones.
- **Credible:** Includes the recommender's relationship to you ("I managed Sarah for 3 years")

---

## Step 6: Handle Negative References

If you suspect a former employer might give a negative reference:

### Option 1: Preemptive Framing
Tell potential employers about the situation upfront (briefly, without drama):
> "I should mention that my departure from [Company] was part of a restructuring, and my relationship with my direct manager was complicated. I'd recommend speaking with [alternative reference] who can speak more directly to my work and impact."

### Option 2: Reference Check Service
Services like Allison & Taylor or CheckYourReference.com will call your former employer posing as a reference checker and report back what's being said. If the reference is negative or inaccurate, you can address it.

### Option 3: Legal Action (Last Resort)
In Canada, providing a false or malicious reference can be grounds for a defamation claim. If you have evidence that a former employer is sabotaging your job search, consult a lawyer.

---

## Step 7: The Reference Sheet

Create a clean, one-page reference sheet to provide when asked:

| Name | Title | Relationship | Contact |
|------|-------|-------------|---------|
| [Name] | VP Operations, [Company] | Direct manager (2022-2026) | email / phone |
| [Name] | Director of Marketing, [Company] | Cross-functional colleague | email / phone |
| [Name] | CEO, [Previous Company] | Former manager (2018-2022) | email / phone |

**Do not put references on your resume.** Provide them separately when asked, and give your references a heads-up before sharing their info.

---

## Key Takeaway

References are not an afterthought. They're a strategic asset. The best time to set them up is during your severance negotiation and in the first week after your layoff, while relationships are fresh. A proactive reference strategy turns a potential vulnerability into a strength.
