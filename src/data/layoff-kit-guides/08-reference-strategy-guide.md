# Reference Strategy Guide

Secure strong references after a layoff, even if your departure wasn't on great terms.

> **Disclaimer:** This is general career advice. Reference practices vary by industry and employer. Legal claims mentioned (such as defamation) are general information, not legal advice. Consult a lawyer for your specific situation.

You don't need to do everything today. Work through this at your own pace.

**Time estimate:** 1-2 hours to set up, then 15 minutes per job application
**What you'll need:** Contact info for 3-5 former managers/colleagues, your target job descriptions, and your severance agreement (if negotiating reference language)

**If you haven't signed your severance yet, read the section on negotiating references into your severance agreement first.**

**Top 3 actions from this guide:**
1. Negotiate a written reference letter into your severance agreement before you sign anything.
2. Ask 3-5 managers and colleagues to be references this week while the relationship is fresh.
3. Request LinkedIn recommendations now. They strengthen your profile during the job search.

---

## Step 1: Build Your Reference List

You need 3-5 references. Aim for a mix:

| Type | Who | Why |
|------|-----|-----|
| Direct manager | Your most recent boss (or the one before) | Validates day-to-day performance |
| Senior leader | Someone 2+ levels above you | Validates strategic impact |
| Peer/colleague | Someone you worked closely with | Validates collaboration and character |
| External stakeholder | Client, vendor, or partner | Validates professional reputation |
| Former manager | A boss from a previous job | Provides a longer track record |

If you're struggling to fill five spots, think beyond traditional managers: professors, volunteer coordinators, freelance clients, professional association contacts, or mentors all count.

**Action:** Write down 5 names right now. If your most recent manager is a problem, skip them (more on this below).

---

## Step 2: Ask the Right Way

### Asking a Former Manager

```
"Hi [Name], I hope you're doing well. I wanted to let you know that
I'm exploring new opportunities after a restructuring at [Company].
I really valued working with you, and I was wondering if you'd be
comfortable serving as a professional reference. I'd be happy to share
what kinds of roles I'm targeting so you can speak to the most relevant
parts of our work together."
```

### Asking a Colleague

```
"Hey [Name], I'm starting my job search and wanted to ask if you'd
be willing to be a reference. I always appreciated how we worked
together on [specific project], and I think you could speak to
[specific skill or quality]. Let me know if you're comfortable."
```

### If Your Manager Was Part of the Problem

If your relationship with your manager was difficult, you don't owe them a spot on your reference list. This is common and hiring managers understand it. Use someone else who saw your work firsthand. Your manager's manager, a peer, or a stakeholder from a different department all work.

Many large companies limit formal references to confirming job title, dates, and sometimes salary. Your manager may still agree to serve as a personal reference separately.

**Action:** Send your reference requests this week while the working relationship is still fresh.

---

## Step 3: Prep Your References

Don't just ask and walk away. Set your references up for success by sending them:

1. The job description for positions you're applying to
2. 3-4 bullet points about what you'd like them to emphasize
3. A reminder of specific projects or accomplishments you worked on together

```
Reference Prep Email:

Hi [Name],

Thank you for agreeing to be a reference. Here's a quick update on
my search and a few things that might help if you get a call.

I'm targeting [type of role] in [industry/function]. The key things
I'm hoping you can speak to:

- My work on [specific project] and the results we achieved
- My ability to [specific skill: lead teams, manage stakeholders]
- My character and how I show up as a colleague

Here's a link to one of the roles I'm in the running for:
[job posting URL]

If there's anything else you'd find helpful, let me know.
I really appreciate your support.
```

**Action:** Send this prep email to each reference before sharing their info with any employer.

## Step 4: Negotiate References Into Your Severance

One of the most underused severance negotiation strategies. Ask for:

1. A written reference letter from your manager or HR
2. Agreed-upon reference language the company will use if contacted
3. A commitment that the company will not provide negative references

```
Severance negotiation script:

"As part of the severance agreement, I'd like to include a written
reference letter from [manager name] and a commitment that any
reference inquiries to the company will be handled with the
agreed-upon language we discuss."
```

Most companies agree because it protects them too (limits liability for what's said in reference checks).

**Action:** Add reference language to your severance negotiation checklist before you sign anything.

---

## Step 5: LinkedIn Recommendations

LinkedIn recommendations are visible on your profile unless you choose to hide them. Not a substitute for phone references, but they strengthen your profile.

```
Request script:

"Hi [Name], I'm updating my LinkedIn as part of my job search.
Would you be open to writing a short recommendation? Even 2-3
sentences about our work on [project] or my strengths in [area]
would be really helpful. Happy to return the favour anytime."
```

A good LinkedIn recommendation is specific (mentions a project or result), short (3-5 sentences), and credible (includes the recommender's relationship to you).

**Action:** Request 3-5 LinkedIn recommendations this week.

---

## Step 6: Handle Negative References

If you suspect a former employer might give a negative reference, you have three options.

**Option 1: Preemptive Framing.** Tell potential employers briefly, without drama:

```
"I should mention that my departure from [Company] was part of a
restructuring, and my relationship with my direct manager was
complicated. I'd recommend speaking with [alternative reference]
who can speak more directly to my work and impact."
```

**Option 2: Reference Check Service.** Services like Allison & Taylor or CheckYourReference.com will call your former employer posing as a reference checker and report back what's being said. Verify any service's reputation before paying.

**Option 3: Legal Action (last resort).** Employers giving references in good faith have legal protection (qualified privilege). Only deliberately false or malicious references may be grounds for a defamation claim. If you have evidence of sabotage, consult a lawyer.

**Action:** If you're worried about a negative reference, use a reference check service before it costs you an offer.

---

## Step 7: Create Your Reference Sheet

A clean, one-page document to provide when asked:

| Name | Title | Relationship | Contact |
|------|-------|-------------|---------|
| [Name] | VP Operations, [Company] | Direct manager (2022-2026) | email / phone |
| [Name] | Director of Marketing, [Company] | Cross-functional colleague | email / phone |
| [Name] | CEO, [Previous Company] | Former manager (2018-2022) | email / phone |

Do not put references on your resume. Provide them separately when asked. Give your references a heads-up before sharing their info.

**Action:** Create your reference sheet and keep it ready. Update it each time you prep a reference for a specific role.

---

## Key Takeaway

References are a strategic asset, not an afterthought. Set them up during your severance negotiation and in the first week after your layoff, while relationships are fresh. A proactive reference strategy turns a potential vulnerability into a strength.
