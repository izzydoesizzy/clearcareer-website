# Benefits Transition Guide

**How to maintain health, dental, and insurance coverage after losing your employer plan.**

> **Important:** This guide provides general educational information about Canadian benefits and insurance. It is not insurance advice. Coverage terms, conversion windows, and premiums vary by provider, policy, and province. Confirm all details with your benefits provider and insurer directly.

Losing your job means losing your group benefits. For most Canadian families, that means dental coverage, prescription drug coverage, vision care, paramedical services (physio, massage, therapy), life insurance, and disability insurance. Here's exactly what to do and when.

---

## What You Keep (Provincial Health)

Your provincial health plan (OHIP in Ontario, MSP in BC, AHCIP in Alberta, RAMQ in Quebec) continues regardless of your employment status. You are still covered for:

- Doctor visits
- Hospital stays
- Medically necessary procedures
- Emergency room visits
- Specialist referrals

**You do not lose this coverage when you lose your job.** There is nothing you need to do to maintain it.

---

## What You Lose (Supplementary Coverage)

What disappears when your group plan ends:

| Coverage Type | Typical Value | Impact |
|--------------|--------------|--------|
| **Dental** | $1,500-$3,000/year | Cleanings, fillings, crowns, orthodontics |
| **Prescription drugs** | Varies widely | Ongoing medications can cost hundreds/month out of pocket |
| **Vision** | $200-$500/year | Eye exams, glasses, contacts |
| **Paramedical** | $500-$2,000/year | Physio, massage, chiro, therapy, naturopath |
| **Life insurance** | 1-2x salary | Death benefit for dependents |
| **Disability insurance** | 60-70% of salary | Income protection if you become unable to work |

---

## The Conversion Window: Your Most Important Deadline

Most group insurance policies give you **31 to 60 days** after your benefit termination date to convert your group coverage to an individual plan with the same insurer. (The exact window varies by policy. Confirm with your benefits provider.)

**Why this matters:**
- Conversion policies typically offer guaranteed acceptance during the conversion window, meaning no medical questions or pre-existing condition exclusions. Terms vary by insurer. Confirm with your provider.
- If you miss the window, you'll need to apply for individual coverage and may be denied or charged higher rates for pre-existing conditions.

**How to find your conversion deadline:**
1. Check your termination letter for the exact benefit end date
2. Call the group insurance provider (the number on your benefits card)
3. Ask: "What is my conversion deadline, and what individual plans are available?"

## Step-by-Step: Transitioning Your Benefits

### Week 1: Inventory Your Coverage

Before your benefits end, use them. Schedule and complete any pending:
- Dental cleanings or procedures
- Eye exams or new glasses
- Prescriptions (get 90-day refills where possible)
- Paramedical appointments
- Any procedures you've been putting off

### Week 2: Assess Your Needs

Ask yourself:
- Does anyone in my family have ongoing prescriptions? (Critical to maintain drug coverage)
- Do we have dental work in progress? (Don't lose coverage mid-treatment)
- Am I in therapy or ongoing paramedical care? (Budget for out-of-pocket if needed)
- Do I need life insurance? (If you have dependents, yes)

### Week 3: Compare Options

You have four main paths:

**Option 1: Convert your group plan** (guaranteed acceptance)
- Pro: No medical questions, immediate coverage
- Con: Premiums are higher than group rates, coverage may be less generous
- Best for: People with pre-existing conditions or ongoing treatments

**Option 2: Buy individual health insurance**
- Pro: Often cheaper than conversion plans, more flexibility
- Con: Requires medical underwriting (may be denied or excluded for pre-existing conditions)
- Providers: Blue Cross, Manulife, Sun Life, Green Shield, Empire Life
- Best for: Healthy individuals/families looking for the best rate

**Option 3: Negotiate benefits continuation in your severance**
- Pro: Employer continues paying for your group plan during the severance period
- Con: Only available if negotiated into the severance agreement
- Best for: Everyone. Always ask for this.

**Option 4: Go without supplementary coverage**
- Pro: Saves money
- Con: Risk. One dental emergency or prescription change can cost thousands
- Best for: Single people in good health with short expected job search

### Week 4: Act Before Your Deadline

Do not let the conversion window expire without making a decision. Even if you don't convert, at least call and understand your options so you're making an informed choice.

## Prescription Drug Bridge Strategy

If you're on ongoing medications, here's how to bridge the gap:

1. **Get 90-day refills** before your benefits end
2. **Ask your doctor** about generic alternatives (often 70-80% cheaper)
3. **Check provincial drug programs**: Ontario's Trillium Drug Program covers high-cost drugs for residents whose drug costs exceed ~4% of household income
4. **Manufacturer patient assistance programs**: Many drug manufacturers offer free medication for people without coverage. Ask your pharmacist.
5. **Costco pharmacy**: Does not require a membership for the pharmacy. Often has the lowest prices.

---

## Dental Coverage Bridge

Dental is usually the most expensive coverage to maintain individually. Options:

- **Finish in-progress work** before your benefits end
- **Dental schools**: University of Toronto, UBC, and other dental schools offer supervised care at 50-70% of regular prices
- **Dental discount plans**: Not insurance, but provide 10-30% discounts at participating dentists
- **Budget $500-$1,000** for out-of-pocket dental if you go without coverage for a few months

---

## Family Coverage Considerations

If you have a spouse or partner with employer benefits, check if you can be added to their plan. Most group plans allow adding family members within 30 days of a "qualifying life event" (losing your own coverage counts).

If both of you are without coverage, prioritize:
1. Prescription drug coverage (highest financial risk)
2. Dental for children (orthodontic and preventive care)
3. Life insurance (if you have dependents)
4. Everything else

## Cost Comparison: What to Expect

Individual plan premiums vary by age, province, and coverage level. Rough ranges for a 40-year-old in Ontario:

| Plan Type | Monthly Cost (Individual) | Monthly Cost (Family) |
|-----------|------------------------|-----------------------|
| Basic (drugs + dental) | $150-$250 | $400-$600 |
| Comprehensive (drugs + dental + vision + paramedical) | $250-$400 | $600-$900 |
| Conversion plan (from group) | $200-$500 | $500-$1,200 |

These are estimates. Get actual quotes from 2-3 providers.

---

## Key Takeaway

Benefits coverage is one of the most overlooked parts of a layoff. Most people focus on the severance number and forget that losing family health coverage can cost thousands in a single dental emergency or prescription change. Know your conversion deadline, negotiate benefits continuation in your severance, and have a plan before your coverage lapses.
