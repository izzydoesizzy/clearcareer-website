# Benefits Transition Guide

Keep your health, dental, and insurance coverage intact after losing your employer plan.

> **Disclaimer:** This is general benefits education, not insurance advice. Coverage terms, conversion windows, and premiums vary by provider, policy, and province. Confirm details with your insurer directly.

You don't need to do everything today. Work through this at your own pace.

**Time estimate:** 1 hour to inventory and assess, 1-2 hours to compare and act
**What you'll need:** Your benefits card, termination letter (for benefit end date), and a list of ongoing prescriptions/treatments

**Top 3 actions from this guide:**
1. Call your group insurer today to confirm your exact benefits end date and conversion deadline.
2. Book dental, vision, and prescription refills now while you still have coverage.
3. Ask about converting your group plan to an individual plan before the window closes (often 30-90 days).

---

## What You Keep (Provincial Health)

Your provincial health plan (OHIP, MSP, AHCIP, RAMQ) continues regardless of employment status. Doctor visits, hospital stays, emergency room, specialist referrals, medically necessary procedures. All covered. Nothing to do here.

**Action:** No action needed. Provincial health coverage is automatic.

---

## What You Lose (Supplementary Coverage)

Everything your employer was paying for disappears when your group plan ends.

| Coverage | Typical Value | What It Means |
|----------|--------------|---------------|
| Dental | $1,500-$3,000/year | Cleanings, fillings, crowns, orthodontics |
| Prescription drugs | Varies | Ongoing meds can cost hundreds/month out of pocket |
| Vision | $200-$500/year | Eye exams, glasses, contacts |
| Paramedical | $500-$2,000/year | Physio, massage, chiro, therapy |
| Life insurance | 1-2x salary | Death benefit for dependents |
| Disability insurance | 60-70% of salary | Income protection if unable to work |

**Action:** List every coverage type you currently use. This becomes your shopping list.

---

## The Conversion Window: Your Most Important Deadline

Most group policies give you **typically 30 to 90 days** after your benefit end date to convert to an individual plan with the same insurer, but some policies allow as few as 30 days. Confirm your exact deadline immediately. During this window, you typically get guaranteed acceptance with no medical questions or pre-existing condition exclusions.

Miss the window and you'll need to apply on the open market. You may be denied or charged more for pre-existing conditions.

**How to find your deadline:**
1. Check your termination letter for the benefit end date
2. Call the number on your benefits card
3. Ask: "What is my conversion deadline, and what individual plans are available?"

Confirm your exact benefits end date before booking appointments.

**Action:** Call your group insurer today and confirm your exact conversion deadline. Put it in your calendar with a 1-week warning.

## Week-by-Week Action Plan

### Week 1: Use Your Benefits

Before coverage ends, schedule and complete anything pending. Dental cleanings, eye exams, new glasses, 90-day prescription refills, paramedical appointments, and any procedures you've been putting off.

**Action:** Book every appointment you can before your benefit end date.

### Week 2: Assess Your Needs

Four questions to answer:
- Does anyone in my family have ongoing prescriptions? (Critical to maintain drug coverage)
- Is dental work in progress? (Don't lose coverage mid-treatment)
- Am I in therapy or ongoing paramedical care?
- Do I need life insurance? (If you have dependents, yes)

**Action:** Write down your family's must-have coverages ranked by financial risk.

### Week 3: Compare Your Four Options

**Option 1: Convert your group plan** (guaranteed acceptance)
- Pro: No medical questions, immediate coverage
- Con: Higher premiums, may be less generous
- Best for: People with pre-existing conditions or ongoing treatments

**Option 2: Buy individual health insurance**
- Pro: Often cheaper, more flexibility
- Con: Requires medical underwriting, may exclude pre-existing conditions
- Providers: Blue Cross, Manulife, Sun Life, GreenShield, Empire Life
- Best for: Healthy individuals/families looking for the best rate

**Option 3: Negotiate benefits continuation in your severance**
- Pro: Employer keeps paying for your group plan during severance
- Con: Only available if you negotiate it
- Best for: Everyone. Always ask for this.

**Option 4: Go without supplementary coverage**
- Pro: Saves money
- Con: One dental emergency or prescription change can cost thousands
- Best for: Single people in good health expecting a short job search

**Action:** Get quotes from 2-3 providers and compare against your conversion plan pricing.

### Week 4: Act Before Your Deadline

Do not let the conversion window expire without making a decision. Even if you don't convert, call and understand your options so you're choosing deliberately.

**Action:** Make your final coverage decision and enroll before your conversion deadline.

## Prescription Drug Bridge Strategy

If you're on ongoing medications:

1. Get 90-day refills before benefits end
2. Ask your doctor about generic alternatives (often 70-80% cheaper)
3. Check provincial drug programs (Ontario's Trillium Drug Program covers high-cost drugs when costs exceed ~4% of household income; check ontario.ca/trillium for current thresholds)
4. Ask your pharmacist about manufacturer patient assistance programs (many offer free medication for people without coverage)
5. Use Costco pharmacy (no membership required, often lowest prices)

**Action:** Refill all prescriptions for 90 days before your benefit end date.

---

## Dental Coverage Bridge

Dental is the most expensive coverage to maintain individually.

- Finish in-progress work before benefits end
- University dental schools across Canada (U of T, UBC, Dalhousie, U of Manitoba, and others) offer supervised care at 50-70% of regular prices
- Dental discount plans provide 10-30% off at participating dentists (not insurance)
- Budget $500-$1,000 for out-of-pocket dental if going without for a few months

**Action:** Complete any in-progress dental work before coverage lapses.

---

## Family Coverage: Spouse's Plan

If your spouse or partner has employer benefits, check if you can be added to their plan. Most group plans allow adding family members within 30 days of a "qualifying life event" (losing your own coverage counts).

If your household has lost all coverage, focus on the highest-risk items first. This is temporary.

If both of you are without coverage, prioritize in this order:
1. Prescription drug coverage (highest financial risk)
2. Dental for children
3. Life insurance (if you have dependents)
4. Everything else

**Action:** Call your spouse's benefits administrator and ask about adding you within the qualifying life event window.

## Cost Comparison

Individual plan premiums vary by age, province, and coverage level. Rough ranges for a 40-year-old in Ontario:

| Plan Type | Monthly (Individual) | Monthly (Family) |
|-----------|---------------------|------------------|
| Basic (drugs + dental) | $150-$250 | $400-$600 |
| Comprehensive (drugs + dental + vision + paramedical) | $250-$400 | $600-$900 |
| Conversion plan (from group) | $200-$500 | $500-$1,200 |

These are estimates for a 40-year-old. Premiums increase significantly with age. A 50-year-old may pay 30-50% more than these estimates. Get actual quotes from 2-3 providers.

**Action:** Request quotes and compare before your conversion deadline.

## Key Takeaway

Benefits coverage is one of the most overlooked parts of a layoff. One dental emergency or prescription change can cost thousands. Know your conversion deadline, negotiate benefits continuation in your severance, and have a plan before your coverage lapses.
