# Severance Negotiation Playbook

**Get the severance you're owed, not just the first number they put on paper.**

> **Disclaimer:** This guide is educational, not legal advice. Every situation is different. Consult an employment lawyer in your province before making decisions about your severance.

You don't need to do everything today. Work through this at your own pace.

**Time:** About 60 minutes to work through. **What you'll need:** Your termination letter, employment contract (if you have one), and your severance calculator results from this kit.

**Top 3 actions from this guide:**
1. Do not sign anything yet. Take the severance letter home and sleep on it.
2. Run the Severance Calculator to find your statutory minimum and common-law estimate.
3. Decide whether to negotiate yourself, hire a lawyer, or get a legal opinion first.

---

## The First Rule: Do Not Sign Anything Yet

Your employer may say "sign by Friday" or "this offer expires in 7 days." These deadlines are often flexible, but confirm with your employer whether there's a genuine business reason. There is no statutory deadline to sign a severance offer in Canada, though employers can withdraw an offer if you wait too long. In most provinces, you have up to two years to file a wrongful dismissal claim.

Take the document home. Read it carefully. Sleep on it.

**Action:** Put the severance letter in a safe place. Do not reply to it yet.

---

## Step 1: Understand What You're Entitled To

There are two layers of severance in Canada:

**Layer 1: Statutory minimum (ESA)**
The legal floor. In Ontario: up to 8 weeks termination pay + up to 26 weeks severance pay (for 5+ years at companies with $2.5M+ payroll). Other provinces have lower floors.

**Layer 2: Common-law reasonable notice**
What courts award based on the Bardal factors: age, tenure, role seniority, and job market. For professionals earning $100K+, this is often substantially higher than the statutory minimum, but the range varies widely by case.

If your offer is close to the statutory minimum, there is likely room to negotiate.

**Action:** Run the Severance Calculator in this kit to see both numbers for your situation.

---

## Step 2: Calculate Your Target Number

Set three numbers using your calculator results:

| Number | What It Is | How to Use It |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| **Walk-away** | Minimum you'll accept (usually ESA + 50%) | Below this, push harder or involve a lawyer |
| **Target** | Mid-point of your common-law estimate | Your opening ask |
| **Stretch** | High end of your common-law estimate | Only if you have strong leverage |

**Action:** Write down your three numbers. You'll use these in Step 5.

---

## Step 3: Identify Your Leverage

What gives you negotiating power:

**Strong leverage:**
- 10+ years tenure
- Age over 50
- Senior or executive role
- Specialized skills with limited job market
- Evidence of discrimination or bad-faith termination
- You were recruited away from a stable job (inducement)
- No written employment contract limiting severance

**Moderate leverage:**
- 3-10 years tenure
- Mid-career professional role

**Limited leverage:**
- Under 2 years tenure
- Junior role
- Contract with a valid termination clause

Even with limited leverage, the statutory minimum is a floor. There is almost always room to negotiate.

**Action:** List your leverage factors. You'll reference these in your counter-offer.

---

## Step 4: Decide Your Approach

**Option A: Negotiate yourself**
Best for straightforward situations where the offer is in the ballpark. Cost: $0.

**Option B: Hire an employment lawyer**
Best for complex situations or high-value packages. Most work on contingency (15-30% of the additional amount negotiated) or flat fee ($500-$2,000 for a review).

**Option C: Get a legal opinion, then negotiate yourself**
Pay for a 1-hour consultation ($300-$600) to understand your rights, then negotiate with that knowledge.

For professionals earning $100K+ with 5+ years tenure, Option B or C usually pays for itself.

**Action:** Decide your approach. If hiring a lawyer, check the Lawyer Directory in this kit.

## Step 5: The Negotiation Email

If negotiating yourself, use this template. Customize the bracketed sections:

```
Subject: Re: Termination and Severance Package

Hi [Name],

Thank you for providing the details of the separation package. I've taken time to review the offer carefully.

I value the time I spent at [Company] and want to reach a resolution that works for both sides. However, after reviewing the terms, I believe the current offer does not reflect my entitlement under common-law reasonable notice, given my [X years] of service, my role as [title], and the current job market for professionals in my field.

Based on my understanding of comparable cases and the Bardal factors, I believe a more appropriate package would include:

1. [X months] of salary continuation (compared to the [Y weeks/months] currently offered)
2. Benefits continuation for the full severance period (health, dental, vision, life insurance)
3. Pro-rated bonus for [current year]
4. [Any other items: equity acceleration, outplacement support, reference letter]

I'm open to discussing this further and reaching a fair agreement. I'd appreciate the opportunity to have a conversation about these terms.

Best regards,
[Your name]
```

**Tips:**
- Stay professional. No anger, no threats.
- Mention "common-law reasonable notice" and "Bardal factors" by name. This signals you know your rights.
- Be specific about what you want. Vague requests get vague responses.
- Don't mention lawyers unless you've actually retained one.

See the Tax Planning Strategies guide in this kit for the tax implications of salary continuation vs. lump sum.

**Action:** Draft your email using this template. Don't send it until you've slept on it.

## Step 6: What to Negotiate Beyond Money

These items cost your employer very little but have real value to you:

| Item | How to Ask |
|------|------------|
| **Benefits continuation** | "I'd like benefits extended for the full severance period" |
| **Written reference** | "Can we agree on reference language I can share with prospective employers?" |
| **Title on reference** | "I'd like my departure described as a company restructuring" |
| **Outplacement support** | "Would the company consider covering outplacement services?" |
| **Equity acceleration** | "Can we accelerate vesting for shares that would have vested during the notice period?" |
| **Pro-rated bonus** | "I'd like my bonus pro-rated through my termination date" |
| **Non-compete removal** | "I'd like the non-compete clause waived in the release" |
| **Keep laptop** | "Can I keep the laptop for job search use?" |

**Action:** Pick 2-3 items from this list to add to your counter-offer.

---

## Step 7: Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Signing under pressure.** There is no legal deadline.
2. **Accepting the first offer.** The first offer may be fair, but it's worth checking against your numbers before accepting.
3. **Getting emotional.** Write emails instead of verbal conversations when emotions are high.
4. **Threatening legal action without a lawyer.** Either retain one or don't mention one.
5. **Forgetting about benefits.** 6 months of family health insurance can be worth more than a $5K salary gap.
6. **Not getting it in writing.** Verbal promises mean nothing.
7. **Signing a release without understanding it.** The release waives your right to sue. Make sure the trade is worth it.

## Step 8: The Timeline

| Day | Action |
|-----|--------|
| Day 1 | Receive offer. Read it. Do not sign. |
| Day 1-3 | Run the calculator. Gather your leverage factors. |
| Day 3-5 | Decide: negotiate yourself or hire a lawyer. |
| Day 5-10 | Send your counter-offer (or have your lawyer send a letter). |
| Day 10-20 | Back-and-forth. Usually 1-3 rounds. |
| Day 20-30 | Reach agreement. Review the final document before signing. |

Most severance negotiations resolve in 2-4 weeks.

---

## When to Walk Away

Litigation is stressful and slow. Weigh the financial benefit against the emotional cost before escalating.

If negotiations stall and the offer is well below your common-law entitlement:

1. **File a complaint** with your provincial employment standards branch (for ESA violations)
2. **File a civil claim** for wrongful dismissal (for common-law amounts)
3. **File a human rights complaint** if discrimination was a factor
4. **Accept the offer** if the gap is small and litigation isn't worth the cost

Your lawyer can advise on the cost-benefit of each option. Most cases settle before trial.

---

## Key Takeaway

Your employer has HR professionals and lawyers who do this regularly. You're doing it for the first time. That's OK. You just need to prepare, know your numbers, and stay calm.

Don't leave money on the table. You earned it.
