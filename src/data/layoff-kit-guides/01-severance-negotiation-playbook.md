# Severance Negotiation Playbook

**A guide to understanding your severance options and preparing for a conversation with your lawyer.**

> **Important:** This guide provides general educational information about Canadian employment law. It is not legal advice. Every situation is different and outcomes depend on your specific contract, circumstances, and province. Before making any decisions about your severance package, consult an employment lawyer licensed in your province.

Many people accept the first severance offer without exploring their options. Initial offers are often below what courts have awarded in comparable common-law cases. This guide gives you the background, framework, and questions to bring to your employment lawyer so you can have an informed conversation.

---

## The First Rule: Do Not Sign Anything Yet

Your employer may give you a deadline. "Sign by Friday." "This offer expires in 7 days."

These deadlines are negotiation tactics, not legal requirements. In most Canadian provinces, you have up to two years to file a wrongful dismissal claim. There is no legal obligation to sign on their timeline.

Take the document home. Read it carefully. Sleep on it. Then decide your next move.

---

## Step 1: Understand What You're Entitled To

There are two layers of severance in Canada:

**Layer 1: Statutory minimum (ESA/provincial legislation)**
This is the floor. The legal minimum your employer must pay. In Ontario, it's up to 8 weeks of termination pay plus up to 26 weeks of severance pay (for employees with 5+ years at companies meeting the payroll threshold). Other provinces have lower statutory floors.

**Layer 2: Common-law reasonable notice**
This is what courts award when employers don't provide adequate notice. It's based on the Bardal factors: your age, length of service, character of employment, and availability of similar work. For professionals earning $100K+, common-law entitlements are typically 2x to 4x the statutory minimum.

**If your offer is close to the statutory minimum, there may be room to negotiate. An employment lawyer can assess your specific situation.**

---

## Step 2: Calculate Your Target Number

Use the severance calculator in this kit to get your ESA minimum and common-law estimate. Then set three numbers:

| Number | What It Is | How to Use It |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| **Walk-away** | The minimum you'll accept (usually ESA + 50%) | Below this, you either negotiate harder or involve a lawyer |
| **Target** | The mid-point of your common-law estimate | This is what you ask for initially |
| **Stretch** | The high end of your common-law estimate | Only achievable with strong leverage (discrimination, bad faith, etc.) |

---

## Step 3: Identify Your Leverage

Before you negotiate, understand what gives you power:

**Strong leverage:**
- Long tenure (10+ years)
- Age over 50 (harder to find equivalent work)
- Senior/executive role
- Specialized skills with limited job market
- Evidence of discrimination or bad-faith termination
- Inducement (you were recruited away from a stable job)
- No written employment contract limiting severance

**Moderate leverage:**
- 3-10 years tenure
- Mid-career professional role
- Active job market in your field

**Limited leverage:**
- Short tenure (under 2 years)
- Junior role
- Employment contract with a valid termination clause

Even with limited leverage, the statutory minimum is just a floor. there is often room to negotiate.

---

## Step 4: Decide Your Approach

### Option A: Negotiate yourself
Best for straightforward situations where the offer is in the ballpark but needs improvement. Cost: $0. Time investment: a few hours.

### Option B: Hire an employment lawyer
Best for complex situations, high-value packages, or when you suspect discrimination or bad faith. Most lawyers work on contingency (they take a percentage of the additional amount they negotiate) or charge a flat fee for a severance review. Cost: $500-$2,000 for a review, or 15-30% contingency.

### Option C: Get a legal opinion, then negotiate yourself
Middle ground. Pay for a 1-hour consultation to understand your rights, then negotiate using that knowledge. Cost: $300-$600.

**For professionals earning $100K+ with 5+ years tenure: Option B or C often pays for itself.**

## Step 5: The Negotiation Email (Template)

If you're negotiating yourself, here's the email to send to HR or your manager:

---

**Subject: Re: Termination and Severance Package**

Hi [Name],

Thank you for providing the details of the separation package. I've taken time to review the offer carefully.

I value the time I spent at [Company] and want to reach a resolution that works for both sides. However, after reviewing the terms, I believe the current offer does not reflect my entitlement under common-law reasonable notice, given my [X years] of service, my role as [title], and the current job market for professionals in my field.

Based on my understanding of comparable cases and the Bardal factors, I believe a more appropriate package would include:

1. **[X months] of salary continuation** (compared to the [Y weeks/months] currently offered)
2. **Benefits continuation** for the full severance period (health, dental, vision, life insurance)
3. **Pro-rated bonus** for [current year]
4. **[Any other items: equity acceleration, outplacement support, reference letter]**

I'm open to discussing this further and reaching a fair agreement. I'd appreciate the opportunity to have a conversation about these terms.

Best regards,
[Your name]

---

### Tips for the email:
- Keep it professional and calm. No anger, no threats, no ultimatums.
- Mention "common-law reasonable notice" and "Bardal factors" by name. This signals you know your rights.
- Be specific about what you want. Vague requests get vague responses.
- Don't mention lawyers unless you've actually retained one. Empty threats backfire.

## Step 6: What to Negotiate Beyond Money

Severance isn't just about the lump sum. These items cost your employer little but have real value to you:

| Item | Why It Matters | How to Ask |
|------|---------------|------------|
| **Benefits continuation** | Health insurance for your family during the job search | "I'd like benefits extended for the full severance period" |
| **Reference letter** | A written reference you can use going forward | "Can we agree on reference language I can share with prospective employers?" |
| **Title/role on the reference** | Controls your narrative | "I'd like my departure described as a company restructuring" |
| **Outplacement support** | Career coaching and resume help (worth $2K-$5K) | "Would the company consider covering outplacement services?" |
| **Equity acceleration** | Unvested RSUs or options vesting during severance | "Can we accelerate vesting for [X shares] that would have vested during the notice period?" |
| **Pro-rated bonus** | Bonus for the partial year worked | "I'd like my [year] bonus pro-rated through my termination date" |
| **Non-compete removal** | Removes restrictions on your next job | "I'd like the non-compete clause waived in the release" |
| **Laptop/equipment** | Keep your work devices | "Can I keep the laptop for job search use?" |

## Step 7: Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Signing under pressure.** There is no legal deadline. Take your time.
2. **Accepting the first offer.** It's often the opening position, not the final offer.
3. **Getting emotional in negotiations.** Keep it professional. Write emails instead of having verbal conversations when emotions are high.
4. **Threatening legal action without a lawyer.** Either retain a lawyer or don't mention one.
5. **Forgetting about benefits.** A $5,000 gap in salary matters less than 6 months of family health insurance.
6. **Not getting it in writing.** Verbal promises mean nothing. Everything must be in the written agreement.
7. **Signing a release without understanding it.** The release typically waives your right to sue. Make sure what you're getting is worth it.

## Step 8: The Timeline

| Day | Action |
|-----|--------|
| Day 1 | Receive offer. Read it. Do not sign. |
| Day 1-3 | Calculate your target using the severance calculator. Gather your leverage factors. |
| Day 3-5 | Decide: negotiate yourself or hire a lawyer. If hiring, schedule consultations. |
| Day 5-10 | Send your counter-offer email (or have your lawyer send a letter). |
| Day 10-20 | Back-and-forth negotiation. Usually 1-3 rounds. |
| Day 20-30 | Reach agreement. Review final document carefully before signing. |

Most severance negotiations resolve within 2-4 weeks. Some take longer if lawyers are involved or if the employer is difficult. That's normal.

---

## When to Walk Away

If negotiations stall and the offer remains well below your common-law entitlement, you have options:

1. **File a complaint** with the provincial employment standards branch (for amounts below ESA minimums)
2. **File a civil claim** for wrongful dismissal (for common-law amounts above ESA)
3. **File a human rights complaint** if discrimination was a factor
4. **Accept the offer** if the gap is small and the cost of litigation isn't worth it

Your employment lawyer can advise on the cost-benefit of each option. Most cases settle before trial.

---

## Key Takeaway

The company has done this before. They have HR professionals and lawyers who negotiate severance packages regularly. You're doing it for the first time.

That doesn't mean you're at a disadvantage. It means you need to prepare, know your numbers, and stay calm. The information in this guide can help you prepare for a conversation with your employment lawyer and understand the negotiation process.

Don't leave money on the table. You earned it.
