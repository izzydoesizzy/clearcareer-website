# Tax Planning Strategies for Severance

**Common tax strategies that Canadian professionals discuss with their accountants after receiving severance.**

> **Important:** This guide provides general educational information about Canadian tax concepts. **It is not tax advice.** Tax implications vary based on your income, province, personal circumstances, and the specifics of your severance arrangement. **Consult a Certified Professional Accountant (CPA) before implementing any strategy described here.** The strategies below are commonly discussed in Canadian tax planning circles but may not apply to your situation.

A lump-sum severance payment gets taxed at source. For professionals earning $100K+, that typically means 30% withheld on amounts over $15,000 before you see a dollar. There are strategies that may reduce this, but they require professional guidance to implement correctly.

---

## How Severance Is Taxed in Canada

When your employer pays severance as a lump sum, they withhold tax at these federal rates:

| Lump Sum Amount | Federal Withholding | Quebec Withholding |
|-----------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Up to $5,000 | 10% | 5% |
| $5,001 - $15,000 | 20% | 10% |
| Over $15,000 | **30%** | **15%** (+ provincial) |

Provincial tax is additional. For a $120K salary in Ontario getting 12 months severance (~$120,000 lump sum), expect roughly $36,000-$40,000 withheld at source.

**The withholding rate is not your actual tax rate.** Your real marginal rate may be higher (43-53% for high earners) or lower (if severance is your only income that year). The difference gets settled when you file your tax return.

## Strategy 1: RRSP Direct Transfer

**Potential impact (varies by situation)**

This is the single most powerful tax strategy for severance. If you have RRSP contribution room, you can direct your employer to transfer part (or all) of your severance directly into your RRSP.

**Why this matters:** When the employer transfers directly, no tax is withheld at source. You keep 100% of the transferred amount, and it reduces your taxable income.

**How to do it:**
1. Check your RRSP contribution room on your latest Notice of Assessment (or log into CRA My Account)
2. Tell your employer (in writing): "I would like $[amount] of my severance transferred directly to my RRSP at [institution name], account number [XXXX]"
3. Your employer sends the money directly. No withholding.
4. Report the transfer on your tax return as both income and an RRSP deduction (net tax impact: zero)

**Example:**
- Severance: $120,000
- RRSP room available: $45,000
- Direct transfer: $45,000 (no tax withheld)
- Remaining lump sum: $75,000 (30% withheld = $22,500)
- Tax saved vs. full lump sum: ~$13,500 in withholding you keep now, plus the long-term tax-deferred growth

**Important:** Do this before the cheque is cut. Once the employer pays you, you can still contribute to your RRSP yourself, but you won't get the withholding benefit.

## Strategy 2: Retiring Allowance Rollover

**Potential impact (varies by situation)**

If your severance is classified as a "retiring allowance" (which most severance payments are), and you have pre-1996 service years, you may qualify for an additional RRSP transfer above and beyond your normal contribution room.

**The rules:**
- $2,000 per year of service before 1996
- $1,500 per year of service before 1989 where employer pension contributions didn't vest

**Example:** If you started work in 1992, you have 4 pre-1996 years = $8,000 extra RRSP room on top of your regular contribution limit.

**How to claim it:** Your employer's payroll department should know about this. Ask them to include the retiring allowance rollover in the direct RRSP transfer. If they don't know, point them to CRA's Guide T4040 (RRSPs and Other Registered Plans for Retirement).

## Strategy 3: Timing Your Severance Across Tax Years

**Potential impact (varies by situation)**

If you're laid off in October, November, or December, ask your employer to defer part of the severance payment to January. This splits the income across two tax years and can keep you in a lower tax bracket in both years.

**Example:**
- Laid off in November 2026
- Total severance: $120,000
- If paid in full in 2026: Pushed into 53% marginal bracket (ON)
- If $60K paid in December 2026 and $60K in January 2027: Each year stays in a lower bracket

**How to ask:** "Can we structure the severance as salary continuation through [date] rather than a lump sum?" This naturally splits the income across pay periods and tax years.

**Caution:** This only works if you expect lower income in the following year (e.g., you'll be job searching for a few months). If you start a high-paying job in January, splitting may not help.

## Strategy 4: Salary Continuation vs. Lump Sum

**Potential impact (varies by situation)**

Some employers offer severance as "salary continuation" (monthly payments that continue as if you're still employed) rather than a lump sum. This has tax implications:

**Salary continuation pros:**
- Tax withheld at your normal payroll rate (may be lower than lump-sum rates)
- Benefits typically continue during the salary continuation period
- CPP/EI premiums continue to accumulate (affects your future benefits)

**Salary continuation cons:**
- If you find a new job during the continuation period, some employers stop or reduce payments
- You lose control of the money (it comes on the employer's schedule)
- If the employer goes bankrupt during the continuation, you may lose unpaid amounts

**Lump sum pros:**
- You get the full amount immediately
- Full control over investment and spending
- No risk if employer has financial trouble

**Ask your employer which options are available.** In some cases, you can negotiate a hybrid: part lump sum (for RRSP transfer), part salary continuation (for benefits and lower withholding).

## Strategy 5: TFSA as a Tax-Free Bridge

**Not a tax savings strategy, but a critical planning tool.**

Your TFSA withdrawals are completely tax-free. If you have TFSA savings, this is the first place to draw from during your job search, before touching taxable accounts.

**2026 TFSA contribution limit:** $7,000 per year (cumulative lifetime limit depends on your age and when you turned 18).

**Strategy:** If you have TFSA savings and also receive severance, draw from the TFSA for living expenses and direct the severance into your RRSP. This effectively lets you live on tax-free money while sheltering your severance from tax.

## Strategy 6: Capital Gains on Stock Options

**Relevant for tech workers and executives with equity compensation.**

If you have unvested RSUs or stock options, your termination triggers tax events:

**RSUs (Restricted Stock Units):**
- Unvested RSUs are typically forfeited on termination (check your agreement)
- If you negotiate accelerated vesting, the vested shares are taxable income at their market value on the vesting date
- Sell immediately to avoid further market risk

**Stock Options:**
- Check your exercise deadline post-termination (usually 30-90 days)
- If options are "in the money" (exercise price below current market price), exercising creates a taxable benefit
- The employee stock option deduction (50% of the benefit) may apply for CCPC shares
- Consider exercising in a low-income year if possible

**Action items:**
1. Check your equity agreement for post-termination rules
2. Note the exercise deadline
3. Talk to a tax professional before exercising or selling

## Strategy 7: Employment Insurance Interaction

**EI does not reduce your severance. But severance can affect your EI start date.**

Under current temporary measures (valid through October 10, 2026), severance payments do not delay or reduce EI benefits. This is a temporary pandemic-era rule that has been extended.

**After October 2026 (if temporary measures end):**
- Lump-sum severance is allocated over your notice period
- EI benefits don't start until after the allocated period ends
- Salary continuation delays EI until the continuation ends

**Action:** Apply for EI immediately regardless of severance. Service Canada will sort out the timing.

## When to Hire an Accountant

A one-time consultation with a CPA costs $200-$500 and is worth it if:

- Your severance is over $50,000
- You have stock options or RSUs
- You have significant RRSP room available
- You're being offered salary continuation vs. lump sum and need to compare
- You have other income sources (rental, business, investment) that complicate the picture

Ask for a "severance tax planning consultation." Any experienced Canadian CPA will know what this means.

## Key Takeaway

Discussing these strategies with a CPA before your severance cheque is issued can make a meaningful difference in your tax outcome. Many of these strategies need to be arranged before the employer processes the payment, so schedule a CPA consultation early. The cost of a one-time tax planning session ($200-$500) is often well worth it.
