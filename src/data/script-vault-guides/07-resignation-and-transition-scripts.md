# Resignation and Transition Scripts

**10 word-for-word scripts for resigning professionally, handling counter-offers, and announcing your move.**

> **Disclaimer:** These scripts are starting points you can adapt. Always review your employment contract, non-compete agreements, and notice period requirements before resigning. Consider consulting an employment lawyer if you have concerns about restrictive clauses.

You don't need to use all of these. Pick the ones that match your situation.

**Time estimate:** 15 minutes to read, then use as needed
**What you'll need:** Your signed offer letter from the new company (do not resign without this), your employment contract, and your calendar

---

## Resignation Rules

1. **Do not resign until you have a signed offer letter in hand.** Verbal offers fall through. Handshake deals collapse. Get it in writing first.
2. **Review your contract.** Check your notice period, non-compete clauses, non-solicit agreements, and any clawback provisions (signing bonus repayment, relocation reimbursement).
3. **Tell your manager before anyone else.** Not your work friend. Not your team. Your manager hears it first, in person or on a call. Not by email.
4. **Keep it professional.** No matter how badly you want to tell your boss exactly what you think of them. The industry is smaller than you think.
5. **Give appropriate notice.** Standard is 2 weeks. Senior roles may require 4 weeks. Check your contract.

## Resignation Conversations

### Script 1: The Standard Resignation (In Person or Video)

```
"Hi [MANAGER NAME], thank you for making the time to talk.

I want to let you know that I've accepted a position at
another company, and I'll be resigning. My last day would be
[DATE, based on your notice period].

This wasn't an easy decision. I've valued my time here and
learned a lot working with you and the team. I want to make
sure the transition is as smooth as possible.

I'm happy to help with [KNOWLEDGE TRANSFER / TRAINING A
REPLACEMENT / DOCUMENTING PROCESSES / WRAPPING UP CURRENT
PROJECTS] during my remaining time.

Thank you for everything."
```

### Script 2: The Resignation (When You Had a Bad Experience)

Even if the job was terrible, leave gracefully. Your reputation follows you.

```
"Hi [MANAGER NAME], I wanted to let you know that I've
accepted a new opportunity and will be moving on. My last
day would be [DATE].

I appreciate the experiences I've had here and the things
I've learned. I'm committed to making the transition smooth
over the next [NOTICE PERIOD].

Please let me know how I can best support the handoff."
```

Notice what's not in this script: complaints, criticisms, or explanations of why you're leaving. Save the candor for your exit interview (if you choose to give one) or for a trusted friend.

### Script 3: The Resignation (When You Love the Job But Got a Better Offer)

```
"Hi [MANAGER NAME], this is a hard conversation because I've
genuinely enjoyed working here. But I've been presented with
an opportunity that I can't turn down, and I've decided to
accept it.

My last day would be [DATE]. I want you to know this decision
had nothing to do with the team or the work. This was about
[CAREER GROWTH / COMPENSATION / LOCATION / PERSONAL REASONS],
and the new role offers something I need at this stage.

I'd love to stay connected, and I'm committed to a strong
finish during my notice period."
```

## Resignation Letters

After the verbal conversation, send a formal letter (email is fine).

### Script 4: The Formal Resignation Letter

```
Subject: Resignation — [YOUR NAME]

Dear [MANAGER NAME],

Please accept this as formal notice of my resignation from
the position of [YOUR JOB TITLE] at [COMPANY NAME], effective
[LAST DAY DATE].

I'm grateful for the opportunities I've had during my time
here, particularly [ONE SPECIFIC POSITIVE: working on X
project, learning from the team, growing in my role].

During my remaining [X WEEKS], I'm committed to ensuring a
smooth transition. Please let me know how I can best support
the handoff of my responsibilities.

Thank you for everything.

Sincerely,
[YOUR FULL NAME]
[YOUR PERSONAL EMAIL]
[YOUR PHONE NUMBER]
```

## Counter-Offer Responses

Your employer may counter-offer when you resign. This is common, especially if you're hard to replace.

### Script 5: Declining a Counter-Offer (Politely)

```
"I really appreciate the counter-offer, and it means a lot
that you value my contribution enough to make it. I've thought
about it carefully.

My decision to leave isn't primarily about compensation. It's
about [CAREER DIRECTION / GROWTH OPPORTUNITY / PERSONAL
REASONS] that I don't think can be addressed here, even with
the adjustment.

I've already committed to the new role, and I want to honor
that commitment. I hope you understand."
```

### Script 6: Buying Time on a Counter-Offer

```
"Thank you. I didn't expect this, and I want to give it
serious consideration. Can I have [48 HOURS / THE WEEKEND]
to think it through?

I want to make sure whatever decision I make is the right
one for everyone."
```

**Important:** Statistics show that 80% of people who accept counter-offers leave within 12 months anyway. The reasons you wanted to leave usually haven't changed. The counter-offer addresses symptoms (money), not causes (growth, culture, direction).

### Script 7: Accepting a Counter-Offer (If You Genuinely Want To Stay)

```
"I've thought about this carefully, and I'd like to accept
the counter-offer. Thank you for making this possible.

To be transparent, the things that led me to explore other
options were [SPECIFIC ISSUES]. The counter-offer addresses
[WHICH ONES IT ADDRESSES]. I'd like to discuss how we can
also address [REMAINING ISSUES] so this is sustainable
long-term.

Can we formalize the new terms in writing?"
```

**Always get counter-offer terms in writing.** Verbal promises from managers during emotional resignation conversations are unreliable.

## Transition and Handoff

### Script 8: Email to Your Team (After Manager Knows)

```
Subject: Moving on — thank you

Hi team,

I wanted to share that I'll be leaving [COMPANY] on [LAST
DAY]. I've accepted a new role that I'm excited about.

Working with this team has been [GENUINE POSITIVE STATEMENT].
I've learned [SPECIFIC THING] from each of you, and I'm
proud of what we accomplished together, especially
[SPECIFIC PROJECT OR ACHIEVEMENT].

During my remaining [X WEEKS], I'll be focused on
[TRANSITION ACTIVITIES: documenting processes, handing off
projects, training]. Please don't hesitate to reach out if
there's anything specific you need from me.

I'd love to stay connected. You can find me at [PERSONAL
EMAIL] and on LinkedIn at [LINKEDIN URL].

Thank you for everything.

Best,
[YOUR NAME]
```

## LinkedIn Announcements

### Script 9: The New Role Announcement

```
I'm excited to share that I'm joining [NEW COMPANY] as
[NEW TITLE].

After [X YEARS] at [PREVIOUS COMPANY], I'm grateful for
[SPECIFIC: the team, the projects, what I learned]. Leaving
wasn't easy, but [NEW COMPANY]'s work on [SPECIFIC THING]
is something I've been watching for a long time, and the
opportunity to [SPECIFIC CONTRIBUTION YOU'LL MAKE] was one
I couldn't pass up.

Thank you to everyone who supported me during this
transition. Special thanks to [2-3 NAMES OF PEOPLE WHO
HELPED: former managers, mentors, networking contacts].

Looking forward to this next chapter. If you're working in
[INDUSTRY/FIELD], let's connect. I'd love to hear what
you're building.
```

### Script 10: The Departure Announcement (When You Haven't Landed Yet)

```
After [X YEARS] at [COMPANY], I've made the decision to
move on.

I'm proud of what I accomplished there, especially
[1-2 SPECIFIC ACHIEVEMENTS]. And I'm grateful to [2-3
PEOPLE OR TEAMS] for making it a place where I grew
significantly as a [YOUR ROLE].

I'm now exploring [TYPE OF OPPORTUNITIES] in [FIELD/
INDUSTRY]. If you know of anything that might be a fit,
or if you'd just like to connect, I'd welcome the
conversation.

Here's what I bring: [2-3 BULLET POINTS OF YOUR KEY
STRENGTHS AND EXPERIENCE].

Onward.
```

## What Not to Do When Leaving

- **Don't badmouth your employer on LinkedIn.** Not in the announcement. Not in the comments. Not in DMs. It reflects poorly on you, not them.
- **Don't slack off during notice period.** How you leave is how you're remembered. Finish strong.
- **Don't take company property or data.** No client lists, no proprietary documents, no code repositories. This can be grounds for legal action.
- **Don't burn bridges with individuals.** You might work with these people again. Industries are small.
- **Don't announce on social media before telling your manager.** The order matters: manager first, then HR, then team, then the world.
- **Don't forget to negotiate your departure.** Unused vacation payout, bonus proration, extended benefits, and reference letters are all negotiable during your notice period.

## Departure Checklist

- [ ] Signed offer letter in hand (before any resignation conversation)
- [ ] Reviewed employment contract for notice period, non-compete, clawback clauses
- [ ] Told manager in person or by call (before anyone else)
- [ ] Sent formal resignation letter by email
- [ ] Negotiated departure terms (vacation payout, bonus, references)
- [ ] Created transition documentation for your replacement
- [ ] Backed up personal files (only personal, not company data)
- [ ] Updated LinkedIn profile
- [ ] Sent farewell message to team
- [ ] Connected with colleagues on personal channels (personal email, LinkedIn)
- [ ] Returned all company equipment on or before last day
- [ ] Confirmed benefits end date and COBRA/continuing coverage options

---

## Key Takeaway

How you leave a job matters as much as how you start one. These scripts help you resign with professionalism, handle counter-offers with clarity, and announce your transition with confidence. Customize the [BRACKETS], practice the conversation before you have it, and remember: you're not just closing a chapter. You're writing the first impression for everyone who hears about it. Make it a good one.
