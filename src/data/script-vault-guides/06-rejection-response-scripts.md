# Rejection Response Scripts

**10 word-for-word scripts for responding to rejection with grace, requesting feedback, and turning a no into a future yes.**

> **Disclaimer:** These scripts are starting points you can adapt. Not all employers will respond to feedback requests or stay in touch. These scripts maximize your chances, but no specific outcome is guaranteed.

You don't need to respond to every rejection. Use these when the opportunity or relationship is worth preserving.

**Time estimate:** 10 minutes to read, then reference as needed
**What you'll need:** The rejection email or context, and 24 hours of emotional distance before sending anything

---

## The 24-Hour Rule

Do not respond to a rejection in the first 24 hours. Your emotional brain will draft a message your professional brain would never send.

Wait a day. Feel the feelings. Then open this guide and pick the right script.

## Graceful Responses

### Script 1: The Standard Graceful Response

```
Subject: Re: [ROLE TITLE] — Thank you

Hi [FIRST NAME],

Thank you for letting me know. While I'm disappointed, I
appreciated the opportunity to learn about [COMPANY] and the
team.

If anything changes with this role or if a similar position
opens up, I'd welcome the chance to be considered again.

Wishing you and the team all the best.

Best,
[YOUR NAME]
```

### Script 2: The Graceful Response (After Multiple Rounds)

```
Subject: Re: [ROLE TITLE] — Thank you for the experience

Hi [FIRST NAME],

Thank you for the update. I won't pretend I'm not disappointed,
as I was genuinely excited about this opportunity and the team.

That said, I want you to know that the interview process was
one of the best I've experienced. [SPECIFIC POSITIVE: the
questions were thoughtful / the team was welcoming / the
transparency about the role was refreshing].

I'd love to stay in touch. If something opens up that aligns
with my background, I hope you'll keep me in mind.

Thanks again for your time throughout the process.

Best,
[YOUR NAME]
```

### Script 3: The Very Brief Response

```
Hi [FIRST NAME],

Thanks for letting me know. I appreciate the opportunity and
wish you well in filling the role.

Best,
[YOUR NAME]
```

Use this when you don't have the energy for more, or when the role wasn't a strong fit anyway. Short and professional is always appropriate.

## Feedback Requests

Most employers won't give specific feedback (legal departments discourage it). But some will, especially after later-stage interviews. It costs nothing to ask.

### Script 4: Requesting Feedback (After Interview)

```
Subject: Re: [ROLE TITLE] — Feedback request

Hi [FIRST NAME],

Thank you for the update. I respect the decision.

If you're able to share any feedback on my interview
performance or application, I'd genuinely appreciate it.
I'm actively working on improving my candidacy and any
specific insight would be valuable.

I understand if company policy doesn't allow this. Either
way, thank you for the opportunity.

Best,
[YOUR NAME]
```

### Script 5: Requesting Feedback (From a Recruiter)

```
Hi [FIRST NAME],

Thank you for letting me know about the [ROLE TITLE] decision.

I'd value any feedback you can share about where I fell short
or what the successful candidate brought that differentiated
them. I want to be a stronger candidate next time.

If there are other roles in your pipeline that might be a
better fit for my background, I'd love to stay on your radar.

Thanks,
[YOUR NAME]
```

### Script 6: When They Give Vague Feedback

Sometimes they'll say: "We went with someone who was a closer fit."

```
Hi [FIRST NAME],

Thank you for that context. To help me improve for future
opportunities, could you share anything more specific? For
example:

- Was there a skill or experience gap?
- Was it related to the interview itself?
- Was the other candidate internal or had specific
  experience I was missing?

Anything you can share would help me target my search more
effectively. I understand if you can't go into detail.

Thanks again,
[YOUR NAME]
```

## Turning No Into Future Yes

### Script 7: Staying on Their Radar

```
Subject: Re: [ROLE TITLE] — Staying connected

Hi [FIRST NAME],

Thank you for the transparency throughout this process.
While this one didn't work out, I remain very interested
in [COMPANY].

I'd like to stay connected. Would it be alright if I checked
in every [2-3 MONTHS] in case something new opens up? I don't
want to be a pest, just persistent.

In the meantime, I'll keep following [COMPANY]'s work on
[SPECIFIC INITIATIVE]. Excited to see where it goes.

Best,
[YOUR NAME]
```

### Script 8: The "Keep Me in Mind" Close

```
Hi [FIRST NAME],

Completely understand the decision. For future reference,
here's a quick summary of what I bring in case another role
opens up:

- [X YEARS] in [FIELD]
- Deep experience in [SPECIFIC SKILL 1] and [SPECIFIC SKILL 2]
- Track record of [KEY ACCOMPLISHMENT]

If anything comes up that matches this profile, I'd love to
be one of the first people you reach out to.

Thanks for the opportunity.

Best,
[YOUR NAME]
```

### Script 9: Following Up 2-3 Months Later

```
Subject: Checking in — [YOUR NAME]

Hi [FIRST NAME],

I interviewed for the [ROLE TITLE] back in [MONTH] and you
kindly said to stay in touch.

Since then, I've [BRIEF UPDATE: completed a certification,
taken on a project, gained experience in relevant area].
I remain very interested in [COMPANY] and wanted to check
if any roles have opened up that might be a fit.

Happy to send an updated resume if helpful.

Thanks for keeping me in mind.

Best,
[YOUR NAME]
```

### Script 10: When the Same Role Re-Opens

```
Subject: [ROLE TITLE] — re-expressing interest

Hi [FIRST NAME],

I noticed the [ROLE TITLE] has been posted again. I interviewed
for this role in [MONTH] and made it to [STAGE REACHED].

Since then, I've [SPECIFIC IMPROVEMENT OR NEW EXPERIENCE THAT
ADDRESSES PREVIOUS GAP]. I believe I'm an even stronger
candidate now.

I'd love the opportunity to be reconsidered. I've submitted
a fresh application through the portal. Would a conversation
be possible?

Thanks,
[YOUR NAME]
```

## What Not to Do

- **Don't respond emotionally.** Even if the rejection was handled poorly. Your response is a professional document.
- **Don't argue the decision.** They've made their choice. Pushing back doesn't change it and burns the bridge.
- **Don't post about it on LinkedIn.** Vague posts about "a company that didn't value me" are transparent and off-putting to other hiring managers.
- **Don't ghost them.** A brief response keeps the relationship alive for future opportunities.
- **Don't demand an explanation.** Ask politely for feedback. Accept whatever they give (or don't give).
- **Don't take it personally.** Hiring is messy, subjective, and influenced by dozens of factors you'll never know about.

## The Rejection Reframe

Every rejection is one step closer to the right fit. Not in a motivational poster way. In a math way. The job market works on conversion rates. X applications lead to Y interviews lead to Z offers. Every rejection moves you through the funnel.

The person who handles rejection with grace stands out. Hiring managers remember candidates who responded professionally to a no. When the next role opens, guess who they think of first?

---

## Key Takeaway

Responding to rejection well is a competitive advantage. Most candidates either ghost or respond emotionally. These scripts position you as professional, resilient, and memorable. Wait 24 hours, pick the right script, customize the [BRACKETS], and send. Then update your tracker, close the loop, and move forward. The right opportunity is ahead of you, not behind you.
