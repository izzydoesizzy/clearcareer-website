# Energy Management Dashboard

**Stop managing your time. Start managing your energy.**

Time management does not work for ADHD brains. You can block 3 hours on your calendar for applications, but if your energy is at zero, those 3 hours produce nothing except guilt. Energy management works differently: you figure out when your brain is ON, match your hardest tasks to those windows, and protect those windows like your job search depends on it. Because it does.

**Time:** 20 minutes to map your energy patterns. 5 minutes per day to check in. **What you'll need:** A week of honest self-observation and this guide.

---

**Your peak window:** The time(s) with the highest average. This is when your brain is most capable of demanding work.

**Your valley:** The time(s) with the lowest average. This is when you should rest or do mindless tasks.

### Common ADHD Energy Patterns

**The Morning Zombie:** Energy is low until 10-11 AM, peaks midday, crashes in the afternoon, sometimes rebounds in the evening. Medication (if used) often creates a mid-morning activation.

**The Night Owl:** Low energy all morning, builds through the afternoon, peaks after dinner. Your best work happens after 8 PM.

**The Sprint-Crash:** Intense bursts of energy followed by complete crashes. No predictable daily pattern, but cycles of 2-3 high days followed by 1-2 low days.

**The Slow Build:** Starts low, builds throughout the day, peaks in late afternoon or evening. Consistent but your best work comes later.

## Step 2: Match Tasks to Energy

### Peak Energy Tasks (Score 4-5)
These require focus, creativity, and decision-making. Do them during your peak window only.

- Writing and customizing cover letters
- Tailoring your resume for specific applications
- Preparing for interviews (practicing answers out loud)
- Writing LinkedIn posts or messages
- Making phone calls or having networking conversations
- Strategic planning (deciding where to apply, who to contact)

### Medium Energy Tasks (Score 3)
These require some attention but not deep focus.

- Researching companies
- Reading job postings and evaluating fit
- Updating your tracking spreadsheet
- Browsing industry news or LinkedIn
- Following up on existing conversations
- Organizing documents and files

### Low Energy Tasks (Score 1-2)
These require minimal cognitive effort.

- Saving job postings to review later
- Scrolling LinkedIn to find people to contact (just saving names, not messaging)
- Filing emails into folders
- Updating your CRM with notes
- Listening to a podcast about your industry
- Rest. Actual rest. Not "I should be doing something" rest.

## Step 4: The Energy Check-In

Every morning before you start, spend 30 seconds checking your energy:

**Today's energy (1-5):** ___

**If 4-5:** Great. Do your peak energy tasks first. Ride the wave.

**If 3:** Medium day. Skip the hardest tasks. Focus on medium-energy activities. Protect your energy for one high-value action.

**If 1-2:** Survival day. Do your minimum viable action (one application, one message, one thing for yourself) and then rest. Tomorrow will be better.

**The critical principle:** On low-energy days, doing less is the strategy. Pushing through when your tank is empty produces terrible applications, embarrassing networking messages, and emotional spiraling. One decent action on a low day beats 5 terrible actions.

## Energy Boosters and Drainers

### Quick Energy Boosters (5-15 minutes)
- Walk around the block (movement metabolizes stress hormones)
- Cold water on your face or wrists (activates the dive reflex, calms nervous system)
- One song, played loud, with dancing (dopamine hit)
- 5 minutes of sunlight (regulates circadian rhythm and mood)
- A snack with protein (blood sugar stability)
- Talking to a real human for 5 minutes (social regulation)
- Changing your physical location (novelty stimulates the brain)

### Common Energy Drainers
- Scrolling social media (feels like rest, is actually depletion)
- Comparing yourself to other job seekers online
- Sitting in one position for more than 90 minutes
- Skipping meals (blood sugar crash wrecks ADHD symptoms)
- Dehydration (even mild dehydration impairs cognitive function)
- Unstructured time (ADHD brains often cannot rest when there is no structure)
- Decision fatigue (making too many choices in one session)

### The 90-Minute Rule

ADHD brains cannot sustain focused effort for more than 60-90 minutes without a break. Do not try. Instead:

- Work for 25-50 minutes (set a timer)
- Break for 5-10 minutes (move, hydrate, step outside)
- Repeat 2-3 times
- Take a longer break (15-30 minutes) after 90 minutes total

This is not a weakness. It is your operating manual. Respect it.

## The Weekly Energy Budget

Think of your energy as a weekly budget, not a daily one.

You have approximately 15-20 hours of productive energy per week (varies by person and medication). Not 40. Not even 30. Fifteen to twenty hours of actual, quality output.

Spend those hours wisely:

| Activity | Weekly Energy Budget |
|----------|---------------------|
| Job applications (customized) | 5-8 hours |
| Networking and messages | 2-3 hours |
| Interview prep | 2-3 hours (more in weeks with interviews) |
| Research and planning | 2-3 hours |
| Admin and tracking | 1 hour |
| **Total** | **12-18 hours** |

If you spend 18 quality hours on your job search this week, you had an excellent week. Do not compare yourself to people who claim to search for 40 hours. They are either lying or inefficient.

---

## Key Takeaway

Energy management is not about doing more. It is about doing the right things at the right times. Your brain has a limited supply of productive energy each day. Match your tasks to your energy, protect your peak windows, and rest without guilt during your valleys.

The job search is a marathon. Energy management is how you finish it without burning out.
