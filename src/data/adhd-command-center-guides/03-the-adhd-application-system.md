# The ADHD Application System

**Stop the chaos. Apply to jobs in batches, not binges.**

The typical ADHD job application pattern looks like this: you spend 4 hours on a Tuesday hyperfocusing on applications, send 8 of them, feel amazing, then do not apply to anything for 2 weeks because you are drained. Or you open a job posting, start customizing your resume, get distracted, open another posting, start that one, and end the day with 6 half-finished applications and zero submitted.

This system fixes both problems. It uses visual tracking, batch processing, and 15-minute sprints to make applications consistent and sustainable.

**Time:** 20 minutes to set up the system. 15-minute sprints to use it. **What you'll need:** A spreadsheet (Google Sheets or Excel), a timer, and the templates in this guide.

---

## The Visual Application Tracker

ADHD brains respond to what they can see. A list buried in a spreadsheet is invisible. A visual board is motivating.

### Option 1: Kanban Board (Best for Visual Thinkers)

Create 5 columns in Trello, Notion, or even sticky notes on a wall:

| Saved | Ready to Apply | Applied | Interview | Decision |
|-------|---------------|---------|-----------|----------|
| Jobs you found and want to evaluate | Jobs you have researched and decided to apply to | Applications you have submitted | Active interview processes | Offers, rejections, pending |

Move each job from left to right. The visual movement is satisfying. Seeing jobs progress through the pipeline gives your brain the novelty and reward it craves.

### Option 2: Spreadsheet Tracker (Best for Data Thinkers)

| Company | Role | Link | Status | Date Found | Date Applied | Follow-Up Date | Contact Person | Notes |
|---------|------|------|--------|-----------|-------------|----------------|----------------|-------|
| | | | | | | | | |

**Status options:** Saved / Researching / Ready / Applied / Phone Screen / Interview / Offer / Rejected / Ghosted

**Color-code the status column.** Green for active. Yellow for waiting. Red for rejected. Gray for ghosted. The colors give instant visual feedback.

## The Batch Processing Method

Instead of doing everything for one application (research, customize resume, write cover letter, apply) all at once, batch similar tasks together.

### Batch 1: The Scan (Monday, 15 minutes)
Find 5-10 job postings that look interesting. Save them to your tracker. Do not evaluate them yet. Just collect.

### Batch 2: The Filter (Tuesday, 15 minutes)
Go through your saved postings. For each one, ask:
- Does this role match my target? (Yes/No)
- Do I meet 80% of the qualifications? (Yes/No)
- Is this company somewhere I would actually work? (Yes/No)

If all three are yes, move it to "Ready to Apply." If any are no, delete it. Fast decisions. Do not deliberate.

### Batch 3: The Customize (Wednesday/Thursday, 15-minute sprints)
Pick 2-3 "Ready" jobs. For each one, spend 15 minutes:
- Tailor your resume summary and top 3-5 bullets to match the posting
- Write or customize a cover letter (use a template, swap out the specifics)
- Save the customized documents

### Batch 4: The Submit (Thursday/Friday, 15 minutes)
Submit the applications you prepared. Do not re-read them 10 times. Do not second-guess. Hit submit.

### Batch 5: The Follow-Up (Following Monday, 10 minutes)
Review your "Applied" column. For anything older than 7 days with no response, add to your follow-up list.

## The 15-Minute Sprint

Every batch uses 15-minute sprints. Here is the exact protocol.

### Before the Sprint
1. Close every browser tab except the ones you need
2. Put your phone in another room or face-down on silent
3. Open your tracker
4. Write on a sticky note: "In the next 15 minutes, I will ________________"
5. Start the timer

### During the Sprint
- Do only the thing on the sticky note
- When a tangent thought appears (and it will), write it on your parking lot notepad and go back to the task
- When the timer goes off, stop. Even if you are mid-sentence. Stop.

### After the Sprint
- Take a 5-minute break (stand up, move, hydrate)
- Decide: do another sprint, or switch to a different task?
- Update your tracker

### The Sprint Math

2 sprints per day = 30 minutes
5 days per week = 2.5 hours
2.5 hours per week = 5-8 quality applications

That is a sustainable, productive job search. Not 8 hours of misery. Thirty minutes of focused sprints.

## Application Templates

### Resume Customization Template (5-Minute Version)

You do not need to rewrite your entire resume for every application. Customize three things:

1. **Professional Summary:** Change the role descriptor and top 1-2 skills to match the posting.
2. **Top 3 Bullets:** Under your most recent role, reorder or rephrase your top 3 bullets to mirror the posting's top requirements.
3. **Skills Section:** Rearrange skills so the ones mentioned in the posting appear first.

Save a master resume. Duplicate it for each application. Make the 3 changes. Done.

### Cover Letter Template

```
Dear [HIRING MANAGER / Hiring Team],

I am writing about the [ROLE TITLE] position at [COMPANY NAME]. With [X YEARS] of experience in [RELEVANT AREA], I am confident I can contribute to [SPECIFIC GOAL OR CHALLENGE FROM THE POSTING].

[ONE PARAGRAPH: Your strongest relevant achievement, with numbers. Match it to their top requirement.]

[ONE PARAGRAPH: Why this company. Reference something specific you learned from researching them.]

I would welcome the chance to discuss how my background aligns with your needs. Thank you for your time.

[YOUR NAME]
```

Keep it to 3-4 paragraphs. Under 200 words. Hiring managers skim cover letters. Short and specific beats long and detailed.

## Dealing with Application Overwhelm

### The "Too Many Options" Freeze
When you see 50 interesting postings and cannot decide which to apply to:
- Pick the 3 with the closest deadline or most recent posting date
- Ignore the rest for now
- You can always come back to them next week

### The "Customization Paralysis" Freeze
When you cannot bring yourself to customize your resume for the 4th time:
- Use the 5-minute version (summary, 3 bullets, skills reorder)
- A slightly customized application beats a perfect application you never send
- 80% customized and submitted always beats 100% customized and sitting in your drafts

### The "This Application Is Not Good Enough" Spiral
When you re-read your application 15 times and keep finding flaws:
- Set a rule: read it twice, maximum. Then submit.
- Remember: no application is perfect. The goal is good enough to get a conversation.
- Ask yourself: "If I spend 30 more minutes on this, will it meaningfully change whether I get an interview?" Almost always, the answer is no.

## Weekly Application Targets

| Search Intensity | Applications/Week | Time/Week |
|-----------------|-------------------|-----------|
| Low (employed, casually looking) | 2-3 | 1-2 hours |
| Medium (actively searching) | 4-6 | 2-3 hours |
| High (full-time searching) | 6-10 | 3-5 hours |

**These are quality, customized applications.** Not mass-apply-with-one-resume applications. Two great applications are worth more than 20 generic ones.

---

## Key Takeaway

The ADHD application system works because it removes the two things that kill ADHD productivity: decision fatigue and sustained effort. Batch processing removes decisions (you already decided what to apply to in the scan batch). Fifteen-minute sprints remove sustained effort (you only need to focus for 15 minutes at a time).

Set up the tracker. Process in batches. Sprint for 15 minutes. Submit and move on.
