# Interview Prep for ADHD Brains

**Handle time blindness, brain blanks, fidgeting, and the anxiety spiral.**

Interviews are hard for everyone. For ADHD brains, they are hard in specific, predictable ways. You lose track of time and ramble for 5 minutes on a question that needed 90 seconds. You blank on an answer you rehearsed 10 times. You fidget in your chair and wonder if they noticed. Your brain goes on a tangent mid-answer and you cannot find your way back.

These are not character flaws. They are ADHD symptoms. And every one of them has a workaround.

**Time:** 30 minutes to set up your launch pad. 15 minutes per practice session. **What you'll need:** Your STAR story bank, a timer, and a fidget object.

---

## The Interview Launch Pad

The launch pad is a one-page document you review 30 minutes before every interview. It contains everything you need in one place so your ADHD brain does not have to retrieve it from memory under pressure.

### Build Your Launch Pad

Print or save this on your phone:

```
INTERVIEW LAUNCH PAD

Company: ________________
Role: ________________
Interviewer(s): ________________
Time: ________________ (set alarm for 15 min before)

My "Tell me about yourself" (key points, not a script):
- Present: ________________
- Past: ________________
- Why here: ________________

My 3 best stories for this role:
1. ________________ (shows: ________________)
2. ________________ (shows: ________________)
3. ________________ (shows: ________________)

My 3 questions to ask:
1. ________________
2. ________________
3. ________________

One thing I know about this company:
________________

One thing I am genuinely excited about:
________________
```

Read this once, 30 minutes before the interview. Then close it. You are loaded. Trust the prep.

## Handling Time Blindness

ADHD brains have no internal clock. A 90-second answer feels like 30 seconds. A 5-minute ramble feels like 2 minutes. This is the single most common interview problem for ADHD adults.

### The Timer Method (Virtual Interviews)

- Place a small clock or phone timer at the bottom of your screen, visible to you but not the camera
- Glance at it after each answer
- Target: 60-120 seconds per answer for standard questions, up to 2 minutes for behavioral questions

### The Physical Cue Method (In-Person Interviews)

- Wear a watch. Glance at it naturally between questions.
- When you start a story, note the minute. If more than 2 minutes have passed, wrap up.

### The Self-Interrupt Method

If you notice yourself rambling, use this phrase:

"But to bring it back to your question, the key point is..."

Then state your conclusion in one sentence. Interviewers respect concise answers. They do not respect 5-minute monologues, no matter how interesting.

### The Answer Framework

Use this internal structure for every answer. It prevents rambling by giving your brain a track to follow:

1. **Answer the question directly** (1 sentence)
2. **Give the context** (1-2 sentences)
3. **Tell the story** (3-4 sentences of action)
4. **State the result** (1 sentence)
5. **Stop talking.**

Total: 6-8 sentences. About 60-90 seconds spoken.

## Handling Brain Blanks

Your interviewer asks a question. Your brain goes completely empty. You panic. The panic makes it worse. This is ADHD working memory failure under stress, and it happens to almost every ADHD adult in high-pressure situations.

### Prevention: The Story Index

On your launch pad, you have 3 stories. But create a mental index of all 10-12 stories in your bank, organized by theme:

- Leadership story: ________________
- Failure story: ________________
- Conflict story: ________________
- Under pressure story: ________________
- Initiative story: ________________

When a question comes, your brain only needs to match the theme, not generate a story from scratch.

### When It Happens Anyway

**Buy time with an honest bridge:**
- "That is a great question. Let me think about the best example for a moment."
- "I have a few examples I could share. Let me pick the most relevant one."

Then pause for 5-10 seconds. This feels like an eternity. It is not. The interviewer perceives it as thoughtfulness.

**If you are still blank after 10 seconds:**
- "I have not encountered that exact situation, but here is a related experience..." Then tell your closest story.
- This is perfectly acceptable. Most interviewers care more about your thinking process than a perfect-match answer.

**If you blank mid-story:**
- Pause. Take a breath.
- "Where was I going with this... Right." Then summarize your conclusion.
- Or: "The short version is: [RESULT]."
- Ending early with a clear conclusion is better than floundering to find where you were.

## Fidget Strategies

ADHD brains need physical stimulation to maintain focus. Sitting perfectly still for an hour is not realistic and attempting it will drain cognitive resources you need for answering questions.

### For Virtual Interviews
- Fidget object below camera view (stress ball, smooth stone, putty)
- Feet movement under desk (tap, press toes into floor, bounce gently)
- Pen in hand (clicking or rolling is fine off-camera)
- Doodle pad below camera (drawing simple shapes while listening helps some ADHD brains focus)

### For In-Person Interviews
- Hold a pen (gives your hands something to do)
- Sit with your feet flat on the floor (grounding)
- Press your thumb and index finger together under the table (invisible stimming)
- Bring a portfolio or notebook to hold and reference

### What NOT to Do
- Do not bounce your leg visibly (distracting)
- Do not click a pen audibly (annoying)
- Do not fidget with your hair or face (reads as anxiety)
- Do not spin in a swivel chair (reads as disinterest)

The goal: enough physical stimulation to keep your brain engaged, without being visible or distracting to the interviewer.

## The Pre-Interview Anxiety Spiral

ADHD amplifies pre-interview anxiety through time distortion and catastrophic thinking. Here is the protocol.

### The Night Before
- Review your launch pad once. Close it.
- Set two alarms (one to wake up, one to start prep)
- Do a 10-minute "worry download" (write every worry on paper, then close the notebook)
- Cutoff: no interview prep after 8 PM. Watch a show. Read. Play a game.

### The Morning Of
- Move your body for 10 minutes (walk, stretch, pushups)
- Eat something with protein
- Review your launch pad one final time
- Take your medication at the usual time (do not change your routine for the interview)

### 10 Minutes Before
- Box breathing: 4 counts in, 4 counts hold, 4 counts out, 4 counts hold. Repeat 4 times.
- Reframe: "I am not nervous. I am energized."
- Grounding: 5 things you see, 4 you touch, 3 you hear, 2 you smell, 1 you taste.
- Read the first two lines of your launch pad to prime your brain.

### If the Anxiety Spikes During the Interview
- Plant your feet flat on the floor (grounding)
- Slow your speaking pace (anxiety speeds you up)
- Take a sip of water (gives you a 3-second pause)
- Remind yourself: "They already liked me enough to schedule this interview"

## Brain-Blank Recovery Scripts

Memorize these phrases. They will save you.

**"Let me think about that for a moment."** (Buys 5-10 seconds)

**"Can you repeat the question? I want to make sure I address it directly."** (Buys 10-15 seconds and ensures you answer the right thing)

**"To be honest, I'm drawing a blank on a specific example. Can we come back to that one?"** (Redirects. Most interviewers will say yes.)

**"I haven't encountered that exact scenario, but here's how I would approach it..."** (Pivots to a hypothetical, which is acceptable for unusual questions)

**"Let me bring that back to the key point..."** (Recovers from a tangent)

## Practice Protocol for ADHD Brains

### Round 1: Solo, With Timer (20 minutes)
- Set a 2-minute timer
- Ask yourself a question from your list
- Answer out loud
- Stop when the timer goes off, even if you are not done
- Do 5-7 questions

### Round 2: Recorded (15 minutes)
- Record yourself on your phone
- Answer 3-4 questions
- Watch back and note: rambling, filler words ("um," "like"), tangents, unclear endings
- Re-record the worst one until it is clean

### Round 3: With a Partner (30 minutes)
- Ask someone to drill you with random questions
- Practice selecting the right story in real time
- Practice the time-check habit
- Get feedback: "Did I ramble? Did I answer the question? Was it too long?"

**One practice session per week is enough.** More than that and you over-rehearse, which makes you sound robotic. You want prepared, not scripted.

---

## Key Takeaway

ADHD interview challenges are predictable. Time blindness, brain blanks, fidgeting, and anxiety spirals all have workarounds. The launch pad gives you structure. The timer gives you boundaries. The fidget strategies give you focus. The recovery scripts give you a safety net.

You do not need to be a perfect interviewer. You need to be a prepared one who knows how to recover when ADHD throws you a curveball. That is what this guide gives you.
