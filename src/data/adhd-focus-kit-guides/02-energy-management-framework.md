# Energy Management Framework

**How to work with your brain's natural rhythms instead of fighting them every day of your job search.**

> **Disclaimer:** This guide offers productivity strategies tailored for ADHD-style energy patterns. It is not medical advice. If you're experiencing chronic fatigue, please consult a healthcare provider.

You don't need to do everything today. Start by tracking your energy for 3 days before making any changes.

**Time estimate:** 15 minutes to read, then 3 days of tracking
**What you'll need:** A simple note on your phone for energy tracking

---

## Forget Time Management. Manage Energy.

Time management assumes all hours are equal. They're not. Especially with ADHD.

You probably have 2-3 hours per day where your brain actually cooperates. Where focus comes easier, decisions feel clearer, and the resistance to starting is lower. The rest of the day is varying degrees of fog, distraction, and executive dysfunction.

Most people waste their peak hours on email, LinkedIn scrolling, and "getting organized." Then they try to do their hardest work (customizing applications, prepping for interviews, networking) during their worst hours.

Flip it.

---

## Finding Your Peak Window

For 3 days, rate your energy and focus every 2 hours on a 1-10 scale. Just a quick number in your phone's notes app.

| 12 PM | | | |
| 2 PM | | | |
| 4 PM | | | |
| 6 PM | | | |
| 8 PM | | | |

After 3 days, the pattern will be obvious. Most people with ADHD find one of these profiles:

**Morning Launcher:** Peak energy 8-11 AM, crashes after lunch, minor revival 7-9 PM.

**Slow Starter:** Fog until 11 AM, peak energy 11 AM-2 PM, gradual decline after.

**Night Owl:** Low energy all morning, functional afternoon, peak 8 PM-midnight.

**Scattered:** No clear peak, but micro-bursts of 30-45 minutes scattered throughout the day.

None of these are wrong. They're your brain's operating system. Work with it.

## The Energy-Matched Schedule

Once you know your peak window, protect it for your hardest tasks.

### Peak Hours (2-3 hours)

**Do:** Application customization, cover letter writing, interview prep, networking calls, anything that requires sustained attention and clear thinking.

**Don't:** Check email, scroll LinkedIn, organize files, update your tracker. These are low-energy tasks pretending to be productive.

### Moderate Hours (2-3 hours)

**Do:** Research companies, save job postings, light networking (commenting on posts, sending brief messages), updating your tracker.

**Don't:** Attempt deep work. Your brain won't cooperate and you'll feel frustrated.

### Low Hours (remaining time)

**Do:** Admin tasks, reading industry articles, light exercise, errands, life maintenance.

**Don't:** Try to be productive at job searching. Low energy plus job search equals frustration and shame. Release yourself.

**Action:** Write out your energy-matched schedule based on your 3-day tracking. Post it where you can see it.

## Body Doubling: Borrowed Focus

Body doubling is having another person present while you work. Not helping you. Not watching you. Just existing in the same space.

This sounds ridiculous. It works exceptionally well for ADHD brains.

The science: your brain's attention system partially regulates through social cues. Another person working quietly nearby creates a gentle external accountability signal that helps your prefrontal cortex stay engaged.

### In-Person Body Doubling

- Work from a coffee shop or library during your peak hours
- Ask your partner to work in the same room (they don't need to be doing the same thing)
- Find a friend who also needs to get work done and sit together
- Use a co-working space (many have day passes for $15-30)

### Virtual Body Doubling

- **Focusmate (free tier available):** 25 or 50-minute video sessions with a stranger who's also working. You state your goal at the start and check in at the end.
- **Study-with-me livestreams on YouTube:** Lower-commitment version. Put one on a second screen.
- **Discord productivity servers:** Communities where people screen-share or camera-share while working.

**Action:** Try one body doubling session this week during your peak hours. Notice the difference.

## The Sensory-Friendly Prep Kit

ADHD brains are often sensory-sensitive. Your environment directly impacts your ability to focus. Before important tasks (interviews especially), set up your sensory environment.

### For Focus Work (Applications, Prep)

- **Sound:** Brown noise, lo-fi beats, or silence. Not music with lyrics. Not podcasts. Your working memory can't handle language input from two sources.
- **Lighting:** Natural light if possible. Avoid harsh overhead fluorescents.
- **Temperature:** Slightly cool is better for focus than warm.
- **Desk:** Clear everything except what you need for this task. Visual clutter is cognitive clutter for ADHD brains.
- **Phone:** In another room. Not on silent. Not face down. In another room.

### For Interviews

- **30 minutes before:** Low stimulation. No news, no social media, no stressful conversations.
- **Sound:** Silence or very soft background music to prevent your brain from seeking stimulation.
- **Fidget tool:** Keep something in your hand under the desk (smooth stone, rubber band, putty). Subtle movement helps ADHD brains focus during conversations.
- **Water and snack:** Low blood sugar amplifies every ADHD symptom. Eat protein 30 minutes before.
- **Notes visible:** Write your key talking points on a sticky note attached to your screen (for video interviews) or on a notecard in your lap (for phone screens). ADHD working memory will blank under stress. Offload to paper.

### For Networking Events

- **Arrive early** when it's quiet and low-stimulation. Walking into a crowded, noisy room is overwhelming.
- **Set a time limit** before you go. "I'm staying for 45 minutes." Knowing the end point reduces the energy cost.
- **Have an exit phrase** ready: "I've really enjoyed talking with you. I'm going to make my way around a bit more. Can I connect with you on LinkedIn?"
- **Decompress after.** Social events drain ADHD brains faster. Plan 30 minutes of quiet time afterward.

## The Energy Deposit System

Think of your daily energy as a bank account. Everything you do is either a deposit or a withdrawal.

**Common deposits:**
- Exercise (especially morning movement)
- Time outside
- Good sleep (the biggest deposit of all)
- Social connection with safe people
- Completing a small task
- Fun and play
- Eating protein

**Common withdrawals:**
- Decision-making (every decision costs energy, even small ones)
- Rejection or ghosting
- Social media scrolling
- Financial stress
- Cluttered environment
- Skipped meals
- Poor sleep
- Social obligations you didn't choose

**The rule:** Before every job search day, make at least one energy deposit before your first withdrawal. Walk for 15 minutes. Eat breakfast. Step outside. Load the account before you start spending.

**Action:** List your top 3 energy deposits and top 3 energy drains. Increase the deposits. Reduce the drains. Adjust weekly.

## The Transition Tax

For ADHD brains, transitioning between activities costs significant energy. Going from "relaxing" to "job searching" isn't a flip of a switch. It's a 15-30 minute warm-up process that feels like pushing through mud.

### Reducing the Transition Tax

- **Use the same workspace** for job searching every day. Your brain creates environmental cues. Same chair, same desk, same setup. This reduces the warm-up time.
- **Create a start ritual** that's the same every time. Example: sit down, put on headphones, open your tracker, pick one task, set a 25-minute timer. The ritual becomes a trigger.
- **Don't go from zero to sixty.** Start with a 5-minute warm-up task (review yesterday's notes, check one email) before your main work.
- **Avoid interruptions during peak hours.** Every interruption resets the transition clock. If your peak window is 10 AM-12 PM, that time is sacred.

## Sleep: The Non-Negotiable

Poor sleep makes every ADHD symptom worse. Every single one. Focus, emotional regulation, impulse control, time perception, motivation. All of them degrade with insufficient sleep.

During job search, sleep is often the first thing to go. Anxiety keeps you up. The lack of morning structure removes your wake-up anchor. Revenge bedtime procrastination (staying up late because the evening feels like the only time you control) steals hours.

**The minimum sleep protocol:**

1. Same wake time every day (even weekends). This is more important than bedtime.
2. No screens 30 minutes before bed. Read a physical book, stretch, or listen to something calming.
3. No caffeine after 2 PM. ADHD brains metabolize caffeine differently and it can interfere with sleep far later than you'd expect.
4. Dark, cool room. 65-68 degrees F is optimal for most people.
5. If you can't fall asleep in 20 minutes, get up and do something boring for 10 minutes. Then try again. Don't lie there fighting it.

---

## Key Takeaway

ADHD energy is not broken. It's variable. Some hours are diamonds. Some hours are gravel. The job seekers who struggle most are the ones trying to turn gravel hours into diamond hours through willpower. Stop doing that. Find your peak window, protect it fiercely, match your tasks to your energy levels, and build deposits before making withdrawals. You don't need more hours. You need to use the good ones better.
