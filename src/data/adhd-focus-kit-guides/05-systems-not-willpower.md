# Systems Not Willpower

**How to build external scaffolding that does the remembering, organizing, and deciding for you.**

> **Disclaimer:** This guide provides productivity system recommendations for ADHD-style brains. It is not medical advice. If executive function challenges are significantly impacting your life, please consult a healthcare professional.

You don't need to set up everything today. Pick one system from this guide. Get it working. Add more later.

**Time estimate:** 20 minutes to read, then 30 minutes to set up your first system
**What you'll need:** Your phone, a notebook, and the willingness to stop trusting your memory

---

## The Core Principle

Your ADHD brain is brilliant at many things: creativity, pattern recognition, crisis performance, connecting ideas, reading people, and generating energy when interest is high.

Your ADHD brain is terrible at: remembering, sequencing, starting, tracking, maintaining, and following through consistently over time.

Stop asking your brain to do the things it's bad at. Build external systems that handle those functions instead. This is not a crutch. It's a prosthetic for a real deficit. You wouldn't tell someone with poor vision to "just try harder to see." You'd give them glasses.

These systems are your glasses.

## System 1: The Launch Pad

A launch pad is a physical space where everything you need for your job search lives. Always in the same place. Always ready.

### Physical Launch Pad

Designate one spot (desk, table, specific chair) as your job search station. On it or near it, always:

- Laptop (charged)
- Notebook and pen
- Today's sticky note with your ONE priority
- Water bottle
- Headphones
- Your printed daily schedule
- Your evidence file (if physical)

**The rule:** Never move these items. They live here. When you sit down at your launch pad, your brain receives the signal: "It's time to work on the job search." No decision required.

### Digital Launch Pad

On your laptop, create a browser bookmark folder called "Job Search" with:

- Your application tracker (spreadsheet or Trello)
- Your top 3 job boards (bookmarked, not searched fresh each time)
- Your resume (cloud link, always current version)
- Your cover letter template
- LinkedIn (direct to messages, not the feed)

**Start every session** by opening this folder. Not email. Not LinkedIn feed. Not news. The launch pad. This eliminates the "what should I do first?" paralysis.

**Action:** Set up your physical and digital launch pads today. Spend 15 minutes organizing each.

## System 2: The One-Page Tracker

Complex tracking systems die within days for ADHD brains. You need the simplest possible tracker that captures enough information to be useful.

### The Spreadsheet (Google Sheets or Excel)

One sheet. Six columns. That's it.

| Company | Role | Date Applied | Status | Next Action | Deadline |
|---------|------|-------------|--------|-------------|----------|
| Acme Co | Marketing Manager | April 3 | Applied | Follow up | April 10 |
| Widget Inc | Brand Lead | April 5 | Phone Screen | Prep answers | April 8 |
| TechCorp | Content Dir | April 7 | Rejected | None | -- |

**Status options:** Applied, Phone Screen, Interview, Final Round, Offer, Rejected, Ghosted (no response in 2+ weeks)

**The rule:** Update the tracker immediately after every action. Not later. Not tonight. Right now. If you wait, you'll forget.

### If Spreadsheets Don't Work For You

Try Trello with 5 columns: To Apply, Applied, Interviewing, Offer, Closed. Move cards between columns. The visual progress is satisfying and works well with ADHD visual processing.

Or try a simple notebook with one line per application: "April 3 / Acme / Marketing Manager / Applied / Follow up April 10." Low tech works if you use it consistently.

**Action:** Set up your tracker now. Pick the format that feels easiest. Enter any current applications.

## System 3: The Nightly Sticky Note

This is the single most effective ADHD productivity tool in existence. It takes 30 seconds.

Every night before you close your laptop:

1. Write tomorrow's ONE most important task on a sticky note
2. Stick it on your laptop keyboard (so you literally can't open your laptop without seeing it)

Examples:
- "Apply to [Company] [Role]"
- "Prep for Thursday interview"
- "Follow up with [Name]"
- "Customize resume for [Industry]"

**Why this works:** ADHD brains lose 30-60 minutes every morning to decision paralysis (what should I work on?). The sticky note eliminates the decision. You sit down, you see the task, you start. No thinking required.

**The rule:** Only ONE task. Not three. Not a to-do list. One. If you finish it and have energy, great, do more. But the sticky note is singular.

**Action:** Do this tonight. Write tomorrow's one task. Stick it on your keyboard.

## System 4: The Calendar Is Your Brain

If it's not in your calendar, it doesn't exist. Treat your calendar as your external memory.

### What Goes in the Calendar

- Interviews and phone screens (obviously)
- Follow-up deadlines ("Follow up with Acme Co" on the specific date)
- Application deadlines (with a reminder 2 days before)
- Networking calls and coffee chats
- Your daily job search blocks (same time every day)
- Your weekly off-day (protected, no search activities)
- Your worry window (see Anxiety Toolkit, Guide 04 of the Emotional Survival Kit)
- Medication reminders (if applicable)
- Exercise and movement blocks

### Calendar Rules for ADHD

1. **Set alerts for everything.** Two alerts: one 24 hours before, one 30 minutes before. For interviews: add a 2-hour-before alert for prep time.

2. **Color code.** Job search = one color. Personal = another. Exercise = another. Your brain processes color faster than text.

3. **Block transition time.** If you have a call at 2 PM, block 1:30-2:00 as "prep and transition." ADHD brains need explicit buffer time.

4. **Schedule the end, not just the start.** A calendar block that says "Job search 9 AM-12 PM" tells your brain when to stop. Without an endpoint, you either burn out or drift.

## System 5: The Decision Eliminator

Every decision costs cognitive energy. ADHD brains have less cognitive energy to spend. So eliminate as many decisions as possible.

### Decisions to Eliminate

**What to wear:** Pick 3-5 outfits for interview/networking days. Rotate. Don't decide fresh each morning.

**What to eat:** Meal prep 2-3 lunches on Sunday. During the week, lunch is decided.

**When to job search:** Same time block every day. Monday through Friday, [time] to [time]. Not "whenever I feel like it." Scheduled.

**Which jobs to apply to:** Batch your research and identification. Spend one session per week finding 5-10 roles. Spend the rest of the week applying to them. Separate finding from doing.

**What to work on first:** The sticky note decides. You don't.

**When to check email/LinkedIn:** Twice a day at set times. Not "whenever a notification appears."

### The Decision Budget

Think of yourself as having 50 decision tokens per day. Every choice spends a token. Big choices (what role to target) cost 5 tokens. Small choices (what to eat, what to wear) cost 1 token each. When you're out of tokens, you default to scrolling or avoidance.

Pre-making decisions is like pre-loading tokens. The more decisions you eliminate in advance, the more tokens you have for the ones that actually matter.

## System 6: The Accountability Structure

ADHD brains respond to external accountability far more than internal motivation. Build accountability into your search.

### Options (Pick At Least One)

**Daily text check-in:** Find one person (friend, fellow job seeker, partner). Text them your point score or one accomplishment at the end of each day. They don't need to respond. The act of reporting creates accountability.

**Weekly accountability call:** Schedule a 15-minute weekly call with someone where you share: what you did last week, what you'll do this week, and where you're stuck.

**Focusmate sessions:** Book 2-3 Focusmate sessions per week during your peak hours. The appointment creates a start time your brain respects because another human is involved.

**Public commitment:** Post your weekly goal on social media or in a community group. "This week I'm applying to 5 roles and having 2 networking calls." Public commitment increases follow-through significantly.

**Action:** Set up one accountability mechanism this week. The social element is the engine that keeps the systems running.

## System 7: The Weekly Reset

Every Sunday (or whatever day works), spend 20 minutes resetting your systems. This prevents the slow drift that kills productivity systems.

### The Reset Checklist

- [ ] Update application tracker with current statuses
- [ ] Review calendar for the upcoming week, add any missing items
- [ ] Write Monday's sticky note
- [ ] Check email for any outstanding follow-ups needed
- [ ] Review point score or streak for the past week
- [ ] Claim any earned rewards
- [ ] Set next week's point/application target
- [ ] Tidy physical launch pad
- [ ] Close any browser tabs that aren't in your Job Search folder

20 minutes. Timer set. When the timer ends, you're done. This prevents the reset from becoming a procrastination project disguised as productivity.

## When Systems Break Down

They will. Every system eventually stops working for ADHD brains. This is not failure. It's predictable.

**When a system stops working:**

1. Don't shame yourself. The system served its purpose. Systems have lifespans.
2. Ask: is the system too complex? Simplify it. Most breakdowns come from over-engineering.
3. Ask: has the novelty worn off? Rotate to a different system for 2 weeks, then come back.
4. Ask: did I stop doing the weekly reset? The reset is what maintains every other system. Restart there.
5. Ask: am I burned out? If yes, the system isn't the problem. See the Burnout Circuit Breaker guide.

The goal is not a perfect system that never breaks. The goal is a system that's easy to restart when it does.

---

## Key Takeaway

Willpower is a neurotypical luxury. ADHD brains need external systems that handle the executive functions their neurology doesn't provide reliably. Launch pads for starting. Trackers for remembering. Sticky notes for prioritizing. Calendars for time awareness. Decision eliminators for cognitive conservation. Accountability for motivation. Weekly resets for maintenance. Build the scaffolding. Let the scaffolding do the heavy lifting. Save your brainpower for the things only your brain can do: being brilliant in interviews, connecting with people, and solving problems nobody else can see.
