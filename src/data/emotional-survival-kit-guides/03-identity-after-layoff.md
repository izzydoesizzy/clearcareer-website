# Identity After Layoff

**How to answer "what do you do?" when you don't know anymore, and how to rebuild a sense of self that doesn't depend on a job title.**

> **Disclaimer:** This guide draws on Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) principles. It is not a replacement for professional therapy. If you're experiencing a prolonged identity crisis that affects daily functioning, please speak with a licensed professional.

You don't need to do everything today. Work through this at your own pace.

**Time estimate:** 25 minutes to read, then work through the exercises over several days
**What you'll need:** Notebook or notes app, about 30 minutes of quiet time for the identity mapping exercise

---

## The Identity Earthquake

For most of us, "what do you do?" is actually "who are you?"

Your job title answered that question every day. Product Manager. Software Engineer. VP of Marketing. Teacher. Nurse. Director of Operations.

Now it's gone. And the question "what do you do?" lands like a punch.

This is an identity crisis. Not the dramatic Hollywood kind. The quiet kind where you wake up on a Monday with no calendar, no team, no purpose, and no idea who you are without the thing that consumed 40-60 hours of your week.

You didn't just lose a job. You lost a version of yourself.

---

## Why "I'm More Than My Job" Doesn't Help

You've heard it. Maybe you've even said it. "I'm more than my job title."

Intellectually, sure. But try believing that at a dinner party when someone asks what you do and your brain goes blank.

The truth is: in North American culture, work is identity. We're socialized to fuse the two from childhood. "What do you want to be when you grow up?" doesn't mean "what activities do you want to do?" It means "what label will define you?"

Untangling identity from employment is real psychological work. It doesn't happen by repeating affirmations. It happens by building a broader sense of who you are.

---

## The ACT Approach: Values Over Labels

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) has a useful framework here. Instead of attaching your identity to roles (which can be taken away), attach it to values (which can't).

A role is: "I'm a marketing director."
A value is: "I'm someone who creates things that connect with people."

A role is: "I'm a software engineer."
A value is: "I'm someone who solves problems and builds things."

Roles are temporary. Values are permanent. When you build identity around values, losing a job is painful but it doesn't erase who you are.

## Identity Mapping: 6 Domains

Your identity lives in six domains. Most people over-invest in one or two and neglect the rest. The goal is balance.

Grab your notebook. For each domain, rate how much of your identity currently lives here (0-10) and write 2-3 sentences about what this domain looks like for you right now.

### 1. Professional Identity

Who you are in relation to your work and career.

Questions to explore:
- What skills do I have regardless of job title?
- What kind of problems do I love solving?
- What do colleagues consistently come to me for?
- If my job title disappeared, what would I still be able to do?

### 2. Relational Identity

Who you are in your relationships: partner, parent, sibling, friend, child.

Questions to explore:
- Which relationships give me energy?
- What kind of friend/partner/parent am I?
- How do the people who know me best describe me?

### 3. Creative Identity

How you express yourself and make things.

Questions to explore:
- When was the last time I made something just because I wanted to?
- What did I love doing as a kid before "career" became the focus?
- What would I create if nobody was watching and it didn't need to be good?

### 4. Physical Identity

Your relationship with your body and physical self.

Questions to explore:
- How does my body feel right now?
- What physical activities make me feel strong or alive?
- Am I sleeping? Eating? Moving? (Honest answers only.)

### 5. Community Identity

Your place in groups, organizations, neighborhoods, causes.

Questions to explore:
- Where do I belong outside of work?
- What communities have I let slide since my career took over?
- What causes or groups would I join if I had the time? (You have the time now.)

### 6. Inner Identity

Your relationship with yourself: beliefs, values, spirituality, personal philosophy.

Questions to explore:
- What do I believe in?
- What principles guide my decisions?
- When I'm alone and quiet, what matters most to me?

**Action:** After mapping all six, look at the distribution. If 8/10 of your identity was in Domain 1 (professional) and the rest are at 2, that explains why the layoff hit so hard. The work ahead is redistributing.

## Rebuilding: The Values Inventory

List your top 5 values. Not what you think you should value. What you actually value. Be honest.

Here's a starter list. Circle the ones that light up for you:

| | | | |
|---|---|---|---|
| Creativity | Security | Adventure | Family |
| Autonomy | Connection | Growth | Health |
| Justice | Knowledge | Leadership | Simplicity |
| Humor | Loyalty | Achievement | Freedom |
| Service | Integrity | Courage | Balance |

Now for each value, answer: **How am I living this value right now, outside of a job?**

If the answer is "I'm not," that's your starting point. Pick one value and take one action this week that honors it.

Example: If you value creativity and you haven't made anything in months, spend 30 minutes drawing, writing, cooking something new, or building something with your hands. It doesn't need to be productive. It needs to be yours.

## 5 Ways to Answer "What Do You Do?"

The dreaded question. Here are five responses that don't require you to say "I'm unemployed" or fake enthusiasm you don't have.

### 1. The Redirect

"I'm in a career transition right now. What about you, what do you do?"

Simple. Honest. Moves the conversation off you before it gets uncomfortable.

### 2. The Skills-Based Answer

"I'm a [skill] person. Most recently I was doing that at [Company], and now I'm exploring what's next."

Example: "I'm a data and analytics person. I spent the last five years at Shopify building their reporting infrastructure, and now I'm looking at where to take those skills next."

### 3. The Values-Based Answer

"I solve [type of problem] for [type of organization]. I'm figuring out where to do that next."

Example: "I help companies understand their customers. I'm in between roles right now and being selective about what comes next."

### 4. The Honest Answer

"I was laid off [timeframe] ago. It's been a process, but I'm getting clearer on what I want next."

This one takes courage. But the vulnerability often opens doors. People want to help. Give them the chance.

### 5. The Non-Work Answer

"Lately? I've been [hobby/interest/project]. It's been a nice change of pace."

Example: "Honestly? I've been spending more time with my kids and rediscovering cooking. Oh, and I'm job searching, but that's the boring part."

**Action:** Pick the response that feels most natural. Practice saying it out loud three times. Seriously. Out loud. So it's ready when you need it.

## The Identity Timeline

Here's something that helps: write the timeline of who you've been.

Not your resume. Your identity timeline. The versions of yourself that existed before this job.

- Age 10: the kid who loved building things
- Age 16: the teenager who was good at writing
- Age 22: the new grad terrified and excited
- Age 28: the professional finding their groove
- Age 35: the leader who built a team
- Now: someone in transition, carrying all of those versions forward

You're not starting over. You're continuing. Every previous version of you still exists. The skills, the knowledge, the personality traits, the resilience. All of it came with you.

The job title was just a label for the current chapter. The book is much longer than one chapter.

---

## When Identity Work Gets Heavy

It's normal for this process to surface big emotions. You might realize you spent a decade building an identity you never actually chose. You might feel angry that work consumed so much of who you were. You might grieve the person you were inside that role.

All of that is valid.

If the weight of it starts affecting your daily life, sleep, relationships, or ability to function, talk to a therapist. This is exactly what they're trained for. You're not weak for needing support during an identity rebuild. You're smart enough to recognize when you need it.

---

## Key Takeaway

Your job gave you a label. It didn't give you your identity. Your identity lives in your values, your relationships, your skills, your creativity, and the way you show up in the world. All of that still exists. A layoff can't take it. The work now is remembering who you are underneath the title, and building a life where that person has room to breathe.
