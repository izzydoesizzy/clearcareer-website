# Tell Me About Yourself

**The most predictable question in every interview. Nail it in 60-90 seconds.**

"Tell me about yourself" is the opening pitch of nearly every interview. It is also the question candidates prepare for the least, because it sounds simple. It is not simple. It is your chance to set the narrative for the entire conversation. Get it right and the interviewer spends the rest of the interview confirming the positive impression you just created. Get it wrong and you spend the rest of the interview digging out of a hole.

This guide gives you three formulas, three example scripts with [BRACKET] fields you can customize, and a practice method that takes 15 minutes.

**Time:** 30 minutes to build your answer. 15 minutes to practice it. **What you'll need:** The job description for your target role and your resume.

---

## The Rules

**Rule 1: Keep it to 60-90 seconds.** This is not your life story. It is a highlight reel. If you go past 2 minutes, you have lost them.

**Rule 2: End with why you are here.** The last sentence should connect your background to this specific role.

**Rule 3: Do not recite your resume.** They have your resume. They can read. Give them the story behind the resume.

**Rule 4: Do not start with "Well, I was born in..."** Start with where you are now professionally. Then go backward only as far as you need to.

**Rule 5: Make it conversational.** This is not a speech. It is the opening of a conversation. Sound like a person, not a LinkedIn profile.

---

## Formula 1: Present-Past-Future

The most versatile formula. Works for any career stage, any industry.

**Structure:**
1. **Present:** What you do now (or most recently did), in one sentence.
2. **Past:** How you got here. The 2-3 most relevant career moves, with one highlight from each.
3. **Future:** Why you are excited about this opportunity. Connect your experience to what they need.

### Example Script 1: Mid-Career Professional

```
"I'm a [CURRENT/MOST RECENT TITLE] with [X YEARS] of experience in [YOUR FIELD/SPECIALTY]. Most recently, I was at [MOST RECENT COMPANY], where I [ONE KEY ACHIEVEMENT WITH A NUMBER IF POSSIBLE].

Before that, I spent [X YEARS] at [PREVIOUS COMPANY] doing [BRIEF DESCRIPTION], where I [ONE HIGHLIGHT]. That's where I developed my strength in [KEY SKILL RELEVANT TO THE TARGET ROLE].

What excites me about this [ROLE TITLE] at [COMPANY NAME] is [SPECIFIC THING: the chance to build a team, the product, the company's mission, the growth stage]. My experience in [RELEVANT SKILL/AREA] maps directly to [SOMETHING FROM THE JOB DESCRIPTION], and I'd love to bring that here."
```

## Formula 2: The Theme Answer

Best for career changers or people with diverse backgrounds. Instead of walking through your timeline, you organize around a theme.

**Structure:**
1. **Theme:** The one thread that connects your experience.
2. **Evidence:** Two to three specific examples that prove the theme.
3. **Connection:** How that theme makes you perfect for this role.

### Example Script 2: Career Changer

```
"The thread through my career has been [YOUR THEME: solving complex problems, building things from scratch, helping people navigate change]. 

In my [X YEARS] in [PREVIOUS INDUSTRY], that looked like [SPECIFIC EXAMPLE]. When I moved into [SECOND AREA/INDUSTRY], it showed up as [DIFFERENT SPECIFIC EXAMPLE]. In both cases, the core skill was [THE SKILL THAT CONNECTS THEM].

I'm making this move to [TARGET INDUSTRY/ROLE] because [HONEST REASON THAT CONNECTS TO THE THEME]. I bring [X YEARS] of [RELEVANT EXPERIENCE], plus a perspective that most people in [TARGET FIELD] don't have because they've only worked in one industry."
```

---

## Formula 3: The Value Proposition

Best for senior professionals and people who know exactly what they bring. This is the most direct formula.

**Structure:**
1. **Who you are:** Title and specialty, in one line.
2. **What you are known for:** Your signature strength or achievement.
3. **What you deliver:** The specific value you bring to an employer.
4. **Why here:** What drew you to this company or role.

### Example Script 3: Senior Professional

```
"I'm a [TITLE/SPECIALTY] who [YOUR SIGNATURE THING: builds high-performing teams, turns around struggling products, opens new markets]. Over the past [X YEARS], I've [HEADLINE ACHIEVEMENT: grown revenue from $X to $Y, built a team from 3 to 30, launched X products].

The thing I do best is [SPECIFIC VALUE PROPOSITION: take a complex situation, break it down, and create a clear path forward]. At [MOST RECENT COMPANY], that meant [BRIEF EXAMPLE].

I'm interested in [COMPANY NAME] because [SPECIFIC REASON: you're at a stage where this skill matters, the product aligns with my expertise, the challenge is the kind of problem I love solving]. I think there's a strong fit between what you need and what I've spent my career getting good at."
```

## Customization Guide

### Match the energy to the company

**Startup:** Be more casual and enthusiasm-forward. Show hunger and adaptability.
**Enterprise/Corporate:** Be more structured and metrics-driven. Show stability and results.
**Creative industry:** Show personality and passion. Be less formulaic.
**Technical role:** Lead with technical skills and specific technologies. Be precise.

### Match the audience

**Recruiter:** Keep it high-level. They need to know if you are in the right ballpark.
**Hiring manager:** Go deeper on relevant experience. They want to know if you can do the job.
**Executive:** Focus on impact and strategic thinking. They want to know if you see the big picture.
**Peer interviewer:** Be collaborative. They want to know if they would enjoy working with you.

### What to leave out

- Personal details (family, hobbies) unless directly relevant or the culture clearly invites it
- Reasons you left previous jobs (they will ask separately if they want to know)
- Negative comments about previous employers
- Salary expectations (not the time)
- "I'm a hard worker" or "I'm a team player" (show, don't tell)

## The Practice Method

### Step 1: Write it out
Use one of the three formulas above. Fill in every bracket. Write the full answer.

### Step 2: Time it
Read it out loud. Time yourself. If it is over 90 seconds, cut something. If it is under 45 seconds, add a detail.

### Step 3: Trim the script
You do not want to memorize word-for-word. That sounds robotic. Instead, reduce your written answer to bullet points:

- Present: [TITLE] at [COMPANY], [KEY ACHIEVEMENT]
- Past: [PREVIOUS ROLE], [HIGHLIGHT], [SKILL DEVELOPED]
- Future: [REASON EXCITED ABOUT THIS ROLE]

### Step 4: Practice from bullets
Tell your story from the bullets, not the script. Do it 5 times. Each time will be slightly different. That is good. It sounds natural.

### Step 5: Record yourself
Use your phone. Watch it back. Check for:
- Length (60-90 seconds)
- Filler words ("um," "like," "so," "you know")
- Energy level (do you sound interested or bored?)
- Eye contact (if practicing for video interviews, look at the camera)

### Step 6: Test it on a human
Tell a friend or family member. Ask: "After hearing that, what do you think I do for a living? Would you hire me?" Their honest reaction is the best feedback.

## Answers to Common Variations

### "Walk me through your resume"
Same answer as "tell me about yourself," but slightly more chronological. Walk forward through time instead of starting with the present.

### "Why should we hire you?"
Use Formula 3 (Value Proposition) without the "who you are" opening. Go straight to what you deliver and why it matters for this role.

### "What brings you here today?"
This is "tell me about yourself" plus "why this company." Use any formula but give extra weight to the "why here" section.

### "Give me your elevator pitch"
Shorter version. 30 seconds. Present + one highlight + why here. That is it.

```
"I'm a [TITLE] with [X YEARS] in [FIELD]. I'm known for [ONE THING]. I'm here because [COMPANY NAME] is doing [SPECIFIC THING] and my background in [RELEVANT AREA] is a direct fit."
```

---

## Key Takeaway

"Tell me about yourself" is not a spontaneous question. It comes up in every single interview. There is zero reason to be unprepared for it.

Pick a formula. Fill in the brackets. Practice it 5 times out loud. Walk in ready to start the conversation on your terms.
