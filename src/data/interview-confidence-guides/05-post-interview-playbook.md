# Post-Interview Playbook

**What to do in the 24 hours after your interview. And the 2 weeks after that.**

The interview is over. You walked out (or closed your laptop) and now your brain is replaying every answer, analyzing every micro-expression, and trying to predict the outcome. That is normal. It is also unhelpful.

This guide gives you a clear post-interview protocol: send the right thank-you note, follow up at the right time, read the signals correctly, and debrief so you keep getting better.

**Time:** 20-30 minutes for the thank-you note. 10 minutes for the debrief. **What you'll need:** The interviewer's name and email, notes from the conversation, and a clear head.

---

## Step 1: The Thank-You Note (Send Within 24 Hours)

Not 48 hours. Not "when I get around to it." Within 24 hours. Ideally within 4-6 hours of the interview.

### Template 1: Standard Thank-You (Works for Most Interviews)

```
Subject: Thank you - [ROLE TITLE] interview

Hi [INTERVIEWER NAME],

Thank you for taking the time to speak with me today about the [ROLE TITLE] position. I enjoyed learning more about [SPECIFIC THING YOU DISCUSSED: the team's approach to X, the upcoming project, the company's plans for Y].

Our conversation reinforced my interest in this role. In particular, [ONE SPECIFIC THING THAT EXCITED YOU ABOUT THE ROLE OR COMPANY, tied to something they said]. I believe my experience with [RELEVANT SKILL OR ACHIEVEMENT] would allow me to contribute to [SPECIFIC GOAL OR CHALLENGE THEY MENTIONED].

Please don't hesitate to reach out if you need any additional information. I look forward to hearing about next steps.

Best regards,
[YOUR NAME]
```

### Template 2: The "Forgot to Mention" Thank-You

Use this if there is something important you did not get to say during the interview.

```
Subject: Thank you + one more thought - [ROLE TITLE]

Hi [INTERVIEWER NAME],

Thank you for the conversation today about the [ROLE TITLE] position. I walked away energized about the opportunity, especially [SPECIFIC THING DISCUSSED].

One thing I did not get to mention: [BRIEF, RELEVANT POINT YOU WISH YOU HAD MADE. Keep it to 2-3 sentences. Tie it to something they said they needed.]

I am very excited about this opportunity and would welcome the chance to continue the conversation. Please let me know if there is anything else you need from my end.

Best regards,
[YOUR NAME]
```

### Template 3: The Panel Interview Thank-You

When you interview with multiple people, send individual notes to each person. Do not copy-paste the same message. Reference something specific from each conversation.

```
Subject: Thank you - [ROLE TITLE] discussion

Hi [INTERVIEWER NAME],

Thank you for being part of today's conversation about the [ROLE TITLE] role. I particularly valued your perspective on [SPECIFIC TOPIC THIS PERSON DISCUSSED OR ASKED ABOUT].

[ONE SENTENCE connecting your experience to their specific question or concern.]

I appreciate the team's time and I'm looking forward to the next steps.

Best,
[YOUR NAME]
```

### Thank-You Note Rules

- **Email, not LinkedIn message.** Email is professional. LinkedIn is casual. Use email.
- **Spell their name correctly.** Check twice.
- **Reference something specific.** Generic thank-you notes go in the trash. Show that you were listening.
- **Keep it short.** 4-6 sentences maximum. Nobody wants a post-interview essay.
- **Do not ask about timeline or next steps.** They will tell you when they are ready.
- **Proofread.** Read it out loud before hitting send. Typos in a thank-you note are worse than no note at all.

## Step 2: The Follow-Up Timeline

### The Waiting Game

| Time Since Interview | What to Do |
|---------------------|-----------|
| 0-24 hours | Send thank-you note |
| 1-7 days | Do nothing. Wait for their timeline. |
| 7-10 days (if they gave a timeline and it has passed) | Send a polite check-in (see template below) |
| 10-14 days (no timeline was given) | Send a polite check-in |
| 14-21 days (after your check-in, no response) | Send one final follow-up |
| 21+ days (still nothing) | Move on. Keep them warm but assume no. |

### The Check-In Email

```
Subject: Following up - [ROLE TITLE]

Hi [RECRUITER OR HIRING MANAGER NAME],

I hope you're doing well. I wanted to follow up on our conversation about the [ROLE TITLE] position on [DATE]. I remain very interested in the opportunity and would love to learn about any updates on the process.

I'm happy to provide any additional information that would be helpful. Please let me know if there's anything you need from my end.

Best regards,
[YOUR NAME]
```

### The Final Follow-Up

```
Subject: Re: Following up - [ROLE TITLE]

Hi [NAME],

Just circling back one more time on the [ROLE TITLE] role. I understand hiring timelines can shift, so no pressure at all. I'm still very interested and would welcome an update when you have one.

Wishing you and the team a great [WEEK/MONTH].

[YOUR NAME]
```

After the final follow-up, stop. Do not send a third or fourth. If they want to hire you, they know where to find you.

## Step 3: Reading the Signals

Interview signals are not always reliable, but patterns exist. Here is how to read them.

### Positive Signals

- The interview ran longer than scheduled
- They introduced you to additional team members (unplanned)
- They asked about your availability, start date, or other offers
- They described specific projects you would work on ("When you join, you'll be working on...")
- They sold the company to you (benefits, culture, growth opportunities)
- They responded to your thank-you note quickly and warmly
- The recruiter reaches out to check on your interest level

### Neutral Signals (Do Not Read Into These)

- Standard-length interview
- Professional but not overly warm demeanor
- "We'll be in touch" at the end
- No response to your thank-you note (many hiring managers don't reply)
- Being asked to complete a take-home assignment (this is standard process, not a signal either way)

### Concerning Signals

- The interview was noticeably shorter than scheduled
- They seemed distracted or checked their phone
- They did not ask about your experience in depth
- They did not sell the role or company at all
- The recruiter goes silent after previously being responsive
- "We have a lot of candidates to get through"
- Significantly delayed timeline with no explanation

**Important:** Signals are indicators, not guarantees. People get hired after terrible-feeling interviews. People get rejected after interviews that felt perfect. Focus on what you can control (thank-you notes, follow-ups, continued searching) and let the process play out.

## Step 5: Keep Searching

The single biggest mistake job seekers make after a great interview: they stop searching.

Do not do this.

Until you have a written offer in hand, you are still on the market. Keep applying. Keep networking. Keep interviewing. This is not disloyal. It is smart. Companies interview multiple candidates simultaneously. You should too.

Reasons to keep searching after a great interview:
- The role might be filled internally
- The headcount might get frozen
- Another candidate might get the offer
- Your timeline might not align with theirs
- You might get an even better offer somewhere else

**Your interview pipeline should always have multiple companies at different stages.** One in early conversations, one in mid-process, one in late-stage. When one drops off, another is already moving.

---

## When You Get the Offer

Do not accept on the spot. Always ask for time.

"I'm really excited about this. Thank you. I'd like to take 24-48 hours to review the full details. Can you send me the offer in writing?"

Then: review the offer against your three salary numbers (from the Salary Number Generator). Negotiate if appropriate. Get everything in writing.

## When You Get Rejected

Send a gracious response (see Cold Outreach Scripts, Thank You #4). Ask if they would be open to keeping you in mind for future roles. Then move on. Every rejection is data, not a verdict on your worth.

---

## Key Takeaway

The interview does not end when you walk out the door. The thank-you note, the follow-up timing, and the debrief are all part of the process. They take 30 minutes total and they separate the candidates who get offers from the ones who get ghosted.

Send the note. Do the debrief. Keep searching. Repeat.
