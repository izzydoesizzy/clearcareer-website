# Anxiety Management for Interviews

**You are not going to eliminate nervousness. You are going to use it.**

Interview anxiety is normal. Your brain is doing exactly what it was designed to do: detecting a high-stakes social evaluation and flooding your body with adrenaline. The problem is not that you get nervous. The problem is that you do not have a plan for what to do with the nerves.

This guide gives you three protocols: one for the night before, one for the morning of, and one for 10 minutes before you walk in. Use all three or pick the pieces that work for you.

**Time:** 10-15 minutes to read. Then practice each protocol once before your next interview. **What you'll need:** Nothing except a willingness to try things that might feel awkward the first time.

---

## Understanding Interview Anxiety

Your body cannot tell the difference between "I am being chased by a bear" and "I am about to be judged by a stranger." The physiological response is the same: elevated heart rate, shallow breathing, tight muscles, dry mouth, racing thoughts.

Here is what helps to know: interviewers expect you to be nervous. They are nervous too (most interviewers have zero training and are also worried about screwing up). A little nervousness actually helps. It sharpens your focus and gives you energy. The goal is not zero anxiety. The goal is manageable anxiety.

## The Night-Before Protocol

### 1. Prepare Your Materials (30 minutes, max)

Do this early in the evening, not at midnight.

- [ ] Review the job description one more time. Highlight 3 key requirements.
- [ ] Review your 3-4 strongest STAR stories that match those requirements.
- [ ] Prepare 2-3 questions to ask the interviewer.
- [ ] Lay out your clothes (if in-person) or test your tech setup (if virtual).
- [ ] Write down the interviewer's name, the time, and the location/link.

That is it. Do not cram. Do not re-read your entire story bank. Do not Google "trick interview questions." You are as prepared as you are going to be.

### 2. The Worry Download (10 minutes)

Set a timer for 10 minutes. Write down every worry you have about tomorrow's interview. Do not filter. Do not judge. Just dump it all onto paper.

"What if I blank on an answer?"
"What if they ask about the gap in my resume?"
"What if I say something stupid?"
"What if they can tell I am nervous?"

Write until the timer goes off. Then close the notebook (or fold the paper). You have externalized the worry. It is on paper now, not circling in your head.

Research from the University of Chicago shows that expressive writing before a high-pressure performance reduces anxiety and improves results. This is not woo-woo. It is cognitive science.

### 3. The Cutoff (Set a time, stick to it)

Pick a time in the evening, ideally 2 hours before bed, after which you do not think about, talk about, or prepare for the interview. Watch a show. Read a book. Play a game. Talk to someone about anything except tomorrow.

Your brain needs downtime to consolidate what you have learned. Studying until midnight does not help. It makes you tired and anxious.

### 4. Sleep Preparation

- No caffeine after 2 PM
- No screens 30 minutes before bed (or use night mode)
- Set two alarms (one for "wake up" and one for "leave by" or "log on by")
- If you cannot sleep, do not fight it. Lie still, breathe slowly, and rest. One night of poor sleep will not ruin your interview. The anxiety about not sleeping is worse than the actual sleep loss.

## The Morning-Of Protocol

### 1. Move Your Body (10-15 minutes)

Do something physical before the interview. A walk, a few sets of pushups, stretching, dancing to a song in your kitchen. It does not matter what.

Physical movement metabolizes the stress hormones (cortisol, adrenaline) that are already building up. It also releases endorphins, which improve mood and reduce pain perception. You will feel calmer and more present.

### 2. Eat Something

An empty stomach amplifies anxiety symptoms. You do not need a big meal. A piece of toast with peanut butter, a banana, some yogurt. Something with protein and complex carbs. Avoid excessive caffeine. If you normally have one coffee, have one coffee. Do not double it because you are tired.

### 3. The Power Pose (2 minutes)

This one sounds silly. Do it anyway.

Stand up. Feet shoulder-width apart. Hands on hips or arms raised in a V. Hold this pose for 2 minutes.

Amy Cuddy's research on power posing has been debated in academic circles, but the subjective experience is consistently reported as helpful: people feel more confident after holding an expansive posture. At minimum, you are standing up straight and breathing deeply for 2 minutes. That alone helps.

### 4. The Confidence Anchor

Read this statement out loud (or in your head if people are around):

"I am prepared. I have real experience and real skills. This interview is a conversation, not an exam. I am evaluating them as much as they are evaluating me. I have done hard things before and I can do this."

Say it once. Mean it. Move on.

## The 10-Minutes-Before Protocol

### 1. Box Breathing (2 minutes)

This is used by Navy SEALs, surgeons, and Olympic athletes. It works.

- Breathe in for 4 counts
- Hold for 4 counts
- Breathe out for 4 counts
- Hold for 4 counts
- Repeat 4-6 times

This activates your parasympathetic nervous system (the "rest and digest" system) and directly counteracts the fight-or-flight response. Your heart rate will drop. Your thinking will clear.

### 2. The Reframe (1 minute)

Do not tell yourself "Don't be nervous." That does not work. Your brain cannot process a negative instruction. Instead, reframe:

**Instead of:** "I am so nervous"
**Say:** "I am excited. My body is giving me energy for this."

This is called anxiety reappraisal. A Harvard study found that people who reframed anxiety as excitement performed significantly better on public speaking tasks than people who tried to calm down. The physiological state is the same. You are just changing the label.

### 3. The Grounding Technique (1 minute)

If your thoughts are racing, use the 5-4-3-2-1 method:

- Name **5 things you can see**
- Name **4 things you can touch**
- Name **3 things you can hear**
- Name **2 things you can smell**
- Name **1 thing you can taste**

This pulls you out of your anxious thoughts and into the present moment. You cannot panic about the future when your brain is busy cataloging your current environment.

### 4. The First 30 Seconds (preparation)

The first 30 seconds of any interaction set the tone. Prepare yours:

- **Smile.** A real one. Smiling releases neuropeptides that reduce stress.
- **Make eye contact.** If virtual, look at the camera, not the screen.
- **Say their name.** "Hi [NAME], great to meet you. Thanks for taking the time."
- **Have your opening ready.** If they say "Tell me about yourself," you know exactly what to say (see Guide 03).

Having the first 30 seconds scripted reduces anxiety because your brain knows it can handle the start. Once you are in the conversation, momentum takes over.

## During the Interview: Emergency Moves

### If You Blank on an Answer

"That's a great question. Let me think about that for a moment."

Then pause for 5-10 seconds. This feels like an eternity to you. To the interviewer, it looks like thoughtfulness. Nobody expects instant answers to complex questions.

### If You Start Rambling

Stop mid-sentence. Say: "Let me bring that back to the point." Then answer the actual question in 1-2 sentences. Interviewers respect self-correction far more than a 5-minute monologue.

### If Your Hands Are Shaking

Rest them on the table or hold a pen. If virtual, keep your hands below the camera. Nobody can see what is happening under the desk.

### If Your Voice Is Shaking

Slow down. Take a breath between sentences. Drink water (bring a bottle). Speaking more slowly makes you sound more confident, even if you do not feel it.

### If You Get a Curveball Question

"I haven't encountered that exact situation, but here's a related experience..." Then tell your closest STAR story. Interviewers care more about your thinking process than a perfect-match answer.

## After the Interview: The Cooldown

Your nervous system needs time to come back down. Do not immediately check your phone for emails or replay every answer in your head.

1. **Move your body again.** Walk, stretch, dance. Burn off the residual adrenaline.
2. **Call or text someone.** Not to analyze the interview. Just to have a normal human conversation and remind yourself that you exist outside of this process.
3. **Write a brief debrief.** What went well? What would you do differently? What questions did they ask? (This helps you prepare for future interviews.) See Guide 05 for the full debrief worksheet.
4. **Send your thank-you note within 24 hours.** Then let it go. You have no control over the outcome. Focus on what you can control: preparing for the next one.

---

## Key Takeaway

Nervousness is not a bug. It is a feature. Your body is giving you energy and focus for a high-stakes situation. The candidates who seem calm in interviews are not actually calm. They have just practiced managing the nerves so many times that it looks effortless.

Prepare the night before. Move and eat the morning of. Breathe and reframe 10 minutes before. Walk in ready.
