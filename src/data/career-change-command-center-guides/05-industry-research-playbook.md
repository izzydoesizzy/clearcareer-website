# Industry Research Playbook

**Understand a new industry in 2 weeks. Go from outsider to informed in 10 days.**

When you change careers, you are not just learning new skills. You are learning a new language, a new culture, and a new set of unwritten rules. The people who successfully transition into new industries do not just show up with transferable skills. They show up speaking the language of their target industry fluently enough that insiders forget they are new.

This playbook gives you a 2-week research protocol to build that fluency.

**Time:** 1-2 hours per day for 10 business days. **What you'll need:** Internet access, a notebook, and LinkedIn.

---

## The Insider Language Method

Every industry has vocabulary that insiders use without thinking and outsiders do not know exists. Learning this vocabulary is the single fastest way to sound credible in a new field.

### How to Build Your Industry Dictionary

1. **Read 10 job postings** in your target role. Highlight every term, acronym, or phrase you do not fully understand.
2. **Read the top 5 industry blogs or newsletters** for 1 week. Note the jargon that appears repeatedly.
3. **Listen to 3-5 podcast episodes** from industry leaders. Pay attention to how they describe their work.
4. **Read 3 earnings call transcripts or annual reports** from major companies in the industry. These reveal the metrics and priorities that matter.

Start a running glossary. Every new term, acronym, or concept goes in it. By the end of 2 weeks, you should have 30-50 terms.

**Your glossary categories:**
- Key metrics (what does this industry measure?)
- Common tools and platforms (what software does everyone use?)
- Regulatory language (what rules govern this industry?)
- Business model terminology (how does money flow?)
- Role-specific jargon (what do people in your target role talk about?)

## The 2-Week Research Protocol

### Week 1: Landscape

**Day 1-2: Industry Overview**
- Read the Wikipedia page for the industry (yes, really, it is a surprisingly good starting point)
- Read 3 recent "State of [Industry]" reports (search "[Industry] annual report 2025/2026")
- Answer: What is this industry? How big is it? Is it growing or shrinking? What are the major trends?

**Key questions to answer:**
- How large is this industry (revenue, employees)?
- What are the top 10 companies?
- Is the industry growing, stable, or declining?
- What are the 3 biggest trends right now?
- What are the 3 biggest challenges?

**Day 3-4: Key Players**
- Identify the top 10-15 companies in the space (by revenue, market share, or reputation)
- For each: note their size, headquarters, recent news, and what they are known for
- Identify 3-5 "emerging" or "disruptive" companies (startups or challengers)

**Create a simple table:**

| ________________ | | | | |

**Day 5: Competitive Landscape**
- How do companies in this industry compete? (Price? Quality? Innovation? Relationships?)
- What is the typical business model? (Subscription? Project-based? Product sales?)
- Who are the customers? (B2B? B2C? Government? Enterprise?)

### Week 2: People and Practice

**Day 6-7: The People**
- Find 20-30 people on LinkedIn who hold your target role at different companies
- Study their profiles: What is their background? Where did they come from? What skills do they list?
- Identify 5-10 people whose career path is similar to the one you want to take

**Patterns to look for:**
- Common educational backgrounds
- Common career paths (where do people come from?)
- Common skills and certifications
- Common language in their "About" sections

**Day 8: The Content Diet**
- Subscribe to 3-5 industry newsletters
- Follow 10-15 industry leaders on LinkedIn
- Join 2-3 relevant LinkedIn groups, Slack communities, or Reddit communities
- Bookmark 3-5 industry publications or blogs

**Day 9: The Money**
- What are the salary ranges for your target role? (Use Glassdoor, Levels.fyi, Payscale)
- How does compensation typically break down? (Base, bonus, equity, commission?)
- What is the career progression? (Entry-level title through senior title, with typical timelines)
- What do people in this industry care about most? (Revenue growth? Patient outcomes? User engagement? Compliance?)

**Day 10: Informational Interviews**
- By now you know enough to have intelligent conversations with industry insiders
- Reach out to 3-5 people for informational interviews
- Use your research to ask specific, informed questions (not "tell me about the industry," but "I noticed [SPECIFIC TREND]. How is that affecting [THEIR COMPANY]?")

## The Insider Test

After 2 weeks, test your knowledge. Can you:

- [ ] Explain what the industry does in 2 sentences to a non-expert?
- [ ] Name the top 5 companies and what differentiates them?
- [ ] Describe 3 major trends and their implications?
- [ ] Define 20+ industry-specific terms without looking them up?
- [ ] Name the tools and platforms most commonly used?
- [ ] Describe the typical career path for your target role?
- [ ] Explain how money flows in this industry (business model)?
- [ ] Talk about a current controversy, challenge, or debate in the industry?
- [ ] Name 5 people whose work or perspective you follow?
- [ ] Ask an industry insider a question that makes them think "this person gets it"?

If you can check 7+ of these, you have enough fluency to start networking and applying with credibility. If you are under 7, spend another week on the areas where you have gaps.

## Using Your Research in Job Search

### On Your Resume
Use industry vocabulary. If the industry calls it "customer success" instead of "account management," use their word. Your transferable skills should be described in the target industry's language.

### In Cover Letters
Reference specific trends, challenges, or companies. "I'm drawn to [Company] because of your approach to [SPECIFIC THING I LEARNED IN MY RESEARCH]." This separates you from every other outsider.

### In Interviews
Ask informed questions. "I've been following the [TREND] in [INDUSTRY]. How is [COMPANY] thinking about that?" This signals that you have done your homework and are serious about the transition.

### On LinkedIn
Start sharing content and commentary about your target industry. Repost articles with your own take. Comment on industry leaders' posts with informed perspectives. This builds your credibility as someone who belongs in this space, even before you land a role.

---

## The Ongoing Research Habit

Two weeks gets you started. Staying current keeps you credible.

After the initial 2-week sprint, maintain a lighter routine:

- 15 minutes per day reading industry content
- 1 informational interview per week
- 1 industry event or webinar per month
- Update your glossary when you encounter new terms

Within 3 months, you will know more about this industry than many people who have worked in it for years but never looked up from their own company.

---

## Key Takeaway

The fastest way to break into a new industry is not a degree or a certification. It is research. Two weeks of focused effort gives you the vocabulary, the context, and the confidence to show up as a credible candidate instead of an obvious outsider.

Insiders can tell when someone has done their homework. Make sure they can tell about you.
