# Financial Runway Calculator

**How many months can you survive while you change careers? Do the math before you make the move.**

Career change advice loves to skip the money part. "Follow your passion" sounds great until you realize your passion does not pay rent for the 6 months it takes to transition. This guide makes the money math explicit, because financial clarity is what separates a successful career change from a panicked retreat back to what you were doing before.

**Time:** 45-60 minutes. **What you'll need:** Your last 3 months of bank statements, pay stubs, and a calculator.

## Step 2: Your Emergency Fund

An emergency fund is separate from your career change runway. It covers the unexpected: car repair, medical bill, home repair. You need this fund untouched during your transition.

**Recommended emergency fund:** 1-2 months of your burn rate.

**Your emergency fund target:** $_____ (burn rate x 1.5)

**Your current emergency fund:** $_____

**Gap:** $_____

If your emergency fund is empty, building at least one month of expenses before making a career move is a priority. Without it, any unexpected cost forces you to abandon your transition.

## Step 4: The Income Bridge

If you have a gap, you need income during the transition. Here are the options, ranked by flexibility.

### Bridge Option 1: Stay Employed and Transition

The safest approach. Keep your current income while building toward the new career in evenings and weekends.

**Pros:** No financial risk. No gap on resume. Full income.
**Cons:** Slower transition. Exhausting. Limited time for networking and interviews.
**Best for:** Career changes that require reskilling (courses, certifications, portfolio building).

### Bridge Option 2: Part-Time or Contract in Current Field

Reduce to part-time or take contract work in your current field. Use the freed-up time for your career change.

**Pros:** Partial income, more time for transition activities.
**Cons:** Lower income, may not be available in your field.
**Best for:** Fields where contract or fractional work is common (tech, finance, consulting, marketing).

### Bridge Option 3: Part-Time in Target Field

Take an entry-level, part-time, or volunteer role in your target field while maintaining some other income.

**Pros:** Build experience and network in the new field. Test the waters.
**Cons:** Low pay. Potentially feels like a step backward.
**Best for:** Career changes where experience matters more than credentials.

### Bridge Option 4: Freelance or Gig Work

Use existing skills to freelance while transitioning.

**Pros:** Flexible schedule, potentially high hourly rates.
**Cons:** Inconsistent income, requires self-discipline.
**Best for:** People with marketable skills (writing, design, consulting, development).

### Bridge Option 5: Full Quit with Savings

Leave your job and focus entirely on the transition.

**Pros:** Full focus and energy on the career change.
**Cons:** Highest financial risk. Resume gap (though gaps are increasingly normalized).
**Best for:** People with 6+ months of runway, a clear plan, and strong discipline.

## Step 5: Education ROI Calculator

If your career change requires paid education (bootcamp, certificate, degree), run this calculation before enrolling.

| Factor | Amount |
|--------|--------|
| Total cost of education (tuition + books + fees) | $_____ |
| Lost income during education (months x current salary/12) | $_____ |
| **Total investment** | **$_____** |
| | |
| Expected salary in new career (first year) | $_____ |
| Current salary | $_____ |
| Annual salary increase from change | $_____ |
| **Years to break even:** Total investment / annual increase | **_____ years** |

**The rule of thumb:** If the break-even is under 3 years, the education is probably worth it financially. If it is 5+ years, reconsider whether there is a cheaper path to the same career (self-study, portfolio building, certifications instead of degrees).

### Questions to Ask Before Enrolling

1. Do employers in my target field actually require this credential, or do they just prefer it?
2. Can I get the same knowledge through free or low-cost alternatives (YouTube, Coursera, library)?
3. What is the job placement rate for graduates of this program?
4. Can I talk to 3 graduates about their experience and outcomes?
5. Is there a payment plan, scholarship, or employer sponsorship available?

## Step 6: The Decision Matrix

| Condition | Green Light | Yellow Light | Red Light |
|-----------|------------|-------------|-----------|
| Runway (months) | 6+ months covered | 3-5 months | Under 3 months |
| Emergency fund | Fully funded | Partially funded | Empty |
| Bridge income plan | Tested and confirmed | Planned but untested | No plan |
| Debt level | Manageable, stable | Moderate, under control | High, growing |
| Partner/family alignment | Discussed, agreed | Discussed, concerns | Not discussed |
| Education cost | Under 1 year income | 1-2 years income | Over 2 years income |

**Mostly green:** You are financially prepared to make the move.
**Mix of green and yellow:** Possible, but build more runway first. Give yourself 2-3 more months of saving.
**Any red:** Pause the career change and fix the red items first. A career change from a position of financial stress is much harder and riskier.

---

## Key Takeaway

A career change is a financial project as much as a professional one. Knowing exactly how much runway you have, how long the transition will take, and what income bridge you will use turns a scary leap into a calculated move.

Do the math. Build the runway. Then change with confidence, not desperation.
